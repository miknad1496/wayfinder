# Liberal Arts Institution Financial Crisis (2024-2026)

## The Enrollment Cliff and Institutional Survival

Liberal arts colleges are facing an existential financial crisis. This directly affects the advice Wayfinder gives about school selection, since a school's financial health is now a critical variable in whether a student should attend.

## Key Data Points

In 2024, 182 colleges received failing financial grades from the Department of Education — up from just 20 in 2021. At least 16 nonprofit institutions announced closures in 2025, and 12+ public or nonprofit colleges merged or closed that year. An economist at Carleton College predicts the college-attending population will decrease 15% between 2025 and 2029 due to the demographic enrollment cliff (fewer 18-year-olds).

Only 12% of students now express preference for a purely liberal arts education. 60% favor applied and career-specific programs. This preference shift is accelerating the financial pressure on liberal arts institutions, which depend heavily on tuition revenue and often lack the endowment cushion of wealthier peers.

Municipal bond managers are actively avoiding higher education debt from financially vulnerable institutions — a signal that professional risk assessors see serious default potential.

## What This Means for Advising

School selection advice must now include institutional financial health as a screening variable. Factors to evaluate: endowment per student (below $50K per student is a warning sign), enrollment trends over the past 5 years (declining is a red flag), tuition discount rate (above 50% means the school is buying enrollment and may be unsustainable), and whether the school has recently cut programs, merged departments, or frozen hiring.

A school that closes or merges during a student's enrollment is catastrophic — credits may not transfer cleanly, the degree brand is damaged, and the student loses community and continuity.

The ROI calculation for a $75K/year liberal arts degree must be evaluated against this backdrop. Strong, well-endowed liberal arts colleges (Williams, Amherst, Pomona, Swarthmore — endowments exceeding $1B) remain excellent choices. Mid-tier and lower-endowment liberal arts colleges charging $60-75K/year with declining enrollment are increasingly risky investments.

Students considering liberal arts should be guided toward institutions with financial stability indicators, or toward liberal arts education within larger universities (honors colleges at state schools) that provide the small-class experience without the institutional risk.
