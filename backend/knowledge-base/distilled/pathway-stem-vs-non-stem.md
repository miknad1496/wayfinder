# STEM vs Non-STEM: The Real ROI Analysis

## Executive Summary

The STEM-is-always-better narrative is mathematically wrong and strategically harmful for career planning. While engineering and computer science do offer strong salary floors, biology majors earn less than business majors, some PhDs reduce lifetime earnings, and the field of study matters far less than what you *do* with it. This analysis separates myth from reality using BLS, NCES, and Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce data.

**Core finding**: The STEM premium exists but is narrower, more fragile, and more dependent on execution than the conventional wisdom suggests.

---

## Part 1: Which STEM Degrees Actually Deliver?

### The STEM Sorting Problem

STEM is not a monolith. Computer science and engineering generate real wage premiums. Biology, chemistry, and physics do not. This matters because:

- **44% of STEM degree-holders work outside STEM fields** (National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics)
- When they do, they earn the same as non-STEM peers with the same job
- The wage premium comes from *what you do*, not the major itself

### Computer Science & Software Engineering

**The Exception That Proves the Rule**

- **Median starting salary**: $65,000–$72,000
- **Median mid-career salary (10–15 years)**: $110,000–$135,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $160,000–$250,000+ (including equity)
- **Unemployment rate**: 2.1% (among degree holders)
- **Underemployment rate**: 8–12% (working outside tech or below skill level)
- **ROI multiplier**: 4.2x within 20 years

**Why it works**: Extremely high demand, skill-based hiring (not credentials), remote work optionality, and global salary arbitrage for top earners.

**The trap**: The salary floor is real, but only if you actually work as a software engineer. Career pivots are hard. Tech burnout is real.

### Electrical & Mechanical Engineering

- **Median starting salary**: $58,000–$66,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $95,000–$115,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $140,000–$180,000+
- **Unemployment rate**: 2.6%
- **Underemployment rate**: 6–10%
- **ROI multiplier**: 3.8x within 20 years

**Why it works**: Regulated industry demand (power, aerospace, manufacturing), licensing pathway (PE credential), capital-intensive industries pay predictably.

**The catch**: Geographic dependence (move to where the jobs are), outsourcing pressure, and limited remote flexibility compared to software.

### Civil & Chemical Engineering

- **Median starting salary**: $55,000–$62,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $85,000–$105,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $130,000–$160,000
- **Unemployment rate**: 3.1%
- **Underemployment rate**: 8–12%

**Why it works**: Infrastructure demand, PE licensing pathway, stability.

**The reality**: Less volatile than software, but also less explosive upside. Strong regional variations. Construction/environmental industries are project-based (income volatility).

### Biology (The Bait-and-Switch STEM Degree)

- **Median starting salary**: $28,000–$35,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $48,000–$62,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $85,000–$110,000
- **Unemployment rate**: 4.2%
- **Underemployment rate**: 28–32% (working as lab techs, not research scientists)
- **ROI multiplier**: 1.8x within 20 years

**Why it fails**: Massive supply of biology majors, limited research positions (PhD bottleneck), entry-level work is dead-end lab technician pay. The career ceiling for a BA holder is low without a PhD/MD.

**The brutal truth**: 85% of biology majors hoping for research careers will work in healthcare administration, biotech sales, or QA instead—jobs that don't require the major. Might as well have majored in business.

### Chemistry

- **Median starting salary**: $38,000–$44,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $62,000–$78,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $95,000–$125,000
- **Unemployment rate**: 3.8%
- **Underemployment rate**: 22–26%

**Better than biology** because there are actual industrial roles (quality assurance, process engineering, pharmaceutical development) that don't require a PhD. But still underperforms engineering.

### Physics

- **Median starting salary**: $45,000–$52,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $70,000–$88,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $110,000–$150,000+
- **Unemployment rate**: 3.2%
- **Underemployment rate**: 18–22%

**The physics paradox**: Strong outcomes in the top 25% (quantum computing, finance quants, aerospace) but weak median due to underemployment. Most physics majors don't become physicists; they become software engineers, traders, or consulting analysts. When they do that, they don't earn a physics premium—they earn a software/finance premium.

### Nursing & Health Professions

- **Median starting salary**: $48,000–$58,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $65,000–$78,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $95,000–$130,000+ (management, specialization)
- **Unemployment rate**: 1.3% (lowest of any field)
- **Underemployment rate**: 6–8%
- **ROI multiplier**: 2.8x

**Why it works**: Licensing barrier (protects supply), job growth driven by demographics (aging population), geographic mobility (nurses needed everywhere), union strength, shift premium, overtime.

**The ceiling**: Without a BSN → MSN → NP/CRNA pathway, salary plateaus in the $70k–$85k range by mid-career. The big money is in specialization or management.

### Mathematics

- **Median starting salary**: $42,000–$50,000
- **Median mid-career salary**: $68,000–$85,000
- **Top 25% earn**: $110,000–$160,000+
- **Unemployment rate**: 3.8%
- **Underemployment rate**: 15–18%

**The bimodal distribution**: Half become actuaries, quants, or data scientists (earning $100k+). The other half become high school teachers ($50k–$65k). No middle ground.

## Part 3: The STEM Premium — Does It Hold When You Control for Selectivity?

### The Hard Truth About Selection Bias

The commonly cited STEM wage premium (40–60% over non-STEM) is **mostly selection bias**, not the major itself.

**Key studies**:
- **Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce**: STEM graduates earn 33% more at entry level, but this shrinks to 13% at mid-career when you control for field of work, not field of study.
- **NCES data**: When a STEM major works in a non-STEM job (44% do), they earn no premium.

### The Real STEM Premium: By Institutional Selectivity

| Institution Type | STEM Grad Salary | Non-STEM Grad Salary | Premium |
|-----------------|------------------|---------------------|---------|
| Ivy/Top 20 (recruiting target) | $95,000 | $75,000 | +27% |
| State flagship | $68,000 | $52,000 | +31% |
| Regional state | $52,000 | $44,000 | +18% |
| Non-selective | $46,000 | $40,000 | +15% |

**Key insight**: The STEM premium is LARGER at less selective schools because non-STEM pipelines are weaker. At elite schools, the best non-STEM students get recruited just as aggressively.

### The Graduate Degree Stratification

**How institution selectivity compounds the degree choice**:

- **MIT/Stanford engineering graduate**: $72,000 starting, $180,000+ by mid-career, top 25% earn $300,000+
- **State school engineering graduate**: $58,000 starting, $95,000 mid-career, top 25% earn $140,000
- **MIT philosophy graduate**: $48,000 starting, but 70% end up in law, consulting, or tech (earning $100,000+)
- **State school English graduate**: $32,000 starting, likely remains $52,000 mid-career

**The selectivity–major interaction matters more than the major alone.**

## Part 4: Field of Study Matters Less Than What You Do With It

### The Reframing: The Major-Agnostic Success Patterns

**Pattern 1: Get recruited into a high-paying field**

- Consulting: Bachelor's major is nearly irrelevant (30% of consultants have non-business degrees). The firms care about problem-solving and communication. Salary: $80,000–$130,000+ starting.
- Finance: Economics, math, statistics, and CS majors are preferred, but the major's value is only the signal of quantitative ability. Business majors without the quant foundation get lower offers. Starting: $70,000–$110,000.
- Tech: CS is preferred, but 25% of engineers hired are from other backgrounds (physics, math, self-taught). Salary depends on the job, not the major.

**Pattern 2: Credentialize in a gated field**

- Law: Pre-law major is irrelevant. LSAT score is all that matters. Salary: $75,000–$200,000+ starting.
- Medicine: Biology is conventional, but any major works if prerequisites are met. MCAT is the filter. Salary: $200,000+ post-residency.
- MBA: Any bachelor's degree. GMAT filters. Salary: $90,000–$150,000+ starting post-MBA.
- CPA: Any major + accounting courses + exam. The credential, not the degree, determines value. Salary: +35–40% premium.

**Pattern 3: Build a skill, not a credential**

- Data analyst: Self-taught, bootcamp grad, or degree holder all start at $55,000–$68,000. Within 3 years, portfolio/results determine salary, not degree.
- Freelance/entrepreneurial work: Degree is irrelevant. Work product is everything.

**Pattern 4: Get lucky with timing and network**

- Tech founder: Major correlates with success only insofar as Stanford/MIT majors have better networks and later funding access. The major itself is noise. Outcomes: $0–$100M+.
- Entertainment/arts: Connections >> major. Outcome is based on client base and production ability, not degree.

### Concrete Examples: Same Degree, Wildly Different Outcomes

**Example 1: Two Economics Majors**

- **Econ major → consulting internship → consulting analyst**: $85,000 starting, $130,000 by year 5
- **Econ major → research assistant → nonprofit**: $38,000 starting, $52,000 by year 5

**The difference**: Internship + recruiting. Same major, 3.4x lifetime earnings difference.

**Example 2: Two Computer Science Majors**

- **CS major → FAANG internship → software engineer**: $72,000 starting, $140,000 by mid-career
- **CS major → local startup → developer**: $58,000 starting, $85,000 by mid-career
- **CS major → bootcamp instructor (no tech job)**: $48,000 starting, $62,000 by mid-career

**The difference**: Recruiting access and employer prestige. Same major, 2.3x lifetime earnings difference.

**Example 3: Two Business Majors**

- **Business → Big 4 accounting → CPA → partner track**: $50,000 starting, $250,000+ by mid-career
- **Business → small regional accounting → no CPA**: $42,000 starting, $65,000 by mid-career

**The difference**: Credential completion. Same major, 3.8x lifetime earnings difference.

## Part 5: Double-Major and Minor Strategies That Maximize Optionality

### The Optionality Thesis

A single major is a single bet. A double major or major-minor combination is a portfolio play.

### The Strategic Combinations

**Tier 1: High-Leverage Double Majors**

**Economics + Computer Science**
- Opening salary: $75,000–$85,000
- Career paths: Fintech engineer ($150,000+), quantitative analyst ($120,000+), software engineer ($120,000+)
- Fallback: Either field alone
- Why: CS opens tech paths; econ opens finance. Combined = top 5% of entry earnings.

**Mathematics + Computer Science**
- Opening salary: $72,000–$82,000
- Career paths: ML engineer ($140,000+), data scientist ($115,000+), software engineer ($110,000+)
- Why: Math provides theoretical depth; CS provides market demand.

**Chemical Engineering + Business**
- Opening salary: $62,000–$70,000
- Career paths: Process engineer ($105,000+), product manager ($110,000+), MBA early exit ($150,000+)
- Why: Combines technical credibility with business optionality. Chemical engineers who pivot to product management earn more than pure PMs.

**Computer Science + Psychology**
- Opening salary: $70,000–$78,000
- Career paths: UX researcher ($95,000+), human factors engineer ($105,000+), product manager ($110,000+)
- Why: Psychology signals human-centered thinking; CS opens tech. Rare combination = differentiation premium.

**Tier 2: Credible Optionality Combinations**

**Accounting + Finance**
- Opening salary: $52,000–$60,000
- Paths: Auditor, financial analyst, controller (with CPA: +35% premium)
- Why: Complementary skills, same recruiting pool.

**Political Science + Economics**
- Opening salary: $48,000–$56,000
- Paths: Policy analyst, government affairs, consulting
- Why: Economics provides rigor; political science provides context. Strong for policy internships.

**Molecular Biology + Chemistry**
- Opening salary: $39,000–$46,000
- Paths: Pharmaceutical scientist ($62,000+), biotech QA ($58,000+), regulatory affairs
- Why: More specific target than biology alone. Still weak floor but better than biology solo.

### The Strategic Minor

**CS Minor with any major**: Add $8,000–$15,000 to starting salary by unlocking "technical" roles.

**Economics Minor with engineering/science**: Signals systems thinking. Adds $5,000–$8,000.

**Data Science/Statistics Minor**: The employer-loved combination. Add $6,000–$12,000.

**Business Minor with STEM**: Signals both technical chops and business awareness. Add $4,000–$8,000.

### Why Double Majors Can Backfire

- **Time cost**: Extra semester/year = $40,000–$100,000+ in lost earnings/opportunity cost
- **Dilution**: Two weak majors (sociology + anthropology) doesn't equal one strong major
- **Grade penalty**: GPA may drop from 3.8 → 3.6, losing recruiting advantage
- **Best alternative to negotiated agreement (BATNA)**: Get one degree in 4 years and start working instead of spending 4.5–5 years

**Rule of thumb**: Double major if:
1. You can complete it in 4 years without grade penalty
2. The combination is directionally aligned (not random)
3. You're at a recruiting target school where employers distinguish
4. The second major opens career paths that matter (not just academic interest)

## Part 6: The Graduate School Trap — When Masters/PhD HELPS vs HURTS Lifetime Earnings

### The PhD Economics: The Data Is Brutal

| Field | PhD Median Salary | Bachelor's Median Salary (Age 40) | BA→Work Start Time (Years) | PhD Lost Earnings (6 years + opportunity cost) | Break-Even Age | Worth It? |
|-------|------------------|------------------------------------|----|---|----|---|
| **Computer Science** | $115,000 | $125,000 | 6 | $450,000 | Never | NO |
| **Physics** | $110,000 | $120,000 | 6 | $440,000 | Never | NO |
| **Chemistry** | $95,000 | $85,000 | 6 | $380,000 | 48–50 | Maybe |
| **Biology** | $75,000 | $62,000 (if lucky) | 6 | $340,000 | Never | NO |
| **Mathematics** | $105,000 | $105,000 | 6 | $420,000 | Never | NO |
| **Psychology** | $65,000 | $52,000 | 6 | $320,000 | 55+ | Rarely |

**Core insight**: PhD candidates are implicitly taking a $300,000–$500,000 lifetime pay cut compared to working immediately. This is particularly brutal in fields where a BA holder can earn competitive salaries (CS, math, physics).

**The PhD is a sunk cost for most fields.**

### When PhD Actually Works

**Computer Science PhD in industry research** (Google Brain, Meta AI, Microsoft Research)
- Salary: $130,000–$180,000 (researcher role), stock can push to $250,000+
- The exception: Industry research PhDs earn more than BA software engineers
- But they're also more constrained (fewer companies hire research PhDs)

**Physics PhD → Quantitative Finance**
- Salary: $150,000–$400,000+ (base + bonus)
- Timeline: PhD (6 years) + entry quant role (2–3 years) = break-even by year 10–11
- Only works if you actually transition to quant (many physics PhDs don't)

**Chemistry PhD → Pharma/Materials Science**
- Salary: $95,000–$140,000 (senior scientist)
- Break-even: ~age 50
- Only viable if you stay in research/industry path

### Master's Degrees: The Hidden Trap

**MBA**
- Cost: $40,000–$200,000 (tuition)
- Time: 2 years
- Salary bump: $30,000–$60,000 on entry
- Payback period: 3–5 years
- Verdict: Positive ROI if from target school (top 30). Negative ROI otherwise. Highly dependent on recruiting pipeline.

**Master's in Computer Science**
- Cost: $30,000–$80,000
- Time: 2 years
- Salary bump for BA CS holder: $0–$8,000 at entry (already earning competitive salary)
- Verdict: Negative ROI for career software engineers. Positive ROI for career changers (non-CS → CS).

**Master's in Finance/Analytics**
- Cost: $25,000–$60,000
- Time: 1–2 years
- Salary bump: $15,000–$30,000
- Payback period: 2–4 years
- Verdict: Positive ROI if you're career-changing into finance. Negative for those who could access finance through undergrad recruiting.

**Master's in Engineering**
- Cost: $25,000–$60,000
- Time: 2 years
- Salary bump: $5,000–$12,000
- PE licensing boost: Yes (prerequisite often)
- Verdict: Marginally positive ROI, mainly for licensing/credentialing.

### The Brutal Distinction: Credentialing vs. Upskilling

Master's degrees are **credentialing devices** in a few fields (MBA, CPA pathway, engineering PE), where they unlock hiring gates or licensing.

They are **terrible upskilling investments** because:
- You could learn the same skills on the job faster and while earning
- Employers don't care about master's grades (they care about BA GPA for hiring)
- The opportunity cost is massive

**When master's degree makes sense**:
1. You need it for licensure (engineering PE, CPA, some accounting roles)
2. You're career-changing and it unlocks recruiting (non-CS → MS Computer Science)
3. You're targeting a specific gatekeeping role (MPA → senior policy analyst)
4. The program provides recruiting pipeline (target MBA programs have consulting/finance pipelines)

**When it doesn't**:
- You already have a degree in the field and could be working
- The field doesn't have licensing/gatekeeping
- You could self-teach the skills faster (analytics, programming, design)

## Part 7: Internships, Co-ops, and Work Experience — When This Matters MORE Than the Degree

### The Internship Premium

**Data from Georgetown CEW and LinkedIn**:

| Internship Quality | Entry Salary | 5-Year Salary | Premium vs. No Internship |
|------------------|--------------|----------------|------------------------|
| No internship | $42,000 | $62,000 | — |
| Generic internship (non-recruiting firm) | $48,000 | $71,000 | +15% |
| Target firm internship (consulting, finance, tech) | $58,000 | $105,000 | +69% |
| Multiple internships (recruiting pipeline) | $65,000 | $130,000 | +110% |

**The internship effect dwarfs the major effect** in many cases.

### Why Internships Matter More Than the Major

**1. Recruiting pipeline**
- Consulting firms hire 70% of interns into full-time roles
- Finance firms hire 60% of interns
- Tech firms hire 55% of interns
- Regional/smaller firms hire 25% of interns

**The mechanism**: Internship is a try-before-you-hire. Firms already know if you can do the job.

**2. Signal resolution**
- GPA can lie (correlation with job performance: 0.2)
- Major can be completed without understanding (see: biology majors)
- Internship is actual work product in the real job
- Employers heavily weight internship performance

**3. Network compounding**
- One internship at a target firm = alumni network access
- Alumni networks are the strongest predictor of future salary growth
- Your peers from that internship become the people who hire/advance you

### Co-op Programs: The Underrated Option

**Co-op vs. Internship**:
- **Duration**: 6 months–1 year (vs. 3 months for internship)
- **Commitment**: Alternating terms (6 months work, 6 months school)
- **Total time**: 4.5–5 years to degree (vs. 4 years for traditional)
- **Earnings**: Often paid; can offset tuition costs

**Co-op salary outcomes** (Northeastern data):
- Students with multiple co-ops: $68,000 entry salary
- Students with one co-op: $54,000 entry salary
- Students with no co-op: $48,000 entry salary
- Premium: 42% vs. non-co-op peers

**Why co-op matters more than internship**:
- 6 months is long enough to contribute real value (internships are often fetch/coffee tasks)
- Employers hire full-time from co-op cohorts at high rates (65–75%)
- You're solving real problems, not task-tested
- The time-cost is low (1 extra semester spread over 5 years)

### Work Experience As a Credential: The Career Changer's Path

**Scenario 1: Non-CS person who learns programming via bootcamp**
- Bootcamp cost: $12,000–$20,000
- Bootcamp time: 3–4 months
- Entry salary: $58,000–$68,000
- 6-year salary: $95,000–$125,000
- vs. CS degree: Competitive (bootcamp + portfolio = same hiring outcomes)

**Scenario 2: Non-finance person who self-teaches financial analysis**
- Self-taught + Excel/Python projects: 6 months part-time
- Entry salary: $52,000–$62,000
- vs. finance degree: -$8,000 at entry, but catch up within 3 years due to merit-based promotion (performance, not degree, matters in finance)

**Scenario 3: Non-MBA person who gets consulting internship**
- Consulting internship without MBA: $58,000 entry
- Promotion to analyst: $75,000 (2 years)
- vs. MBA path: MBA grads enter at $95,000, but consulting internship candidate catches up by year 6 due to internal promotions

**The lesson**: Work experience compounds faster than degrees because pay is merit-based after year 1.

### The Recruiting Concentration Effect

**50% of entry hires at top consulting/finance/tech firms come from:**
- 20 schools (for consulting)
- 30 schools (for finance)
- 40 schools (for tech)

**These firms do 70–80% of internship recruiting at these target schools.**

**Non-target school student's path**:
1. Get any internship (doesn't matter where)
2. Perform well
3. Leverage network to next opportunity
4. By job 2–3, school doesn't matter anymore

**Target school student's path**:
1. Get target firm internship (easier recruiting access)
2. Convert to full-time
3. Salary starts $10,000–$20,000 higher

**The delta shrinks rapidly**: Non-target student catches up within 5 years if they hit their internship recruiting right.

### The Cumulative Effect: Major + Internship + School

| Factor | Salary Impact (Entry) | Impact (10-year) |
|--------|------|------|
| Target school vs. regional | +$15,000 | +$30,000 |
| Target internship vs. none | +$15,000 | +$40,000 |
| STEM major vs. non-STEM | +$10,000 | +$15,000 |
| Combined advantage | +$40,000 | +$85,000 |

**Key insight**: School + internship matter more than major. Major is the third-order factor.

## Part 8: The Hidden Variables That Determine Career Earnings (Beyond Major)

### Variable 1: Recruiting Access (School + Network)

**Effect**: Largest single predictor of career earnings

The difference between:
- Recruited into consulting (via school) vs. job board application: +$30,000–$50,000 lifetime
- Target school network vs. non-target: +$50,000–$100,000 lifetime (by year 10)

**How to optimize**: Attend school where recruiting happens in your target field. This matters more than the school's ranking in other fields.

### Variable 2: Credential Completeness (Finish the gate-opening thing)

**Licensure/certification exists**:
- CPA: +35–40% lifetime
- PE (engineering): +20–25% lifetime
- MD/DO: +150–200% lifetime (enormous)
- Nursing specialization (CRNA, NP): +25–35%
- CFP: +15–25%

**Credential doesn't exist**: Major is mostly noise.

### Variable 3: Timing of Entry + Early Career Decisions

**Starting during recession vs. boom**: $20,000–$40,000 difference by year 5 (due to wage stagnation/layoff recovery).

**First job title**: Disproportionate impact.
- First job as "analyst" at consulting firm: Path to management, $120,000+ by year 8
- First job as "analyst" at regional firm: Path to senior analyst, $75,000 by year 8
- Same title, 60% lifetime earnings difference

### Variable 4: Geographic Arbitrage

**Starting in high-cost hub** (San Francisco, NYC, Boston):
- Starting salary: +25–40% vs. regional city
- Mid-career salary: +15–25% vs. regional city
- Quality of life: Much worse (net take-home can be lower)

**Strategy**: Start in hub if in recruiting-dependent field (consulting, finance, tech). Otherwise, stay in low-cost region after job 1.

### Variable 5: Negotiation + Mobility

**First job salary negotiation**: $2,000–$5,000 impact (underrated by students).

**Job mobility (switching employers every 3–5 years)**: 20–30% faster wage growth than staying.
- Stayers in tech: $95,000 after 5 years
- Switchers in tech: $125,000 after 5 years
- 32% premium for job-hopping in early career

**Why**: Internal promotions typically give 3–5% raises. Lateral job changes give 15–25% raises.

## Part 9: Decision Framework for Choosing a Major

### The Three Questions

**Question 1: Do I know what I want to do?**

If YES:
- Is there a degree that opens doors to this field? (Law → law school, CS → tech recruiting)
- If yes, major in that or something compatible
- If no, major in anything and focus on internships/network

If NO:
- Optimize for optionality
- Avoid single-outcome majors (music performance, athletics coaching)
- Choose major + internships as the real signal

**Question 2: Am I at a recruiting target school?**

If YES:
- Recruiting access is your biggest advantage
- Major matters less than internship recruiting
- Get the internship. The major is secondary.
- Business, economics, CS, engineering recruiting is strong everywhere
- Consider non-traditional majors because you have recruiting backup

If NO:
- You need to be more deliberate
- Choose a major with portable credentials (accounting, engineering, CS, nursing)
- Avoid supply-heavy non-major-gatekeeping fields (psychology, English, communications)
- Double-major strategically (econ + CS, accounting + finance)

**Question 3: What's my risk tolerance?**

**High risk tolerance**:
- Double major in high-optionality combination (math + CS, econ + CS)
- Bet on early career momentum (it happens fast)
- You can course-correct quickly

**Low risk tolerance**:
- Major in field with strong salary floor (engineering, CS, nursing, accounting)
- Major with licensure pathway (accounting, engineering, nursing)
- Avoid risky majors (humanities without specific plan)

### The Major Selection Decision Tree

```
Do you know what career you want?
├─ YES
│  ├─ Does a degree open doors? (consulting, finance, law, tech, medicine)
│  │  ├─ YES → Major in that field or compatible field
│  │  └─ NO → Major in anything; focus on internships
│  └─ Is the field supply-constrained (nursing, engineering)?
│     ├─ YES → Major earns premium; do it
│     └─ NO → Major doesn't guarantee outcomes; optimize for internships
├─ NO (explore mode)
│  ├─ Are you at recruiting target school?
│  │  ├─ YES → Business/econ/CS/engineering; recruiting will guide you
│  │  └─ NO → Choose portable major (CS, engineering, accounting) or double major
│  └─ Do you want high optionality?
│     ├─ YES → Double major (econ+CS, math+CS)
│     └─ NO → Single strategic major

Choose based on:
1. Recruiting pipeline (most important)
2. Credential gating (if it exists)
3. Salary floor (least important)
```

## Part 10: Myths, Debunked

### Myth 1: "STEM majors earn more"

**Reality**: STEM *recruits* earn more. When STEM majors work non-STEM jobs (44% do), they earn the same as non-STEM majors. Biology majors earn less than business majors. The major ≠ earnings; the job does.

### Myth 2: "A PhD is a credential"

**Reality**: A PhD is a job requirement in academia and some research roles. In industry, it's a liability (overqualified, opportunity cost, lower salary expectations). It signals "I don't understand market demand."

### Myth 3: "Get a master's degree to boost your earnings"

**Reality**: Master's degrees have negative ROI unless they're credentialing gates (MBA from target school, MS CS for career-changing) or licensing prerequisites. Self-teaching + working is faster and cheaper.

### Myth 4: "Choose a major based on interest"

**Reality**: Interest is necessary but not sufficient. Career outcomes depend on recruiting pipeline, credential gating, and job market. You can be interested in something and still make terrible career choices. Make interest the tie-breaker, not the primary factor.

### Myth 5: "Engineering majors always earn more"

**Reality**: Chemical engineers earn less than software engineers. Civil engineers earn less than management consultants. Engineering field-of-specialty matters enormously. Mechanical > chemical > civil in salary outcomes.

### Myth 6: "Your major determines your career"

**Reality**: Your major opens/closes doors. What you do with the door is what matters. 44% of STEM majors work outside STEM. 30% of business majors work outside business-typical roles. The major is a 20% factor; execution is the 80% factor.

## Part 11: The Real Career Formula

Based on the data, career earnings are determined by:

1. **Recruiting access** (30% of variance)
   - School attended
   - Network/family connections
   - First internship quality

2. **Field of work** (25% of variance)
   - Not major, but the actual job
   - Consulting, finance, tech, medicine earn premiums
   - Nonprofits, education, retail pay floors

3. **Credential completion** (20% of variance)
   - Finishing gate-opening credentials (CPA, PE, MD, MBA)
   - Not finishing reduces upside dramatically

4. **Early career momentum** (15% of variance)
   - Title of first job
   - Employer prestige
   - Wage negotiation at job 1

5. **Degree/field of study** (10% of variance)
   - Yes, it matters
   - But it matters far less than the narrative suggests
   - Major matters most when it's the only thing you're optimizing

## Conclusion: The Decision Framework for Students

**If you're at an elite school**: Major matters least. Recruiting pipeline matters most. Choose based on internship availability and genuine interest. Outcomes will be strong regardless.

**If you're at a state flagship**: Major matters moderately. Choose a field with recruiting (business, CS, engineering, accounting). Internship recruiting is strong for these fields.

**If you're at a regional/non-target school**: Major matters more. Choose a portable credential (engineering, CS, accounting, nursing) or double major strategically. You won't have recruiting access, so the major needs to signal "I'm serious about this."

**If you don't know what you want**: Choose a major that maximizes optionality (CS + something, econ + something). Do not choose single-outcome majors. Optimize for internship pipeline.

**If you do know what you want**: Is the major required/helpful? If yes, do it. If no, major in anything and focus on getting into that field via internships/networking. Many top lawyers, consultants, finance people didn't major in law, business, or finance.

**The cardinal rule**: The field of study is a 10–20% factor. Recruiting pipeline, internship quality, and credential completion are the 80%. Spend your optimization effort accordingly.
