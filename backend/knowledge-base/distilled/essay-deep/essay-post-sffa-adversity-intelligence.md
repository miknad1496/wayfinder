## Executive Summary: The Perfect Storm in College Essays

Three tectonic shifts have converged in the 2025-2026 admissions cycle to create an unprecedented strategic landscape:

1. **SFFA Decision (June 2023)** eliminated race-conscious admissions but explicitly preserved the right to discuss race in essays
2. **College Board's Landscape Tool Death (September 2025)** eliminated the automated neighborhood-level adversity data that admissions officers relied on for context
3. **Current Administration Executive Orders** mandate that colleges prove they're not using "hidden racial proxies" in admissions

The result is a paradoxical crisis: students are being told to emphasize adversity and context in essays at the exact moment when adversity essays have become dramatically overdone and transparently gamed. Meanwhile, the institutional safety net that used to provide automatic context (Landscape) is gone, forcing students to self-report circumstances that were previously supplied by data.

This is the most consequential shift in essay strategy since the Common App itself.

## The Three Tectonic Shifts Explained

### Shift 1: SFFA Decision (June 2023) — Race Can't Be A Checkbox, But Can Be A Story

The Supreme Court's decision in *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard* banned race-conscious admissions at colleges across the country. But it explicitly preserved students' right to write about how race has affected their life. This created a paradox that persists today:

- **What died:** Race as an institutional sorting mechanism. Schools can no longer build diversity through race-conscious selection.
- **What survived:** Race as a narrative element. Students can absolutely write about their racial identity, discrimination they've faced, cultural context, code-switching, family immigration history, or any aspect of how race shapes their perspective.

The practical consequence: race-based context must now be *earned through narrative*, not provided through demographic boxes. An admissions officer can no longer assume a student's socioeconomic context based on race. That context must be *explained by the student themselves*.

**What this means for essay strategy:** The death of race-based institutional sorting created an opportunity—students with genuine experiences of racial identity or race-based adversity have powerful material to work with. But it also created a liability—many students and consultants misread this as "write about adversity" when what it actually means is "write authentically about your actual experience."

### Shift 2: Landscape Tool Death (September 2025) — The Loss of Automatic Context

The College Board's Landscape tool, introduced in 2019, was a quiet revolution in admissions data. It gave admissions officers neighborhood-level context on every applicant: crime rates, median household income, school quality, college enrollment rates, food insecurity rates, and dozens of other socioeconomic variables tied to zip codes.

Landscape was never visible to students. It worked in the background. An AO pulling up your application would see that you came from a neighborhood in the 8th percentile for income, with a high school ranked in the bottom quartile for college placement. You didn't have to tell them—the data did.

In September 2025, under pressure from the Trump administration's emphasis on "merit-based" admissions and concerns about "hidden racial proxies" (Landscape disproportionately highlighted neighborhoods that are predominantly non-white), College Board discontinued the tool.

**What this means practically:** Admissions officers now have LESS institutional data about a student's circumstances. They can see GPA and test scores. They can see what school you attended. But they cannot automatically see the socioeconomic context of your neighborhood. They can no longer assume anything about your family's income, school resources, or community challenges.

This context now has to come directly from students—through the essay, through the Additional Information section, or not at all.

**The cascading consequence:** Schools scrambled to compensate for this loss. Many schools added explicit prompts about background, circumstance, and adversity to their supplements for the 2025-2026 cycle. Others reworded their Additional Information guidelines to specifically ask for context. They were trying to get information they used to receive automatically.

## The Perfect Storm: Why Adversity Essays Are Now The Biggest Trap

### The Problem in Plain Terms

Landscape's death + SFFA's preservation of narrative rights + executive orders demanding schools prove they're "merit-based" = a collision course where *every admissions consultant in the country* started telling students to emphasize adversity.

Here's what happened:

**The Department of Education's guidance documents** mentioning "adversity" 25 times created a gold rush mentality. Consultants read this as a signal: "Adversity is what colleges want."

**19 major colleges** either dropped or significantly modified their diversity-oriented supplemental prompts for 2025-2026. Why? Fear of executive branch scrutiny. But this created a vacuum. Schools that dropped "Tell us how your identity shapes your perspective" added "Tell us about an obstacle you've overcome" instead. They were still looking for context—just using different language.

**Student behavior shifted dramatically:**
- Students without genuine hardship started manufacturing or exaggerating adversity
- Students with genuine hardship started writing "trauma porn"—raw pain without reflection, agency, or learning
- First-generation students all started with the same "I'll be the first in my family to attend college" opening
- Students from comfortable backgrounds started finding "adversity" in not getting the college they wanted at age 14 or having to choose between two summer internships

**The result:** Admissions officers are now drowning in adversity essays. They're the new "sports injury comeback story." Every third essay is about overcoming something, achieving something, proving something.

And the experienced AO can smell the manufactured ones instantly.

## The Wayfinder Contrarian Position: How Winners Actually Play This

The students who will succeed in the 2025-2026 cycle are NOT the ones who signal hardship the loudest. They are:

### 1. Students Who Show Authentic Context Without Performing Victimhood

There's a crucial difference between:
- "I grew up in poverty and it was devastating" (trauma porn)
- "I grew up in a low-income neighborhood where my parents worked essential jobs. This meant I learned to manage my own schedule early, which developed my independence. Here's how that independence shaped how I approach problems" (authentic context with agency)

The first is self-pitying narrative. The second is contextualized self-understanding.

Experienced AOs can feel the difference in prose immediately. Real adversity survivors don't need to perform their pain for strangers. They can describe it factually.

### 2. Students Who Demonstrate Agency

This is the core distinction that separates competitive essays from self-indulgent ones:

Agency is not "I overcame X." Agency is "Because of X, I built Y" or "In response to X, I learned Z."

Agency requires:
- Naming the circumstance (factual, not emotional)
- Showing what the student did about it (action, not reaction)
- Demonstrating what they learned or created (reflection with insight)

A student who writes "I experienced food insecurity" is just naming a fact. A student who writes "Food insecurity in my household taught me that a single meal could change someone's day, so I started a lunch program at my school that now serves 200 students weekly" is showing agency.

One of these applicants will be memorable to an AO. The other is just another hardship narrative.

### 3. Students Who Use the Partition Strategy

This is the single most important tactical insight for the post-Landscape world:

**The Additional Information section is for FACTUAL CONTEXT. The essay is for WHO YOU ARE.**

Additional Information should answer: "What circumstances do you come from that an admissions officer wouldn't know by looking at GPA and test scores?"

Examples:
- "My parents are immigrants from Nigeria. My mother is a nurse, my father is an engineer. We live in suburban Pennsylvania. My high school is affluent and 87% white. I am one of four Black students in my graduating class."
- "My parents divorced when I was 10. I live primarily with my father and spend weekends with my mother. My father works night shifts as a security guard. I manage most household responsibilities (cooking, cleaning, helping my younger brother with homework)."
- "My high school is in a rural area with limited advanced courses. We do not offer AP courses. The highest level math available is Pre-Calculus, which I completed in 11th grade."
- "I have a younger sibling with cerebral palsy. I serve as a translator between my mother (who speaks limited English) and doctors, schools, and therapists."

Notice the pattern: These are FACTUAL, NEUTRAL, SPECIFIC statements. They don't perform emotion. They don't demand sympathy. They provide information an AO needs to contextualize everything else about your application.

Then, separately, your essay(s) should explore who you ARE as a person, what you think about, what you're curious about, how you engage with problems, what you value.

The essay about your personality doesn't have to be about adversity at all. It could be about:
- Your intellectual obsession with medieval history
- How you think about friendship
- Your approach to failure (not trauma—just how you handle not being the best at something)
- A conversation that changed your mind about something
- How you see problems and imagine solutions
- What makes you laugh
- What you actually care about when no one is watching

This partition is crucial because:
- It prevents AOs from confusing circumstance with identity
- It makes the Additional Information section data-like (factual, useful for file review)
- It frees the essay to be actual writing about an actual person
- It prevents you from over-explaining yourself in one section and then having nothing interesting to say elsewhere

## Strategic Guidance By Student Type

### Scenario 1: Students With Genuine Hardship

**Your advantage:** Real material. Authentic experience. The narrative credibility that students manufacturing hardship cannot fake.

**Your risk:** Treating hardship as your identity rather than your context.

**The partition strategy for you:**

**Additional Information section:**
- Lead with the factual circumstances in neutral language
- Explain what circumstances required you to do or understand or manage
- Stick to what an AO needs to know; avoid performance or emotion
- Length: typically 300-500 words (circumstances don't need much explanation)
- Tone: direct, clear, matter-of-fact

Example: "My mother has been battling kidney disease since I was in 7th grade. This has meant that our family's medical costs are substantial and unpredictable. My father's job as a contractor doesn't provide health insurance. Since 10th grade, I've been responsible for managing our household finances, paying bills, and tracking my mother's medications and appointments. I work 20 hours per week at a local grocery store to contribute to household income. This has limited my extracurricular activity to one club (debate team) and one academic competition (Science Olympiad)."

That's not the essay. That's the context layer.

**Your essay:**
- You can absolutely reference the circumstance ("given how I've had to manage finances and healthcare logistics for my family")
- But the essay should be ABOUT YOUR ACTUAL THINKING, not about your circumstances
- Show what you've developed: resilience is overused; instead show "I've developed a systems-thinking approach to problem-solving," "I learned to make decisions with incomplete information and uncertainty," "I understand how small inefficiencies compound," "I can manage competing priorities with grace"
- Show agency: "Because I've had to navigate complex healthcare systems, I'm interested in healthcare policy and considering medical school" is agency. "My mother is sick and I had to help, so I'm a compassionate person" is just describing what happened.

**The tone calibration:**
- Avoid "despite adversity, I persevered" narratives (everyone knows you did; this is evident from you being here)
- Avoid trauma details (AOs don't need to know your mother's exact diagnosis or the medical procedures involved)
- Avoid blame (don't write "the healthcare system is broken"; write "I learned how complex healthcare systems are")
- Be specific enough to be credible, general enough to maintain dignity

**When to be explicit vs. when to let the reader infer:**
- Explicit: circumstances that aren't visible in your file (family financial situation, care responsibilities, school resource gaps, neighborhood context)
- Implied: emotional impact (don't tell them "it was hard"; show it through your choices and thinking)

**How much detail is enough (less is often more):**
- The rule of thumb: if you can explain it in one sentence, don't take a paragraph
- One sentence per major circumstance: "My family's income is below the poverty line"; "I have significant care responsibilities for a family member"; "My school has limited AP/honors options"
- One paragraph per circumstance to explain the practical implications
- Total: 3-5 paragraphs maximum for Additional Information

### Scenario 2: Students WITHOUT Genuine Hardship (The Majority of Applicants)

**Your situation:** You grew up in a stable household. Your parents are both alive, employed, and capable. You went to a decent public school or a good private school. Your family has enough money. You had summer camps, music lessons, family vacations. You haven't experienced serious illness or loss. You weren't homeless or food insecure. You didn't work 20 hours a week at 16.

This describes approximately 65% of students applying to selective colleges, yet fewer than 20% of college essays actually own this fact.

**Why this matters:** Admissions officers KNOW most applicants are privileged. They're not suspicious of privilege. They're suspicious of people trying to perform unprivileged.

**The trap you must avoid:**
- Calling minor inconveniences "adversity" (having to wake up early for school, getting a B, not making the varsity team, your parents getting divorced when you were 2)
- Manufacturing hardship (this is transparent and insulting to students with actual hardship)
- The "challenge of privilege" essay done wrong (which reads as completely tone-deaf)

**What actually works:**

**Option 1: Write about something you're genuinely interested in, period.**

Your essay doesn't need to be about overcoming anything. It can be:
- An intellectual curiosity (why you're fascinated by environmental chemistry, or ancient Rome, or game theory)
- A community contribution that isn't framed as "I overcame to help others" but as "I noticed a problem and built something"
- Your values and how you live them (not performatively, but actually)
- A conversation or experience that changed how you think about something
- Your actual sense of humor, personality, or weirdness

Example: "I'm obsessed with the economics of farmers markets. I've spent the last two years interviewing 30+ farmers in three states about why they participate in farmers markets rather than selling to distributors, what they sell, and how economics of scale work differently when you're face-to-face with customers. Here's what I've learned..." (This is interesting. This is you. This doesn't require adversity.)

**Option 2: The "challenge of privilege" done right**

If you want to write about being privileged, you can—but it requires sophistication:

Wrong: "I realized how lucky I am, so I volunteered at a homeless shelter and it changed my perspective." (This is narcissistic and framed around your self-realization.)

Right: "I grew up in the suburbs assuming that effort + intelligence = success. In 10th grade, I started volunteering and realized how much luck and circumstance I had. Instead of writing a self-congratulatory essay about this realization, I'm now studying economic policy because I want to understand the actual structural mechanisms that create inequality. Here's what I'm reading..." (This is self-aware and action-oriented.)

**Option 3: Intellectual humility and growth**

You don't need adversity to have grown. You can show growth through:
- Changing your mind about something important (and explaining why and how)
- Failing at something you cared about and learning something real from it (not "sports injury comeback" but actual failure in something meaningful to you)
- Realizing how much you don't know (this is underrated in essays)
- Pursuing something difficult because it's hard, not because you had to

**The permission structure:**
Most students from privileged backgrounds feel they need to apologize for their privilege or downplay it in their essays. You don't. Admissions officers expect privilege. What they want is honesty, self-awareness, and something actually interesting about how you think.

Your advantage is that you probably have time and resources to pursue genuine intellectual or community interests. Lean into that. Don't perform hardship you don't have.

### Scenario 3: First-Generation Students

**Your power:** First-generation status is still one of the most meaningful contexts in admissions. You bring perspective, resilience, and a unique understanding of education's transformative power that many students don't have.

**Your challenge:** The first-gen narrative has become standardized to the point of cliché. "I will be the first in my family to attend college" is such a common opening that it's now a liability rather than an asset.

**The overused narrative:** "My parents didn't go to college, so they didn't know how to help me, so I had to figure everything out myself, and now I'm grateful and determined to make them proud."

Every AO has read this 200 times. It's real. It's powerful. And it's tired.

**What actually works:**

**Reframe: What's your unique perspective that comes from being first-gen?**

Don't lead with the fact of being first-gen. Lead with what that means you understand or see differently:

- "Most of my friends assume college is the next obvious step. I understand it differently—as a concrete opportunity to change trajectory. This makes me more intentional about why I'm applying to specific schools and what I want to study."
- "My parents couldn't help me navigate the college process, so I had to become my own research project. I learned to find information, evaluate options, ask questions. These are skills I didn't know I was developing."
- "I grew up hearing education described as a key—the key to better jobs, better circumstances, better lives. I'm now skeptical of that simple framing and interested in understanding what education actually does and doesn't do."
- "My parents believe deeply in education but don't have the language for what happens in college. So when I talk to them about what I'm studying, I'm building the language together with them."

Notice the pattern: These don't center your family's limitation; they center your actual thinking.

**In your Additional Information:**
- Yes, name that you're first-generation. It matters.
- Specify: are you first-gen college? First-gen to attend a selective college? First-gen in your family in the US?
- If relevant, explain how your family's background shapes your circumstances (language barriers, financial constraints, navigation challenges)
- Keep it factual and brief

**In your essay:**
- Assume they know you're first-gen from the app
- Write about your actual intellectual interests, questions, or growth
- If you want to reference first-gen status, do it as context for something else, not as the main topic
- Show what your perspective brings (not what your family's limitation requires you to overcome)

**The dignity frame:**
When you write about your family, assume they're smart and capable, even if they weren't able to help with college navigation. This matters for tone. It's the difference between:
- "My parents couldn't help me because they didn't know how" (diminishing)
- "My parents couldn't help me navigate college processes they hadn't experienced" (factual and respectful)

Your essay isn't about your family. It's about you. Reference your family context only as needed.

### Scenario 4: International Students

**Your complexity:** The SFFA + Landscape changes affect you differently than US-based students.

**SFFA's impact on international students:**
- International students are not affected by SFFA's race-consciousness ban (SFFA applied to US-based institutions, and universities handle international and US applications separately)
- However, you can absolutely still write about your cultural identity, national context, or immigrant experience in your family
- But you should assume that your experience of "race" or ethnicity is different from US racial categories. You can write authentically about your identity without trying to fit it into US racial politics.

**Landscape's impact on international students:**
- You were never in Landscape (it only covered US neighborhoods)
- So you lose less. But you gain: any context about your school, country, or circumstances now needs explicit explanation
- The Additional Information section becomes even more critical for you than for US-based students

**What to explain in Additional Information:**
- Your educational system (many US AOs are unfamiliar with IB, A-Levels, or national systems they don't recognize)
- Context about your school (is it a selective independent school or a government school? How does it compare in your country?)
- Economic context (cost of living is cheaper in some countries; what does your family's income actually mean in your context?)
- Any academic disruption (school closures from conflict, climate events, political instability)
- Language barriers (if English is not your first language, you can note this; it contextualizes your application)

**What to write about in essays:**
- Your actual curiosity and thinking (don't center "I come from another country" as your identity)
- What you understand about your country/culture that Americans might not (without condescending explanation)
- Genuine questions you have or problems you want to solve
- Your perspective as an outsider looking in (to the US, or to other cultures)

**The balance:**
- Explain context without centering your national origin as your personality
- Show what your background brings to your thinking without exoticizing it
- Don't apologize for being international; own it as part of your perspective

## The New Supplemental Prompt Landscape: Reading What Schools Actually Want

In response to the Landscape tool's death and political pressure around racial equity, schools made dramatic changes to supplemental prompts for 2025-2026.

### What Changed:

**Schools that ADDED adversity/context prompts:**
These schools recognized that without Landscape data, they needed explicit prompts to get circumstantial context. Examples:
- Many added: "Tell us about a circumstance or challenge in your background that shaped you"
- Some added: "What should we know about you that's not apparent from your transcript and scores?"
- A few added: "How does your background inform your perspective?"

These are schools trying to solve the Landscape gap. They WANT to know about your circumstances. That's not pressure; that's actual interest.

**Schools that REMOVED diversity prompts:**
19 major colleges dropped or significantly modified prompts that explicitly asked about identity, background, or diversity. Examples of what was removed:
- "How do you identify, and how has that shaped you?"
- "Tell us about your community and your role in it"
- "What does diversity mean to you?"

These removals signal political caution, not philosophical change. Most of these schools still value diversity; they're just nervous about being visibly committed to it under current political pressure.

**Schools that REWORDED to be more oblique:**
Some schools kept the intent of the prompt but made it harder to target. Examples:
- Old: "Tell us about your identity"
- New: "Tell us about a community you belong to"
- Old: "How has your race shaped your experience?"
- New: "What perspective do you bring that would be different from other applicants?"

The new wording asks the same thing, but it's safer from political scrutiny (you're not explicitly mentioning race, so it's harder to accuse the school of race-consciousness).

### How to Read What Each School Actually Wants:

**Rule 1: Prompts reveal institutional values more than policy does.**

If a school ADDED an adversity or context prompt, they're saying: "We want to understand your circumstances." Answer that directly and factually.

If a school REMOVED a diversity prompt but you still see value in your identity/background, you can still write about it in your main essay or through the "Tell us about yourself" space that almost every school has.

If a school REWROTE a prompt to be more oblique, understand that they still care about the same thing, they're just being more cautious with language. "What community do you belong to?" is just a safer way to ask "Tell us about your identity/culture/background."

**Rule 2: Compare the school's public statements with their prompts.**

If a school's website talks about "diversity and inclusion" but their supplements don't ask about identity, that's a signal that they're afraid of political scrutiny. You can still write about identity if it's core to who you are, but understand the institutional ambivalence.

If a school's website doesn't emphasize diversity much, but their supplements include "Tell us about your background" prompts, they're prioritizing understanding all applicants' contexts, not specifically seeking underrepresented perspectives.

**Rule 3: Short prompts often matter more than long ones.**

A 50-word prompt about "your background" matters more than a 500-word prompt about "an intellectual problem you want to solve." Why? Because the school's word budget is limited. What they ask about directly is what they're prioritizing.

## The Additional Information Section: Now The Primary Mechanism For Context

With the Landscape tool eliminated, the Additional Information section (or "Additional Context" or "Anything Else" depending on the platform) has become the PRIMARY mechanism for providing adversity and circumstantial context to admissions officers.

Understand this: This section is not optional. If you have circumstances that shaped your application, this section is where you explain them.

### What to Include:

**Factual circumstances that an admissions officer wouldn't know from GPA/scores:**

- Family structure and household dynamics (parental occupation, family income tier, primary language spoken at home, immigration/refugee status)
- Care or financial responsibilities (who do you support; what are your household duties)
- Educational limitations (school size, course offerings, resources, college counselor caseload)
- Academic disruption (absences from health, family emergencies, moves, school closures)
- Health or family issues (chronic illness, disability, mental health challenges, loss of family members)
- Community context (neighborhood safety, housing stability, food/housing insecurity, environmental hazards)
- Financial aid situation (can state if you need significant aid; this contextualizes your school list)

**Specific, brief examples:**
- "My father works as a commercial painter. My mother is a paralegal. We live in Atlanta. My household income is approximately $95,000/year. I am one of four of six children attending college; my older siblings attended state schools. I worked 10 hours/week during the school year and 30 hours/week in summers to contribute to household income and save for college."
- "I have a younger brother with autism. I have significant responsibility for his daily care (transportation, meal prep, behavioral support, homework help). My mother works full-time as a nurse; my father passed away when I was 12."
- "My high school is in a rural area. We do not have AP courses available. The highest level science is Honors Chemistry, which I took in 11th grade. My school does not offer language courses beyond Spanish 2. The nearest library is 25 minutes away."
- "I have had significant health challenges (anxiety and depression) treated with medication and therapy since 9th grade. This affected my grades in 9th and 10th grade, when I was unmedicated and undiagnosed. Since treatment, my grades have returned to my baseline."

Notice the pattern: factual, specific, neutral, brief.

### What NOT to Include:

- **Sob stories:** Don't perform emotion or ask for sympathy. "It was devastatingly hard" is performance. Factual description is more powerful.
- **Blame or excuses:** Don't use this section to explain away grades or low test scores. "I did poorly in calculus because my teacher was bad" is an excuse. "I took calculus while managing significant family responsibilities" is context.
- **Over-disclosure:** Medical privacy matters. You don't need to explain your sister's exact diagnosis or your father's imprisonment. General categories are enough: "family member's serious illness," "a family member's incarceration," etc.
- **Future plans or aspirations:** This is not the place to explain why you want to be a doctor or what you'll study. Stick to context, not goals.
- **Things that are already visible in the application:** Don't repeat that you're first-gen or from an underrepresented background if that's already in the app. Add context only if there's something not apparent from the file.

### The Tone:

- **Neutral, not emotional:** Your circumstances don't need emotional language. Let the facts speak.
- **Matter-of-fact, not self-pitying:** You're explaining context, not asking for sympathy.
- **Brief and specific:** Don't give every detail; give enough detail to be credible and useful.
- **Respectful of everyone involved:** Don't diminish parents, family members, or your community. Be factual and dignified.

Example of bad tone:
"My childhood was devastating. My parents were poor and I had to work constantly and never got to do fun things like normal kids. It was so hard and unfair."

Example of good tone:
"My family's income is approximately $35,000/year. I worked 20 hours/week throughout high school to contribute to household expenses. This limited my extracurricular participation but developed my time management and responsibility."

See the difference? One is self-pitying narrative. One is contextual explanation. Both describe similar circumstances. Experienced AOs respond to the second one.

### Word Count Strategy:

The Additional Information section usually allows 250-650 words depending on platform. Use the space you need, but not more:

- **If you have 1-2 major circumstances:** 250-350 words is plenty
- **If you have multiple layered circumstances:** 400-550 words
- **Rarely should you exceed 650 words unless the prompt specifically asks for more

Structure suggestion:
- Opening sentence: "I want to provide context about my circumstances that may not be apparent from my application."
- 1 paragraph per major circumstance (family structure, care responsibilities, educational limitations, health issues, financial situation)
- Closing sentence (optional): "This context shapes how I approach my education and my future."

That's it. You don't need elaborate transitions or storytelling. This is data.

### When to Leave It Blank:

Yes, some students should not use the Additional Information section.

Leave it blank if:
- You have no material circumstances worth explaining (your application is straightforward; you grew up in stable circumstances; you had access to resources)
- Everything relevant is already explained in your essays
- You're tempted to use it for things that belong in essays (your intellectual interests, your values, your personality)
- You have no authentic circumstances to describe and would be manufacturing context

This is more honorable than most students do. Most students feel obligated to fill every box. You're not. If you don't have circumstances that contextualize your application, leave it blank. AOs read silence as "this is a straightforward file" which is perfectly acceptable.

### How AOs Actually Use This Section in Committee:

Understanding how your Additional Information section actually gets read will change how you write it:

1. **First read (triage): "Does this student have circumstances that explain significant gaps in their profile?"**
   - If you have low grades or test scores, this is where you explain why
   - If you have minimal extracurriculars, this is where you explain if there are responsibilities preventing participation
   - If there's anything unusual about your transcript, this clarifies it

2. **Context layer: "What do I need to know about this student's circumstances to evaluate them fairly?"**
   - Financial aid situation (helps determine if they can afford to attend)
   - Educational access issues (affects how heavily to weight GPA)
   - Circumstances that demonstrate resilience or maturity
   - Anything that makes the rest of the application make sense

3. **Integrity check: "Does this student's Additional Information align with what I see elsewhere in their application?"**
   - If they claim significant care responsibilities, do their extracurriculars reflect that?
   - If they claim educational limitations, does their school list make sense?
   - If they describe family circumstances, does anything in their essays contradict this?

4. **Late reading: After the committee has seen grades, essays, and other materials, AOs may return to Additional Information to finalize their judgment.**
   - This is where small contextual details tip the balance
   - This is where "My school didn't offer AP courses" explains why another student who had more AP options is weighted more heavily

The key insight: Your Additional Information section is not where you tell your story. It's where you provide the data that helps AOs contextualize the story you tell in your essays.

## The Strategic Partition: The Core Framework For Post-Landscape Success

The single most important strategic insight for the 2025-2026 admissions cycle is the partition between Additional Information and Essays:

**Additional Information = Context (factual)**
Essays = Identity/Thinking (narrative)

This partition solves multiple problems simultaneously:

1. **It prevents over-explanation.** You don't need to explain your entire life story in the main essay. The essay can actually be about something interesting.

2. **It makes your circumstances data-like.** AOs can scan Additional Information quickly and extract useful context without having to parse emotional narrative.

3. **It prevents the "trauma-as-identity" trap.** Your circumstances are context. Your identity is what you do with context.

4. **It frees your essays to be actually interesting.** Your essay doesn't have to justify your existence or perform your pain. It can explore ideas, show how you think, reveal your personality.

5. **It solves the Landscape gap.** Without automatic data about your circumstances, you're providing it yourself—but in a structured, scannable way that works for admissions officers.

6. **It handles political sensitivity.** If a school has removed diversity prompts for political reasons, you can still provide full context in Additional Information without forcing schools to explicitly ask for identity information.

### The partition in practice:

**Same student, two approaches:**

**Approach 1: Everything in the essay (the old trap)**
"I grew up in a low-income neighborhood where my mother worked two jobs to keep us housed and fed. My father was incarcerated when I was eight. I became responsible for my younger brother's care. School was my escape. I worked at McDonald's starting in 10th grade. I maintained a 3.8 GPA despite everything. I'm determined to make my mother proud and be the first in my family to attend college."

Problems: This is narrative-heavy, it conflates circumstances with identity, it's emotionally manipulative, it centers the student's suffering, and by the time you get to the interesting part (what they actually value or think about), you've used all your words.

**Approach 2: Partition (the new strategy)**
