## OVERVIEW

This diagnostic framework enables admissions coaches to read any college essay draft and identify exactly what's wrong within 5 minutes. It's organized in four tiers of increasing specificity: the initial read, story-level problems, execution-level problems, and school-fit mismatches.

Each diagnosis includes a severity rating (1-5), root-cause analysis, specific coaching interventions, and success signals so coaches know when a revision has addressed the core issue.

---

## TIER 1: THE INITIAL READ (First 30 Seconds)

These four diagnostic questions separate functional essays from broken ones. They should be evaluated in order—each answer narrows the scope of potential problems.

### Question 1: Does the Essay Have a Specific Moment, Scene, or Story?

**What to Look For:**
- A concrete scene with sensory details (dialogue, physical action, time/place markers)
- Specific people, places, objects named, not generic references
- The essay grounded in *when* and *where*, not floating in abstraction
- A narrative arc with something happening, changing, or being revealed

**When Present (Positive Signal):**
- The essay has narrative weight; the reader experiences something, not just hears about it
- The writer is showing, not telling—demonstrating character through action
- Foundation exists for meaning-making; something specific is being reflected upon
- Path forward is clear: refine the moment, deepen the reflection

**When Absent (Red Flag):**
- Essay relies entirely on abstract reflection, claims, or explanations
- Reader encounters only ideas without anchoring scenes
- Typical language: "I've always believed," "Throughout my life," "One important lesson was..."
- Indicates either story-selection failure or structural confusion (reflection without narrative)

**Diagnostic Decision Tree:**

```
If SPECIFIC MOMENT present:
  → Move to Question 2

If NO SPECIFIC MOMENT:
  → Diagnosis Path: MISSING NARRATIVE (Tier 2)
     - Check: Is there a good story that the writer just hasn't shown yet?
     - Check: Is the chosen topic inherently abstract/unstoried?
     - Severity: 3-4 (may need structural rebuild or story choice revision)
```

---

### Question 2: Can You Hear a Distinct Human Voice in the First Paragraph?

**What to Look For:**
- Vocabulary and phrasing that feels natural, not formal or polished
- Sentence structure varies (some short, some longer—not uniform)
- Personal idiom, humor, or distinctive perspective comes through
- The reader hears *this specific person*, not a generic applicant
- Absence of: clichés, thesaurus-upgraded language, "essay-voice" formality

**When Present (Positive Signal):**
- Reader connects immediately with a person, not an application
- Voice carries through the essay—consistent personality
- Authenticity reads as credible; the student sounds their age
- Foundation for emotional engagement and relatability

**When Absent (Red Flag):**
- Writing feels "adult," over-polished, or written-for-admissions
- Vocabulary feels deployed rather than natural ("utilize" instead of "use")
- Every sentence sounds intentional in a way that kills authenticity
- Common indicators: parent or counselor involvement, overwriting, AI polish

**Diagnostic Decision Tree:**

```
If DISTINCT VOICE present:
  → Move to Question 3

If NO DISTINCT VOICE:
  → Diagnosis Path: VOICE FAILURE (Tier 3)
     - Check: Is this the student's natural writing or someone else's?
     - Check: Has the student over-edited for "essay tone"?
     - Severity: 2-3 (usually fixable with permission to sound like themselves)
```

**Voice Red Flags:**
- First paragraph uses words like "pivotal," "formative," "journey," "passion"
- Sentence structure: complex-complex-complex (no variation or short punches)
- Tone: formal, distant, detached
- Consistency check: Does the writer sound different on page 2?

---

### Question 3: Is There a Clear "So What"? Does the Reader Know Why This Matters to the Writer?

**What to Look For:**
- By the end of the essay, the reader understands *why* the writer is telling this story
- The connection between the specific moment and something larger about the student is explicit
- Reader can articulate: "This student values X" or "This experience matters because Y"
- The "so what" feels earned, not declared
- Absence of: forcing a life lesson, overgeneralization, unclear relevance

**When Present (Positive Signal):**
- Essay has purpose and direction
- Reader walks away understanding not just what happened, but why it matters
- Reflection is present and integrated (not just appended at the end)
- Foundation for school fit analysis: the reader knows something about what drives this student

**When Absent (Red Flag):**
- Reader finishes essay unclear why they were told this story
- Narrative and reflection are disconnected (great story, but meaning is forced or absent)
- Typical pattern: "And this experience taught me to work harder" (generic, disconnected moral)
- Essay feels pointless—entertaining or emotional, but not meaningful

**Diagnostic Decision Tree:**

```
If "SO WHAT" is Clear:
  → Move to Question 4

If "SO WHAT" is Unclear or Missing:
  → Diagnosis Path: MISSING REFLECTION (Tier 3)
     - Check: Does the reflection exist but feel disconnected?
     - Check: Is the moment powerful but the "why it matters" forced?
     - Severity: 2-4 (usually fixable with coaching on meaning-making)
```

**"So What" Red Flags:**
- Last paragraph pivots to a moral lesson disconnected from the story
- Writer makes claims ("This made me a better person") without evidence
- Reader can't articulate what value or insight the essay reveals
- Reflection is all abstract; the specific detail doesn't illuminate anything
- Essay could be about a different student; it's not student-specific

---

### Question 4: Does It Surprise You at Least Once?

**What to Look For:**
- An unexpected turn, insight, or detail that shifts the reader's understanding
- Writer reveals something about themselves that's non-obvious
- A moment where the essay takes a direction the reader didn't anticipate
- A detail or connection that feels fresh, not predictable
- Can be: twist in the narrative, unexpected humor, honest vulnerability, counterintuitive insight

**When Present (Positive Signal):**
- Essay stands out; reader is engaged and curious
- Indicates genuine self-knowledge and willingness to be honest
- Shows the writer is thinking, not just producing expected content
- Creates memorable moment that AOs will remember the student by

**When Absent (Red Flag):**
- Essay is competent but forgettable
- Every element is predictable: topic choice, narrative arc, conclusion
- Indicates either safe topic selection or underdeveloped thinking
- Suggests writer is performing "good essay" rather than being authentic

**Diagnostic Decision Tree:**

```
If SURPRISE PRESENT:
  → Move to TIER 1 Synthesis

If NO SURPRISE:
  → Diagnosis Path: LACK OF DISTINCTIVENESS (Tier 2)
     - Check: Is the topic inherently safe or overdone?
     - Check: Does the writer have deeper insight they're not sharing?
     - Severity: 2-4 (depends on whether this is topic or execution)
```

**Surprise Red Flags:**
- Topic is on the "10,000 times per year" list (overcoming hardship, leadership, sports, volunteer trip)
- Writer takes the predictable path through the narrative
- Insight at the end is a cliché ("adversity made me stronger")
- Student plays it safe; nothing vulnerable or risk-taking
- The essay reads like what the student *thinks* admissions wants, not what they actually think

## TIER 2: STORY-LEVEL DIAGNOSIS

If the essay fails Tier 1 (especially questions 1 or 4), the problem may be at the story level—the topic or narrative choice itself, not the execution. These diagnoses address systemic story problems.

### The "Nothing Happened" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Student chose a low-stakes topic with minimal narrative arc
- Essay is entirely internal reflection with no external event
- Typical topics: "A time I failed a test," "Why I love science," "My biggest strength"
- Writing may be competent, but the foundation is thin

**Example Content Patterns:**
- "I've always loved reading because it..." (no specific moment)
- "During the pandemic, I learned to..." (universal experience, not distinctive)
- "Working at my part-time job taught me..." (no scene, just reflection)

**Root Cause:**
- Student was afraid to risk vulnerability; chose safe, broad topic
- Student didn't understand that essays need narrative, not just reflection
- Limited life experience and uncertain what's "essay-worthy"
- Coach pressure or institutional messaging: "Write about your achievements"

**Severity:** 3/5 (fixable if better story exists; requires new approach if not)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Walk me through the most embarrassing moment you've had in the last two years. What happened?"
2. "Tell me about a time you changed your mind about something. What made you see it differently?"
3. "What's something you do that your friends would be surprised to know about?"
4. "When was a time you felt completely out of place?"
5. "Is there something you've kept hidden because you thought people would judge you?"

**Goal of Questions:** Uncover stories with actual stakes, vulnerability, or stakes that didn't make it into the essay.

**Success Signal:**
- Student identifies a more compelling, specific moment
- New story has a clear turning point or revelation
- Student expresses, "Oh, I didn't think that was essay-worthy"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Explain: "Essays aren't about accomplishments—they're about moments where you learned something about yourself, or saw the world differently."
- Reframe: "The best essays are when you're surprised by your own reaction or realization."
- Assign: "Tell me three specific moments in the last year when something shifted in how you saw something."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Identify the moment with genuine stakes (vulnerability, change, or surprise)
2. Rebuild essay around that moment as the core narrative
3. Show reflection emerging *from* the moment, not imposed on it

---

### The "Everything Happened" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Student tries to cover 4-5 different stories or moments in one essay
- Paragraphs jump between topics without coherent thread
- Each idea gets 1-2 sentences before moving to the next
- Structure reads like a list or montage, not a cohesive narrative
- Common in: students trying to show "depth of experience" or resume-style essays

**Example Content Patterns:**
- "First, I led a fundraiser. Second, I played varsity soccer. Third, I volunteered in Peru. Finally, I learned Spanish."
- Paragraph breaks coincide with life events, not narrative logic
- Each section could be its own story, but none gets developed

**Root Cause:**
- Student misunderstood the task: thought they needed to justify all their activities
- Anxiety about leaving things out; trying to make a comprehensive case
- Lack of focus or discipline (scattered thinking, not focused narrative)
- Teacher or counselor advice: "Show all your strengths"

**Severity:** 3/5 (structure needs rebuild; usually has good material buried in it)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Of all these moments, which one genuinely surprised you or taught you something unexpected?"
2. "If you could only tell me one story that would help me understand you, which would it be?"
3. "Which of these experiences connects most directly to a value you hold or a way you see yourself?"
4. "Where do you appear in these stories? Which ones show you in a moment of vulnerability or growth?"
5. "Which story would you be disappointed if the admissions officer didn't read?"

**Goal of Questions:** Help student identify the one story with deepest personal resonance.

**Success Signal:**
- Student can articulate why one story matters more than others
- Proposed revision focuses on single narrative with depth
- Student accepts that leaving out accomplishments is okay

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Explain: "Essays aren't a resume. They're about ONE moment that reveals something true about you."
- Demonstrate: Show how trying to cover everything dilutes the impact of everything
- Reframe: "You've proven your achievements in your activities list and transcript. This essay is about *how you think* and *what you care about*."
- Assign: "Pick the story that makes you most uncomfortable or vulnerable. That's probably your essay."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Identify the narrative with deepest personal stakes
2. Remove all other stories
3. Expand that story to fill the essay (scene-setting, sensory detail, reflection)
4. Cut mercilessly; every sentence should serve that single narrative

### The "Wrong Story" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Student picked a topic that's technically true but *safe*
- There's a more honest, vulnerable story the student is avoiding
- Essay reads like "what I think admissions wants" not "what's actually true about me"
- Common pattern: student chose an achievement or a leadership story when the real story is failure or confusion
- Indicators: essay feels rehearsed, polished before even drafting, delivered with apology ("this probably isn't good")

**Example Content Patterns:**
- Student writes about leading a club when the real story is the depression that made them quit
- Student writes about their heritage to fill a diversity essay when the real story is culture clash
- Student writes about a community service project to highlight achievement when the real story is learning they don't want to go into that field

**Root Cause:**
- Fear of judgment or misunderstanding
- Assumption that admissions wants to hear about strengths, not vulnerabilities
- Previous feedback that discouraged honesty ("That's too personal" or "Don't share that")
- Cultural or family messaging: "Don't air dirty laundry"
- Counselor or parent pressure toward a "safer" narrative

**Severity:** 3-4/5 (essay itself may be well-written, but the foundation is wrong)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "What's the story you're *not* telling in this essay?"
2. "If this essay wasn't going to admissions, what would you write instead?"
3. "What part of your life would you be embarrassed if admissions found out about?"
4. "Is there something you've been keeping quiet because you thought it would hurt your application?"
5. "If I told you this essay was just for me and admissions would never see it, would you tell a different story?"

**Goal of Questions:** Create a safe space for the student to admit the real story.

**Success Signal:**
- Student pauses and acknowledges a truer, less polished story
- Student expresses relief or vulnerability: "I was afraid that story would hurt me"
- Student can articulate why the safer story is not actually about them

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Normalize: "The best essays are often about moments that feel risky to share. That's usually a sign you're onto something real."
- Challenge: "Tell me—what's the story you're not telling? Not because it's worse, but because it's more true?"
- Reframe: "Admissions doesn't need more proof that you're accomplished. They want to understand you."
- Permission: "You're allowed to fail in this essay. You're allowed to be confused. That's human."
- Assign: "Write the story you've been avoiding for 20 minutes. Don't edit it. Just write it."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Acknowledge that the safer story isn't the real one
2. Coach through the vulnerability of sharing the true story
3. Identify what makes the true story risky (vulnerability, failure, confusion, unpopularity)
4. Reframe risk as power: "This is what makes you memorable"
5. Build the essay around the truer narrative

### The "Someone Else's Story" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- It's technically the student's story, but the voice and framing feel like someone else's
- Phrasing is sophisticated in ways inconsistent with the student's age/ability
- Structural coherence and argument sophistication suggest adult influence
- Plot points or insights seem coached or curated rather than organically developed
- Student is vague about their own motivations within the story ("My mom thought this would be good" or "My counselor suggested I write about...")
- Writing quality is distinctly higher than other student writing samples

**Example Content Patterns:**
- Story involves accomplishment that parent clearly orchestrated
- Narrative structure is polished in a way that suggests significant outside editing
- Insight or conclusion feels grafted on, not earned from the story
- Student describes what happened and what they learned, but the learning sounds like an adult's interpretation

**Red Flags:**
- Student is hesitant or apologetic about their own essay
- Multiple drafts have become progressively "better" but also more formulaic
- When asked to talk about the essay verbally, student is less articulate than in writing
- Essay contains ideas or vocabulary the student doesn't naturally use in conversation

**Root Cause:**
- Well-meaning parent or counselor has over-edited or co-written
- Student was too deferential to authority figures in the drafting process
- Institutional pressure ("Your essay needs to be this good") led to outside help
- Student lacks confidence and assumed they couldn't write it themselves
- Cultural expectation that parents guide heavily (common in some communities)

**Severity:** 4-5/5 (The essay itself may be good, but it's not admissible as the student's work; requires radical rebuild)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Walk me through writing this essay. How much of it is in your exact voice?"
2. "What did your parents/counselor/teacher change about your draft?"
3. "If you had to write this again today without any edits, what would change?"
4. "Tell me this story out loud, as you'd tell it to a friend. Now let's compare to what you wrote."
5. "Are there parts of this essay that don't sound like you?"

**Goal of Questions:** Get student to recognize the gap between their authentic voice and what's on the page.

**Success Signal:**
- Student admits that parts of the essay aren't authentically theirs
- Student can rewrite key sentences in their actual voice
- Student expresses relief at permission to sound "less polished"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Be honest (but kind): "This essay sounds like it's been heavily edited. Part of what admissions wants to hear is your voice, not a polished version of your voice."
- Set a boundary: "I can give you feedback, but I can't rewrite. This essay has to come from you."
- Rebuild protocol:
  - Student rewrites draft with explicit instruction: "Write this like you're texting a friend, not writing for adults"
  - Student reads aloud; coach marks where it sounds off
  - Student does final revision with goal of authentic voice
- Assign: "Write a paragraph about this story without showing it to anyone until I see it. Just you and the page."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Acknowledge the over-editing without shame ("This is so common when adults try to help")
2. Establish a boundary: student's voice, student's insights
3. Have student read aloud; mark sentences that don't sound like them
4. Rebuild essay with explicit permission to sound like a 17-year-old, not a 45-year-old
5. Implement: Coach and student only; no other edits

### The "Trauma Dump" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay describes a significant hardship, loss, abuse, or difficult experience
- The experience is genuine and powerful, but the essay is overwhelmingly narrative
- Little to no reflection on meaning or growth
- Reader feels the weight of the experience but doesn't understand what it means to the writer
- May read as cry for help or emotional venting rather than an application essay
- Student has not processed the experience enough to reflect on it

**Example Content Patterns:**
- Essay is a blow-by-blow account of a parent's illness or death
- Details of abuse or trauma are graphic or extensive
- Student describes the experience but doesn't say what they learned or how it changed them
- Ending is abrupt or generic ("This made me stronger" without evidence)
- Reader is moved but also uncomfortable, unsure what role they're supposed to play

**Red Flags:**
- Essay reads like a journal entry or therapy session
- No distance or perspective; experience is still raw
- Student describes others' reactions ("My mom cried") but not their own internal reality
- Heavy focus on suffering without agency or growth
- Sentence: "I don't usually tell people this story"

**Root Cause:**
- Genuine, significant trauma that hasn't been processed
- Belief that admissions *should* know this story, without clarity on why
- Counselor told student to write about it without sufficient coaching on how
- Student's definition of essay: "tell what happened" not "reflect on what it meant"
- Insufficient emotional processing time (too recent)

**Severity:** 3-5/5 (depends on how close the trauma is; may need to wait; otherwise requires significant coaching)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Have you talked to anyone about this experience? A counselor, therapist, or trusted adult?"
2. "When you think about this experience, are you still processing it, or have you made sense of it?"
3. "What do you want admissions to understand about you as a result of this experience?"
4. "How has this experience changed you? Not 'suffering changed me' but specifically—what do you believe or do differently?"
5. "If you had to pick one moment when you realized something was different, what would it be?"

**Goal of Questions:** Assess whether the student has processed enough to reflect, and identify the real insight underneath the narrative.

**Success Signal:**
- Student can articulate something they understand about themselves *because* of the experience
- Essay shifts from "here's what happened" to "here's what this taught me about myself"
- Student demonstrates agency: "I made a choice" or "I realized" rather than just "this happened to me"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Normalize: "Writing about difficult experiences is brave and important. And it's also tricky because we have to do it in a way that gives readers understanding, not discomfort."
- Create space: "Tell me—what is it that admissions needs to know about you? Not the experience itself, but what it reveals about who you are?"
- Establish coaching rule: "We're going to zoom out. Instead of the full story, pick one moment that changed something for you."
- Reframe: "This isn't about the trauma. It's about you—your resilience, your values, your way of moving forward."
- Assign: "Write about one moment that surprised you during this experience. Or one moment when you realized something."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Acknowledge the significance of the experience
2. Establish: this essay is not about the event; it's about what the event revealed about the writer
3. Zoom in: pick a single moment or realization, not the full narrative
4. Add distance: focus on internal processing, growth, or changed perspective
5. Ensure agency: the student is not a victim in the essay; they are someone navigating and understanding

**Optional Severity Note:** If the trauma is very recent (less than 6 months) or unprocessed, recommend that the student choose a different topic for the personal statement and use Additional Information to briefly contextualize (without the full narrative).

### The "Humble Brag" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay is nominally about reflection or a challenge, but actually about achievement
- Student frames an accomplishment as a learning moment to make it seem more authentic
- Excessive details about success, praise, or awards
- The "challenge" is actually a humble brag ("I had to choose between my two acceptances" or "Everyone wanted me to start, but I decided to focus on academics")
- Typical phrasing: "I thought I had failed, but actually, I succeeded"
- Reads as achievement-focused, not insight-focused

**Example Content Patterns:**
- "I didn't make the first team initially, but then I became captain and won the championship"
- "My first restaurant concept failed, but now I've successfully launched three"
- "I was worried about my GPA, but it's actually a 4.0"
- "People underestimated me because I was [privileged demographic], but I proved them wrong"

**Red Flags:**
- The challenge is always resolved in the student's favor
- Story includes dialogue where others praise the student
- Excessive quantification: "won 12 competitions," "raised $50K," "300 volunteer hours"
- Vulnerability is claimed but not actually present
- Essay answers "How great am I?" rather than "Who am I?"

**Root Cause:**
- Student genuinely achieved something and wants to discuss it, but chose essay instead of résumé
- Counselor suggested framing achievements as "learning moments"
- Student believes admissions wants to hear about success, not failure
- Misunderstanding of what reflection means: "I thought about what I did well"
- Cultural messaging: celebrate success publicly

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (the essay doesn't have fundamental problems; it's just off-target)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "What was genuinely hard about this experience? Not the obstacle, but your internal struggle?"
2. "When did you doubt yourself? What did you think about that doubt?"
3. "What did you learn that surprised you? Something you didn't expect or predict?"
4. "If this story had a different ending—if you'd failed—what would that have meant to you?"
5. "What does this story reveal about how you handle setback, confusion, or something you can't control?"

**Goal of Questions:** Shift focus from achievement to insight or internal struggle.

**Success Signal:**
- Student can identify a genuine moment of vulnerability or doubt within the larger achievement
- Revised essay focuses on the internal struggle, not the external accomplishment
- Achievement becomes evidence of growth, not the point

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Name it (gently): "This essay is really about how accomplished you are, and that's already clear from your résumé. Let's zoom in on something else."
- Reframe: "Admissions wants to know who you are when things are hard or you don't know the answer. What's that like for you?"
- Redirect: "Tell me the part of this experience where you felt least confident. Let's start there."
- Assign: "Rewrite the essay from the moment of real uncertainty. Not the setup and payoff, just the moment where you didn't know what would happen."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Acknowledge the achievement without centering it
2. Find the genuine vulnerability or struggle within the story
3. Rebuild essay around the moment of uncertainty, not the moment of triumph
4. Focus on internal processing: what did you realize, question, or learn about yourself?
5. Ensure the ending is not "and I succeeded" but "and I learned" or "and I understood"

### The "Lesson Learned" Essay

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay tells a story only as a setup for a moral lesson
- The "lesson" is predictable (adversity strengthens you, teamwork is important, failure teaches humility)
- Story and reflection are disconnected; the lesson could apply to 10,000 essays
- Typical structure: story for 70%, generic moral for 30%
- Ending paragraph is entirely explanation of the lesson, not integrated with narrative
- Student: "So the moral of the story is..."
- Feels like a Aesop's fable, not a college essay

**Example Content Patterns:**
- "I joined the soccer team and realized the importance of teamwork."
- "When my grandmother died, I learned to cherish the people I love."
- "I failed my first test and learned that failure is important."
- "My volunteer work taught me that serving others is meaningful."

**Red Flags:**
- Last paragraph starts with "I learned," "This experience taught me," "The lesson was"
- Moral is stated, not shown
- Same lesson could be attached to any story
- No genuine surprise or complication
- Essay reads like middle-school creative writing

**Root Cause:**
- Fundamental misunderstanding of what essays do
- Student was told: "Essays should have a lesson or takeaway"
- School writing instruction emphasizing thesis and conclusion
- Student doesn't yet understand the difference between story and essay
- Counselor feedback: "What did you learn from this?"

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (usually a coaching shift, not a story change; fixable with clarity on what essays actually do)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Forget the lesson for a moment. Just tell me what happened and how it felt."
2. "What changed about how you thought or felt after this? Not what lesson you learned—what changed in you?"
3. "If I removed the last paragraph, would the story still stand on its own?"
4. "What surprised you about this experience? What didn't go the way you expected?"
5. "What does this story say about you that you don't have to explicitly state?"

**Goal of Questions:** Help student understand that essay meaning should be *implicit*, not stated.

**Success Signal:**
- Student can remove the lesson paragraph and still convey meaning through the story itself
- Rewritten essay shows growth/change through action and reflection, not declaration
- Student understands: "The lesson is there, but I don't state it directly"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "Your story is solid, but you're spelling out the lesson like the reader needs help. Trust the reader."
- Explain: "The best essays show change, not state it. Your job is to narrate the moment, not explain its meaning."
- Demonstrate: Show how the same story can convey meaning without a concluding moral
- Assign: "Delete the last paragraph. Rewrite the ending to show—not tell—what you understand now."
- Rule: "If you use the word 'learned,' cut it. Show the learning through how you think or act differently."

**How to Redirect:**
1. Cut the explicit moral statement
2. Look for the implicit lesson within the narrative
3. Rebuild the ending to show understanding through reflection or reframing, not declaration
4. Test: "If I didn't read the moral, would I still understand what changed for you?"
5. Final revision: "This lesson is there, but show it. Don't say it."

## TIER 3: EXECUTION-LEVEL DIAGNOSIS

If Tier 1 passes (the story is solid and voice is present) but the essay still doesn't shine, the problem is in the execution—how the writer has crafted, structured, and developed the essay. These are the most fixable problems because the foundation is sound.

### Weak Opening (Generic, Cliché, or Buried Lead)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- First sentence is generic, abstract, or could apply to thousands of essays
- Essay "warms up" for 2-3 paragraphs before getting to the real story
- Opening lacks sensory detail, specificity, or human interest
- Typical openings: rhetorical question, dictionary definition, broad statement about the world
- Reader doesn't immediately want to keep reading

**Example Weak Openings:**
- "Life is full of challenges."
- "Throughout my life, I have always..."
- "Ever since I was young, I wanted to..."
- "Have you ever wondered what it feels like to..."
- "In the 21st century, technology is..."
- "The definition of leadership is..."

**Root Cause:**
- Student doesn't understand that opening matters; focus is on getting the "real story" later
- Fear of starting too small or specific
- Writing instruction emphasizing thesis first
- Trying to "hook" with a question or big idea instead of a specific moment

**Severity:** 2/5 (purely a revision issue; story and voice are fine)

**Success Signal:**
- Reader is immediately interested
- Opening establishes a specific time, place, person, or moment
- Reader wants to keep reading based on the first sentence alone

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "What's the most vivid moment in your essay? Why isn't it in the first paragraph?"
2. "If you could start with the most specific detail or moment, where would you go?"
3. "What's happening in your body or surroundings at the moment you want to write about?"
4. "What would make a stranger lean in and listen? What's interesting about your story?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "Your story's great, but it doesn't start until paragraph 2. Let's move the good stuff up."
- Technique: "Start with the smallest, most specific moment. Not the big idea—the thing happening right now."
- Demonstrate: Show how a generic opening becomes specific: "The classroom was quiet" → "Mrs. Chen's marker squeaked against the board."
- Assign: "Rewrite the opening as a sentence that could only describe THIS moment, not any moment."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Cut everything before the moment
2. Start with specific sensory detail or dialogue
3. Establish time/place/person in first three sentences
4. Make the first sentence do one job: pull the reader in with specificity

---

### Missing Scene-Setting (Tells Instead of Shows)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Reader understands what happened, but doesn't experience it
- Story is told in summary, not through scenes
- Writing relies on explanation: "I realized," "I felt," "This showed me"
- Lacks: dialogue, physical action, sensory detail, sense of place
- Common phrasing: "I was nervous. I walked up. I performed. It went well."
- Reader has intellectual understanding but not emotional experience

**Example Tell vs. Show:**
- TELL: "I was incredibly nervous as I stood up to give my presentation."
- SHOW: "My hands shook as I gripped the podium. The fluorescent lights felt suddenly hot. I could hear my own breathing, loud in my ears."

**Root Cause:**
- Student defaulted to summary instead of scene
- Unsure how to translate internal experience into external detail
- Word count anxiety: trying to cover too much ground
- Writing instruction that emphasized summary over scene

**Severity:** 3/5 (story is there, but execution is thin; requires rewriting)

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Close your eyes and walk me through that moment like you're watching a movie. What do you see, hear, feel?"
2. "What were you aware of physically at that moment?"
3. "What did you say? What did someone else say? Can you remember dialogue?"
4. "Where were you? What did the place look like, smell like, sound like?"
5. "What was the first thing that told you what was happening?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Name it: "I understand what happened, but I'm not experiencing it. Let me see it."
- Technique: "Pick the most important moment. Spend a paragraph just on that moment—slow it down."
- Assign: "Rewrite this section as a scene. Include at least one line of dialogue, one sensory detail, and one physical action."
- Rule: "Never tell me you felt something. Show me what that feeling looked like in your body or in the world."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Identify the key moments (turning points, realizations, changes)
2. Zoom in on each moment; make it a scene, not a summary
3. Add sensory detail: what you heard, saw, felt, smelled, tasted
4. Include dialogue: what was actually said
5. Show action: what you and others were doing
6. Move reflection outside the scene: "That moment taught me..." comes after showing the moment

---

### Underdeveloped Reflection (Story Without Meaning)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay has a clear story with good scene-setting
- But reflection is minimal or missing
- Reader knows what happened, not why it matters
- Typical gap: great narrative for 80%, then a paragraph of thin reflection
- Reader is left thinking, "So what? Why are you telling me this?"
- Distinction from "Missing Reflection": here, reflection *exists* but is superficial

**Example Underdeveloped Reflection:**
- Story: detailed account of a failure in a science project
- Reflection: "I learned that failure is important. It helped me grow."
- Missing: specific insight about what you understand now, how you think differently, what this revealed about you

**Root Cause:**
- Student ran out of ideas when it came time to reflect
- Reflection is hard; easier to stick with narrative
- Assumption that story speaks for itself
- Limited practice with genuine reflection vs. platitude

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (the hard part is done; now deepening is needed)

**Success Signal:**
- Reflection section is as substantial as the narrative
- Reader understands a specific, non-obvious insight about the writer
- Reflection is complicated: shows growth but also uncertainty or ongoing learning

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Why does this story matter enough to tell to admissions? What about it changed something in you?"
2. "How do you think about [the central issue] differently now than before this story?"
3. "What question did this experience raise for you? Are you still thinking about it?"
4. "What do you understand about yourself that you didn't understand before?"
5. "If this happened again, how would you approach it differently? Why?"
6. "Is there anything this experience made you question or reconsider?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "Great story. Now help me understand what it means. Don't tell me a life lesson—tell me what you were thinking."
- Technique: "Reflection isn't about sounding wise. It's about being honest about what you understand now."
- Assign: "Answer each of these questions about your story in a paragraph. Pick the one that feels most true."
- Expand: "Take your one-sentence reflection and turn it into a full paragraph. What's beneath it?"

**Revision Formula:**
1. Identify what shifts in the essay (realization, change, new understanding)
2. Spend a full paragraph on how you thought *before* vs. *now*
3. Be specific about the shift, not generic
4. Avoid "This taught me that..." format; instead: "I used to think X, but now I understand Y"
5. Show the reflection as ongoing, not concluded
6. Connect the personal insight to something about your character, values, or outlook

---

### Overdeveloped Reflection (Too Much Telling, Not Enough Story)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay is mostly analysis and commentary; story is sparse
- Multiple paragraphs of abstract reflection
- Small story details get swallowed by big ideas
- Reader hears the student thinking, not experiencing
- Typical balance: 30% story, 70% reflection
- Reads like a philosophy essay, not a personal narrative

**Example Overdeveloped Reflection:**
- Story: "I volunteered at a shelter on Tuesday"
- Reflection: Three paragraphs about poverty, systemic inequality, human dignity, personal privilege, responsibility, etc.

**Root Cause:**
- Student is more comfortable with ideas than narrative
- Over-identification with intellectual identity ("I'm a thinker")
- Belief that reflection means philosophizing
- Over-correction from underdeveloped reflection

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (story foundation is there; needs rebalancing)

**Success Signal:**
- Essay is 60% story, 40% reflection (rough split)
- Reflection is grounded in the specific story, not abstract
- Reader experiences the moment before understanding its significance

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Can you tell this story in two paragraphs without any explanation? Just what happened?"
2. "What's the smallest moment that contains the biggest shift?"
3. "Pick one sentence of reflection that's essential. Cut everything else. What remains?"
4. "What would happen if you removed this reflection paragraph? Does the story still stand?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "You're thinking very hard here, and that's good. But let the story breathe first. Don't explain everything."
- Technique: "Reflection should answer 'Why?' about the story, not answer 'What is?' about the world."
- Assign: "Cut your reflection in half. Keep only the sentences that are directly about THIS story, not about the topic in general."
- Rule: "Every sentence of reflection should connect back to a specific moment in your story."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Restore balance: more story scene, less analysis
2. Cut abstract reflection that could apply to any story on this topic
3. Keep only reflection that's specific to this student's realization
4. Test: "Can someone else's story have the same reflection paragraph? If yes, cut it."
5. Integrate: weave reflection through the narrative, not as a final section
6. Shorten: reflection should be shorter than story, not equal or longer

---

### Voice Collapse (Starts Authentic, Becomes Formal/Stiff Midway)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- First paragraph sounds like a real person
- By paragraph 3, the voice becomes more formal, distant, or "essayish"
- Voice inconsistency: short sentences and contractions early, then complex sentences and formal register
- Reader can point to "the moment it changed"
- Usually happens when student moves from narrative to reflection
- Typical trigger: shifting from "what happened" to "what I learned"

**Example Voice Collapse:**
- Paragraph 1: "I completely bombed the interview. Like, walked out thinking, 'That was a disaster.'"
- Paragraph 3: "The experience engendered a comprehensive reassessment of my approach to interpersonal dynamics."

**Root Cause:**
- Student was told essays need to sound "mature" or "polished"
- Belief that reflection requires formal register
- Shifting audience (now writing for admissions, not just telling a story)
- Over-correction after being told voice was "too casual"

**Severity:** 2/5 (easy to fix; don't rewrite, just make voice consistent)

**Success Signal:**
- Same voice throughout (consistent register, rhythm, vocabulary)
- Reflection sounds like the student thinking, not like a formal essay

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "How would you tell this story to a friend? How is that different from what's written?"
2. "Read the first paragraph aloud. Now read paragraph 3. Hear the difference?"
3. "Which version sounds more like you?"
4. "What words in the later paragraphs would you never actually use?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Name it: "I notice your voice changes partway through. Let me show you."
- Permission: "Formal doesn't mean better. Authentic matters more."
- Assign: "Go through and mark every sentence that doesn't sound like you. Rewrite those in your actual voice."
- Rule: "If you wouldn't say it in conversation, don't write it in this essay."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Read aloud to identify where voice shifts
2. Rewrite later paragraphs in the same voice as the opening
3. Replace formal vocabulary with natural equivalents
4. Vary sentence length and complexity like in opening
5. Add contractions, casual markers, personal idiom throughout
6. Test: read entire essay aloud. Does it sound like one person?

---

### Structural Confusion (Reader Gets Lost)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay has good content, but reader can't follow the logic or timeline
- Paragraphs jump between ideas without clear transitions
- Flashbacks or non-chronological structure are disorienting, not illuminating
- Reader doesn't know when things happened or in what order
- Typical problem: narrative bounces between different time periods without clear markers
- Transitions are missing or weak

**Example Structural Confusion:**
- Paragraph 1: Present day, competition
- Paragraph 2: Flashback to childhood without clear signal
- Paragraph 3: Back to present, but reader isn't sure when
- Paragraph 4: Another flashback, confused timeline
- Reader is lost

**Root Cause:**
- Student didn't plan structure before writing
- Tried to use flashbacks without clear framing
- Assuming the reader will understand the time jumps they understand
- Multiple story threads not clearly separated

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (fixable with reorganization)

**Success Signal:**
- Reader can follow the narrative sequence clearly
- Transitions signal time shifts explicitly
- Structure serves the meaning, not confuses it

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Walk me through the timeline. When does the story start, jump to, and return to?"
2. "If you drew a timeline of this essay, what does it look like?"
3. "Does the non-chronological order serve the meaning, or just confuse the reader?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "I'm losing the thread of when things happened. Let me suggest a clearer order."
- Option A: "Let's try telling this chronologically first. Does that work better?"
- Option B: "If you're using flashbacks intentionally, let's mark the time shifts more clearly."
- Assign: "Create a timeline: what happens first, second, third? Reorder your paragraphs to match."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Map the timeline: When does the essay start? What's the sequence of events?
2. Decide: chronological order, or deliberate non-chronological structure?
3. If chronological: reorganize paragraphs to follow the timeline
4. If non-chronological: add time signals ("Years earlier," "When I was nine," "Looking back")
5. Ensure transitions between times are explicit
6. Test: reader should never be confused about when something is happening

---

### Weak Ending (Trails Off, Cliché Conclusion, Forced Lesson)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay ends abruptly or with a summarizing sentence that adds no new thought
- Final paragraph is a cliché: "This experience changed my life" or "I'm grateful for what I learned"
- Ending is tacked-on, not emerging from the essay itself
- Final thought is generic and could be attached to any essay
- Reader feels the essay just... stops
- Typical weak endings: "In conclusion," life lessons, circular returns to opening

**Example Weak Endings:**
- "And that's why volunteering is important."
- "This experience taught me to never give up."
- "I am grateful for this opportunity."
- "And thus my perspective has evolved."
- [Essay just ends without a final thought]

**Root Cause:**
- Student didn't plan an ending; just stopped when word count was reached
- Instinct to summarize or conclude (from school writing)
- Fear of ambiguity; wanting to spell out meaning
- Belief that endings need to have a "point"

**Severity:** 2/5 (usually not a problem with fundamentals, just the landing)

**Success Signal:**
- Final sentence lands with meaning or resonance
- Reader finishes and feels the essay is complete
- Ending is surprising or specific, not generic
- Ending emerges from the essay itself, not imposed from outside

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "What's the one thing you want the reader to understand about you after this essay?"
2. "How do you see yourself or the world differently as a result of this story?"
3. "Is there a final image or moment that captures what you want to leave them with?"
4. "What's a specific detail that represents what this story means to you?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "Your essay just stops. Let's think about where it should land."
- Technique: "Endings don't need to be conclusive. They can be opening onto something new."
- Assign: "Skip the summary. Write the last moment, image, or thought. Just one specific thing."
- Rule: "The last sentence should surprise the reader or make them see something in a new way."

**Revision Formula:**
1. Delete any concluding paragraph that just restates the lesson
2. Ask: "What's one specific, final thought or image that represents this story?"
3. End with that thought or image
4. Make sure the final sentence is memorable, specific, and new (not repetition)
5. Optional: end with a question or forward-looking thought
6. Test: does the ending feel like it belongs to THIS essay, not essays in general?

**Strong Ending Patterns:**
- A specific image or moment from the story
- A question the essay has raised
- A forward-looking thought about what comes next
- A quiet acknowledgment of what changed
- A surprising perspective turn

---

### Word Count Problems (Too Long/Short, Padding, Compression Needed)

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay significantly exceeds the word limit (over 650 when limit is 650)
- Essay is well under the minimum, padded with unnecessary filler
- Compression is visible: important moments condensed to one sentence
- Padding is visible: unnecessary words, repeated ideas, transitions that go nowhere
- For over-length: every paragraph feels essential, hard to cut
- For under-length: story feels rushed or ideas underdeveloped

**Root Cause:**
- Over-length: student was focused on story and didn't monitor length; now everything seems essential
- Under-length: student is done with story but has word room left and resorts to filler
- Word count anxiety leading to either bloat or compression
- Unclear what the word limit actually is

**Severity:** 1-2/5 (fixable if the content is strong; mostly editing)

**Success Signal:**
- Essay is within word limit
- No padding or filler
- Every sentence earns its place
- Important moments get the space they deserve

**Intervention for Over-Length:**

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "If you had to cut 50 words, what would go?"
2. "Are there any paragraphs that don't move the story forward?"
3. "Is any moment told twice, just differently?"
4. "What's the absolute core story? What's supporting material?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "You've got 700 words in a 650-word limit. Let's trim without losing essence."
- Technique: "Cut descriptions that repeat the mood. Cut transitions that just connect sentences. Cut explanations that the reader already understands."
- Assign: "Mark every sentence that doesn't move the story forward. That's where we cut."
- Rule: "Every sentence should do one of three things: advance the story, reveal character, or add a sensory detail."

**Cutting Strategy:**
1. Identify padding: unnecessary descriptions, repeated ideas, weak transitions
2. Compress summary passages: "I worked there all summer" can be shorter
3. Cut full sentences that can become phrases
4. Remove qualifiers and weak modifiers
5. Cut any "nice to have" reflection; keep only essential
6. Ensure cuts don't remove voice or specificity

**Intervention for Under-Length:**

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Which moment in the story deserves more space and attention?"
2. "Where are you summarizing that you could show instead?"
3. "Is there anything about the setting or people you haven't described?"
4. "What's one moment that's told in one sentence that could be a paragraph?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Diagnose: "You've got room to develop. Let's make the story deeper, not just longer."
- Technique: "Pick the most important moment. Slow down. Spend a full paragraph on it."
- Assign: "Where are you telling when you could be showing? Expand those sections."
- Rule: "Add length by developing, not by adding new ideas. Go deeper into what's already there."

**Expansion Strategy:**
1. Identify the core moment of the story
2. Slow down that moment: add sensory detail, dialogue, physical action
3. Expand reflection to match story length
4. Add specific details where you've used generalities
5. Develop character moments: what people said or did, not just what they meant

## TIER 4: SCHOOL-FIT DIAGNOSIS

Essays can be well-written, compelling, and still be wrong for a specific institution. This tier addresses mismatches between essay quality and school fit.

### Good Essay, Wrong Tone for the School

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay is strong, but the voice/tone feels mismatched to the school's culture
- Example: hyper-casual essay sent to a formal school, or overly academic essay sent to a quirky school
- School reads the essay and thinks, "This doesn't sound like our students"
- Story and execution are good; the cultural calibration is off

**Red Flags by School Type:**

**For Prestigious/Formal Schools (Princeton, Yale, Georgetown):**
- Essay is too casual, colloquial, or self-deprecating
- Tone is joking when seriousness is expected
- No evidence of intellectual engagement
- Voice sounds like "a student," not a thoughtful person

**For Progressive/Quirky Schools (Reed, Wesleyan, Macalester):**
- Essay is overly formal or conventional
- No humor, personality, or playfulness
- Tone is earnest without irony
- Sounds like what admissions wants, not what student thinks

**For Rigorous/Science-Focused Schools (MIT, Caltech, CMU):**
- Essay is humanities-focused without technical depth
- Tone is emotional rather than analytical
- No problem-solving or intellectual rigor shown
- Reads like student doesn't belong in a technical environment

**Root Cause:**
- Student sent same essay to multiple schools without calibration
- Misunderstood the school's culture
- Wrote the essay before researching the school deeply
- Over-corrected based on stereotype of the school

**Severity:** 2-3/5 (essay is good, but needs recalibration for this school)

**Success Signal:**
- Tone matches school culture
- Specific voice stays but calibrated to fit
- Admissions thinks, "This student would be happy here"

**Intervention:**
- Analyze the school's admitted student essays (if available)
- Discuss the school's culture and values
- Identify tone shifts needed
- Revise without losing authentic voice
- Test: "If an AO at this school read this, would they think 'this is one of us'?"

---

### "Why Us" Essay That Could Be About Any School

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Essay demonstrates knowledge of the school, but in generic ways
- Could swap school names and essay still works (mostly)
- Typical content: "Your strong academics," "Beautiful campus," "Great programs," "Location"
- Depth is surface-level; evidence of superficial research
- Reader can't tell if student has genuinely engaged with the school

**Red Flags:**
- Mentions programs or features that don't actually exist at this school
- Lists school characteristics without personal connection
- No professors, specific programs, or campus details mentioned
- Reads like the student visited the website and read admissions materials

**Root Cause:**
- Student didn't do deep research
- Assumed generic praise would be enough
- Wrote essay too close to application deadline
- Belief that visiting campus or research would take too much time

**Severity:** 3/5 (will hurt admissions chances; requires genuine research and rewrite)

**Success Signal:**
- Essay could not be about any other school
- Specific programs, professors, traditions, or opportunities mentioned
- Personal connection to school is clear and specific
- Admissions believes student has genuinely researched the school

**Intervention Questions for Coach:**
1. "Have you visited campus or spoken to current students? What specifically interested you?"
2. "Are there professors doing work that connects to your interests?"
3. "What's a tradition, program, or opportunity unique to this school?"
4. "Why THIS school and not three others with similar characteristics?"

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Assign intensive research:
  - Read faculty biographies; identify 2-3 whose work connects to student's interests
  - Research specific programs, opportunities, interdisciplinary opportunities
  - Find and read 2-3 student-written pieces about the school (blogs, forums)
  - Identify school traditions, opportunities, or culture elements unique to this institution
  - Connect student's specific interests to school specifics

**Revision Formula:**
1. Remove any generic praise ("excellent academics," "diverse community")
2. Replace with specific details: professor names, program names, campus traditions
3. Show research: "Professor Chen's lab focuses on X, which connects to my interest in Y because..."
4. Connect personally: "This opportunity matters to me because of [specific student reason]"
5. Make it impossible to swap school names: specific details only about this school
6. Test: could a current student reading this say, "Yes, that's accurate about our school"?

---

### Supplemental That Repeats the Personal Statement

**Diagnostic Signals:**
- Supplemental covers similar topics, stories, or ideas as the personal statement
- Reader learns nothing new about the student
- Supplemental seems to justify or reiterate points from personal statement
- Wasted opportunity: could have shown a different facet of the student

**Red Flags:**
- Same achievement, accomplishment, or story appears in both essays
- Supplemental is framing of personal statement ("How I grew from the experience in my PS...")
- Could be deleted without changing the application

**Root Cause:**
- Student didn't understand the purpose of supplemental (show a different dimension)
- Didn't have other good stories and felt obligated to use the same one
- Lack of planning across all essays

**Severity:** 3/5 (hurts application; shows poor planning)

**Intervention:**
- Map all essays: what dimension of the student does each show?
- Identify if supplemental adds new information
- If not: find a different story for the supplemental
- Ensure each essay shows a different facet (achievement vs. values vs. intellectual interests, etc.)

---

## DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT FORMAT FOR COACHES

When a coach reads an essay and identifies a problem, they should use this format to organize their feedback and intervention:

```
PRIMARY ISSUE: [one sentence describing the core problem]

ROOT CAUSE: [why this happened]

TIER: [which diagnostic tier this falls under]
- For Tier 1 failures: list which questions failed
- For Tier 2: which story-level pattern
- For Tier 3: which execution problem
- For Tier 4: which school-fit issue

SEVERITY: [1-5, where 5 = needs complete rewrite]

COACHING CONVERSATION STARTER:
[One opening question that helps student see the issue themselves]

COACHING QUESTIONS: [3-5 follow-up questions]

INTERVENTION: [specific coaching action]

SUCCESS SIGNAL: [how to know when revision fixed it]

WORD ESTIMATE FOR REVISION: [how much work this will take]
```

---

## QUICK REFERENCE: DECISION TREE SUMMARY

**IF the essay fails Tier 1:**
- Q1 No (no specific moment): → Tier 2: Missing Narrative or Wrong Story
- Q2 No (no distinct voice): → Tier 3: Voice Failure
- Q3 No (no "so what"): → Tier 3: Missing Reflection
- Q4 No (no surprise): → Tier 2: Lack of Distinctiveness

**IF the essay passes Tier 1 but feels weak:**
- Move to Tier 3 (Execution): Opening, Scene-Setting, Reflection balance, Voice consistency, Structure, Ending, Word count
- Then to Tier 4 (School Fit): Tone calibration, "Why Us" depth, Supplemental repetition

**IF the essay is well-written but feels off:**
- Move to Tier 4 (School Fit): Is it the right tone, depth, and focus for THIS school?

---

## COMMON COACHING MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Focusing on grammar instead of substance.** Grammar errors are cosmetic. Story, voice, and reflection are the foundation. Fix those first.

2. **Accepting generic reflection.** "This taught me to work harder" isn't reflection. Push for specificity: "What shifted in how you see [X]?"

3. **Over-editing.** Coach's job is to ask questions and guide; student's job is to write. If the coach rewrites, it's no longer the student's essay.

4. **Assuming the story is fixed.** If Tier 1 fails, don't polish execution. Go back to story choice. No amount of craft fixes a broken foundation.

5. **Not addressing the root cause.** Fixing the symptom (weak opening) without addressing the root (no specific moment) will leave the essay still broken.

6. **Skipping the "So What."** Many coaches let students get away with stories that are interesting but meaningless. Essays need meaning.

7. **Accepting the first answer.** When you ask coaching questions, push back. The first answer is usually surface. The second or third answer is usually truer.

8. **Letting school fit slide.** An excellent essay sent to the wrong school with the wrong tone will underperform. Calibration matters.

---

## CALIBRATION NOTES FOR CONSISTENCY

**Within a coaching team:** Use this decision tree to discuss essays together. When coaches disagree on diagnosis, trace it back through the tiers. Often, the disagreement is actually about root cause (one coach sees a Tier 3 voice issue; another sees a Tier 2 story issue). This tree helps resolve those disagreements.

**With students:** Don't dump the entire tree on them. Use it to guide conversation and identify interventions. The tree is for coaches, not for explaining to students.

**With families:** Never use severity scores directly with families. Instead: "This essay is strong but needs one more revision cycle on reflection" or "This essay is on track; let's refine the opening."

---

## CLOSING NOTE

This diagnostic framework is designed to help coaches move quickly from reading to action. In 5 minutes with this tree, a coach should be able to:

1. Identify exactly what's wrong (Tier 1, 2, 3, or 4)
2. Understand why it happened (root cause)
3. Know what intervention to apply (questions and coaching moves)
4. Understand what success looks like (success signals)
5. Estimate the effort required (severity and rework estimate)

The goal is consistency, speed, and effectiveness. Coaches should be able to say to a student, "Here's what's happening and here's how we fix it"—and students should feel understood and empowered to revise, not defensive or overwhelmed.
