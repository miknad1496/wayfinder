# Financial Aid: EFC and Asset Rules for High-Wealth Families

## Common Questions This Covers

- "We inherited money — how does this change our college strategy?"
- "We just inherited $500K / $1 million / $2 million. What happens to financial aid?"
- "My son/daughter is a sophomore and we just came into money. How does this completely change things?"
- "Does an inheritance affect college financial aid?"
- "We're suddenly wealthy. Do we still qualify for scholarships?"
- "How do assets and savings affect how much college costs?"

## How Assets Affect Expected Family Contribution (EFC)

The Expected Family Contribution is NOT a measure of what a family can "afford." It is a formula-driven calculation that determines how much federal and institutional aid a student qualifies for. Higher assets mean higher EFC, which means LESS financial aid. When a family inherits money or comes into sudden wealth, the EFC goes UP — meaning the family qualifies for LESS aid, not more.

### FAFSA Asset Treatment (Federal Methodology)

The FAFSA uses the Federal Methodology (FM) to calculate the Student Aid Index (SAI), which replaced the EFC starting 2024-2025.

Key asset rules under FAFSA:
- **Parent assets are assessed at up to 5.64% per year.** A family with $500K in reportable assets adds ~$28,200 to their EFC annually.
- **Student assets are assessed at 20%.** A student with $50K in their name adds $10,000 to EFC.
- **Primary home equity is NOT reported on FAFSA.** This is a major planning variable.
- **Retirement accounts (401k, IRA, Roth) are NOT reported on FAFSA.** Only non-retirement investment accounts count.
- **529 plans owned by parents are reported as parent assets** (5.64% assessment rate).
- **529 plans owned by grandparents are NOT reported on FAFSA** as of 2024-2025 simplification.
- **Asset Protection Allowance has been effectively eliminated** in recent FAFSA changes. Previously shielded ~$10K-50K depending on parent age.

### CSS Profile Asset Treatment (Institutional Methodology)

The CSS Profile, used by ~250 selective private schools, is MORE aggressive than FAFSA:
- **Home equity IS counted.** A family with $800K in home equity will see a significant EFC increase. Some schools cap home equity at 1.2x-2.0x income; others count it fully.
- **Business/farm equity IS counted** (FAFSA excludes small businesses with <100 employees).
- **Non-custodial parent income and assets ARE counted.** Divorced families cannot hide assets by putting them in the non-custodial parent's name.
- **Trust funds, real estate investments, and other non-retirement holdings are all counted.**

### Large Inheritance or Windfall Impact

When a family receives a large inheritance (e.g., $500K, $1M, $2M+):

**The EFC goes UP, not down.** This is the most common misconception. A $2M inheritance does NOT mean EFC is $0. It means EFC is likely HIGHER than the full cost of attendance at most schools.

Specific impact of a $2M cash inheritance:
- **FAFSA:** $2M × 5.64% = ~$112,800 added to EFC per year. This alone exceeds the cost of attendance at most public universities. The family will qualify for ZERO need-based federal aid (no Pell Grant, no SEOG, no subsidized loans).
- **CSS Profile:** Even more aggressive. $2M in liquid assets + any existing home equity + income = EFC potentially exceeding $150,000-200,000/year. No institutional need-based aid at any CSS Profile school.
- **Merit aid is unaffected.** Academic scholarships based on GPA/test scores do not consider family assets. A high-achieving student with a $2M family inheritance can still receive merit scholarships.
- **The strategic question changes from "how do we pay?" to "where do we get the best value?"** The family is now a full-pay family. They should evaluate schools based on outcomes (earnings ROI, career placement, academic fit) rather than net price.

### Strategic Planning for High-Asset Families

For families with $500K+ in liquid assets:
1. **Accept full-pay status.** Do not expect need-based aid. Plan accordingly.
2. **Prioritize merit aid.** Target schools where the student's stats are above the 75th percentile — these schools offer the most generous merit packages.
3. **Consider the Efficiency Index.** Full-pay families should compare 10-year earnings outcomes against sticker price. A $85K/year school with $75K median starting salary has a different value proposition than a $25K/year public school with $55K median starting salary.
4. **Asset repositioning has limited utility.** Moving money into retirement accounts or paying down the mortgage before filing FAFSA can reduce reportable assets, but large inheritances are difficult to shelter completely. Consult a financial planner who specializes in college financial aid.
5. **Timing matters.** FAFSA and CSS Profile use "prior-prior year" income. An inheritance received in the student's junior year of high school will affect the freshman year aid calculation. An inheritance received after filing has less immediate impact but must be reported if the school requests updated information.

## The Full-Pay Advantage

Being a full-pay family is not purely disadvantageous:
- **Some highly selective schools give preference to full-pay applicants** in borderline admission decisions. This is not officially acknowledged but is widely understood in admissions consulting.
- **Full-pay families have more leverage in merit aid negotiations** at schools outside the top 20. Schools want revenue certainty.
- **No loan burden.** The long-term financial impact of graduating debt-free is substantial — median student loan debt is ~$30K, with monthly payments of $300-400 for 10 years.

## Common EFC Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "We inherited money, so we don't need aid" = EFC is $0 | EFC is calculated from assets; more assets = higher EFC = less aid |
| "Our EFC is $0 because we have no income" | Assets are assessed independently of income. Large assets with low income still produce high EFC. |
| "$2M means we can afford any school" | Mathematically true, but EFC may exceed COA, meaning zero need-based aid at every school |
| "We should hide assets to get more aid" | Asset sheltering is legal (retirement accounts, home equity on FAFSA) but large liquid inheritances are difficult to hide and misrepresentation is federal fraud |
