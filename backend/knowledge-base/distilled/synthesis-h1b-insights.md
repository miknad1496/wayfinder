# H1B Visa Compensation Intelligence: What Companies Actually Pay

**Source**: H1B LCA Disclosure Data FY2025 Q4
**Created**: March 2026
**Purpose**: Reveal the real salary landscape for visa-sponsored roles and the massive pay gap between top-tier and outsourcing employers

---

## The Two-Tier H1B Market

The H1B data reveals a stark bifurcation in the visa sponsorship market. There are fundamentally two types of H1B employers, and the compensation gap between them is enormous:

**Tier 1 — Direct Employers (FAANG, Finance, Enterprise):** Google, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan. These companies sponsor H1B workers at or above domestic market rates. A software developer at Google on H1B earns a median of $200,000 — higher than the overall H1B software developer median of $139,360.

**Tier 2 — IT Consulting/Outsourcing Firms:** Cognizant, Infosys, TCS, HCL, Wipro, Mphasis. These companies file the most H1B petitions by volume but pay significantly below market. A computer systems engineer at Cognizant earns a median of $115,128 vs. $178,020 at Amazon — a 35% gap for comparable work.

**Strategic Insight:** The company on an H1B offer matters more than the job title. Two "Software Developer" offers can differ by $80-100K depending on the sponsor.

## Strategic Decision Frameworks

### Framework 1: Evaluating an H1B Offer

When comparing H1B-sponsored offers:
1. **Look up the sponsor in the H1B data** — is this a Tier 1 (direct employer) or Tier 2 (outsourcing/consulting) sponsor?
2. **Compare the offered salary to the H1B median for that SOC code** — offers below P25 are underpaying, even for H1B
3. **Check if the sponsor has a pattern of Level I filings** — Level I = entry-level wage; some consulting firms file most positions at Level I regardless of experience
4. **Consider mobility** — Tier 2 sponsors often have non-compete clauses and limited transfer options; Tier 1 sponsors offer more career flexibility

### Framework 2: H1B as Negotiation Leverage

H1B workers often accept below-market pay because they believe their visa ties them to the employer. The data shows this is not inevitable:
- Top-tier companies pay H1B workers at or above domestic rates
- The prevailing wage for your SOC code + metro area is the floor, not the ceiling
- Having an approved H1B petition from any employer gives you portability (H1B transfer)
- Counter offers using the BLS median for your role + metro as your benchmark, not the H1B minimum

### Framework 3: Career Path for H1B Professionals

The highest ROI career move for an H1B professional at a Tier 2 sponsor:
1. Gain 1-2 years of US work experience at the Tier 2 firm
2. Begin interviewing at Tier 1 direct employers (who will sponsor H1B transfer)
3. Expect a 40-80% salary increase by moving from Tier 2 to Tier 1
4. This jump (e.g., $95K at TCS → $170K at Microsoft) is the single highest-ROI career move in the H1B landscape

## H1B Level System Explained

Employers must file H1B petitions at a wage level (I-IV) corresponding to the role's experience requirements:

| Level | Description | Typical Salary Position |
|-------|-------------|------------------------|
| Level I | Entry | 17th percentile of prevailing wage |
| Level II | Qualified | 34th-50th percentile |
| Level III | Experienced | 50th-67th percentile |
| Level IV | Fully Competent | 67th+ percentile |

**Red Flag:** If a company files most of its H1B petitions at Level I, they're either genuinely hiring juniors or they're systematically underclassifying roles to minimize required wages. Check the level distribution when evaluating an offer.

For Software Developers in FY2025: Level I (3,383 filings), Level II (13,955), Level III (7,484), Level IV (6,253). A roughly normal distribution — but individual companies vary wildly.
