# Admissions: Disciplinary Records, Expulsions, and Criminal History

## Overview

Students with disciplinary records — suspensions, expulsions, or criminal convictions — face a more complex admissions landscape, but options exist at every level. The key variables are the severity of the infraction, the age at which it occurred, whether the record has been sealed or expunged, and how the student frames the experience in their application.

## The Common App Disciplinary Question

Starting with the 2023-2024 cycle, the Common Application asks:

> "Have you ever been found responsible for a disciplinary violation at any educational institution you have attended from the 9th grade (or the international equivalent) forward, whether related to academic misconduct or behavioral misconduct, that resulted in a disciplinary action?"

This includes: suspensions (in-school and out-of-school), expulsions, academic dishonesty findings, and required withdrawals.

### What Schools See

- **Transcript notation:** Most high schools note suspensions and expulsions on the transcript. Some states require it; others leave it to school policy.
- **Counselor report:** The school counselor's Common App form includes a section about disciplinary history. Counselors are expected to be truthful.
- **Self-disclosure:** The student must answer honestly. Lying about a disciplinary record that the school discovers through transcripts or counselor reports is grounds for rescinding admission or expulsion from the college.

## Expulsion: What Colleges Will Still Accept You

Expulsion is the most severe disciplinary action — permanent removal from the school. It is always noted on the transcript and always disclosed by the counselor.

### Schools That Regularly Admit Students with Expulsions

1. **Community colleges:** Open-admission institutions do not screen for disciplinary history. A student expelled from high school can enroll in community college, build a strong academic record, and transfer to a 4-year school. This is the most common and most effective pathway.

2. **Open-admission 4-year public universities:** Many state universities with high acceptance rates (70%+) admit students with disciplinary records as long as academic requirements are met. Examples vary by state — check each school's admissions policy.

3. **Schools with holistic review that explicitly consider context:** Some selective schools will consider the circumstances, the student's age, time elapsed, and evidence of growth. These schools look for:
   - Was the student a minor when the incident occurred?
   - How much time has passed since the expulsion?
   - What has the student done since (academic record, community involvement, personal growth)?
   - Is there a compelling narrative of learning and change?

4. **Alternative and second-chance programs:** Some institutions specifically serve students with disrupted education histories. These include therapeutic boarding schools with college prep tracks and bridge programs designed for re-entry.

### The Transfer Strategy (Most Common Path After Expulsion)

1. Enroll in community college immediately or after a gap period.
2. Complete 30-60 credits with a strong GPA (3.5+ recommended).
3. Apply to transfer to a 4-year institution. Transfer applications at most schools do NOT ask about high school disciplinary history — they evaluate the college transcript.
4. The high school expulsion effectively becomes irrelevant once a strong college record is established.

This strategy works because transfer admissions evaluates the applicant as a college student, not a high school student. The disciplinary incident is distanced by time and academic performance.

## Criminal Records and College Admissions

### Juvenile vs. Adult Records

- **Juvenile records (offenses committed before age 18):** In most states, juvenile records are sealed or confidential by default. Many states automatically seal juvenile records when the person turns 18 or after a waiting period. Sealed records do NOT need to be disclosed on college applications in most circumstances.
- **Adult records (offenses committed at 18+):** These are public record and generally must be disclosed when asked.
- **The critical variable is state law.** Some states (California, New York, Illinois) have strong juvenile record protections. Others allow juvenile felonies to be treated as adult records depending on the severity of the offense and whether the case was transferred to adult court.

### The Age-17 Felony Question

A 17-year-old convicted of a felony may be processed through either the juvenile or adult system depending on:
- **State law:** In some states, 17-year-olds are automatically treated as adults for all criminal matters. Other states set the adult threshold at 18.
- **Severity of offense:** Many states allow "waiver" or "transfer" of serious felonies (assault, robbery, drug trafficking) from juvenile to adult court.
- **Prosecution decision:** In some jurisdictions, the prosecutor decides whether to charge as juvenile or adult.

If the case was processed through the juvenile system and the record has been sealed, the student generally does NOT need to disclose it on college applications. If the case was processed as an adult felony, it is a public record and must be disclosed when asked.

### How Colleges Evaluate Criminal Records

Most selective colleges that ask about criminal history use the Common App question or a similar form. The evaluation process typically includes:

1. **Context review:** The admissions committee reads the student's explanation of the incident alongside the rest of the application.
2. **Time elapsed:** An incident at age 14 is viewed very differently from one at age 17 or 18.
3. **Severity assessment:** Non-violent offenses (drug possession, theft) are treated more leniently than violent offenses.
4. **Evidence of rehabilitation:** Community service, clean record since the incident, letters from mentors or counselors, completion of diversion programs.
5. **Campus safety consideration:** Some schools conduct a separate safety review for applicants with felony convictions, particularly for violent offenses.

### The "Ban the Box" Movement in College Admissions

Several states and the Common App itself have evolved on this issue:
- **The Common App removed the criminal history question in 2018** but still asks about disciplinary violations.
- **Some states (California, Colorado, Louisiana, Maryland, Washington) prohibit public colleges from asking about criminal history** on initial applications.
- **Individual schools vary widely.** Some ask about both disciplinary and criminal history; others ask only about disciplinary history; some ask about neither.

### Schools That Do NOT Ask About Criminal History

Many schools, particularly in states with "ban the box" laws, do not ask about criminal history at all during the admissions process. At these schools, a felony conviction has zero impact on the admissions decision because the school never learns about it.

## Strategic Guidance for Families

### If Your Child Was Expelled

1. **Do not panic.** Expulsion closes one door but does not close all doors.
2. **Secure the transcript.** Understand exactly what notation appears on the transcript and what the counselor will report.
3. **Consider the community college pathway.** This is the single most effective route to a 4-year degree after expulsion. It gives the student time to mature, build a new academic record, and apply as a transfer student.
4. **Consult an education attorney** if there are questions about whether the expulsion can be modified, appealed, or removed from the record.
5. **Prepare the narrative.** When the student does apply to 4-year schools (either as a freshman or transfer), the explanation essay must demonstrate genuine accountability, specific lessons learned, and concrete evidence of growth. Do not make excuses. Do not blame others. Show what changed.

### If Your Child Has a Criminal Record

1. **Determine if the record is sealed.** If the offense was committed as a juvenile and the record is sealed, it likely does not need to be disclosed. Consult a juvenile defense attorney to confirm.
2. **If disclosure is required, honesty is non-negotiable.** Misrepresenting criminal history on a college application is grounds for rescission if discovered.
3. **The explanation matters more than the conviction.** Admissions officers are trained to evaluate context. A student who committed an offense at 15, completed a diversion program, maintained a clean record for 3 years, and can articulate what they learned will be evaluated very differently from a student who minimizes or deflects.
4. **Target schools thoughtfully.** Research each school's policy on criminal history. Apply to schools that do not ask, and to schools with holistic review processes that consider context.

## Key Data Points

- Over 60% of 4-year institutions in the US ask about criminal history on their applications (as of 2023).
- Students with criminal records who complete community college transfer programs have graduation rates comparable to the general transfer population.
- The recidivism rate for individuals who complete a college degree is approximately 5%, compared to 40%+ for those without higher education.
- Federal financial aid eligibility is generally NOT affected by criminal history, with the exception of drug-related convictions that occurred while the student was receiving federal aid.
