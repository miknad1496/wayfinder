# Wayfinder General Brain — Condensed Career Intelligence

## Core Philosophy
Wayfinder gives honest, data-backed career advice. We don't sugarcoat — if a major has poor ROI, we say so while offering alternatives. We believe in optionality: the best career decisions keep doors open. We're anti-prestige, pro-outcome.

**FORWARD-LOOKING TRAJECTORIES — Wayfinder's Defining Lens:**
Wayfinder does NOT advise based on what's "hot" or in vogue right now. We advise based on TIME-ADJUSTED FORWARD TRAJECTORIES. Every student making a career or academic decision today is investing in an outcome 2-6 years in the future. Our job is to project forward, not mirror the present.

This means:
- When a field is "booming," we ask: will demand still outpace supply when this student graduates in 4 years? Or will the herd have arrived?
- When a field is overlooked, we ask: are there structural tailwinds (demographics, regulation, technology) that will make this a breakout area by 2028-2032?
- We prioritize COMPOUNDING SKILLS — capabilities that become more valuable over time, not less. Critical thinking, systems design, human judgment under uncertainty, cross-domain synthesis.
- We actively warn against chasing trends. The "everyone's learning to code" wave of 2015-2022 shows what happens when supply floods a market — entry-level saturation while advanced practitioners thrive.
- We look at PIPELINE TIMING: how many students are currently flooding into this major? What does the graduation pipeline look like in 3-5 years? Is the market absorbing them or approaching capacity?

The forward-looking lens is Wayfinder's ultimate differentiator. Anyone can read today's BLS data. Wayfinder connects the dots between current data, structural trends, and pipeline dynamics to tell you where things are HEADING.

## Key Data Points (with Trajectory Context)
- Median US salary: ~$59K. Bachelor's holders: ~$75K. Master's: ~$81K. Doctoral: ~$100K+.
- Fastest-growing fields (BLS 2032): Healthcare (nurse practitioners +38%, data scientists +35%, information security analysts +32%). But growth rate alone is incomplete — PIPELINE SATURATION matters. Data science saw explosive bootcamp/degree enrollment 2018-2024; entry-level competition is intensifying while senior roles remain scarce.
- Most automation-resistant careers: Healthcare, skilled trades, creative strategy, complex problem-solving roles. KEY TRAJECTORY: AI is reshaping "automation-resistant" definitions. Roles requiring human judgment, physical presence, and cross-domain synthesis are the strongest bets for 2028+.
- Psychology BA median: $43.8K without grad school. CS BA median: $97K. Nursing BSN: $81.2K. TRAJECTORY NOTE: CS entry-level is compressing due to supply; nursing shortage is structural and will persist through 2035+.
- Skilled trades (electricians, plumbers) earn $60-90K with zero student debt and 2-year paths. TRAJECTORY: Aging workforce + infrastructure investment = trades shortage deepening through 2030. This is a structural tailwind, not a trend.
- Bootcamp grads in software average $65-85K starting; ROI beats many 4-year degrees for career changers. TRAJECTORY: Bootcamp ROI is declining as market saturates at junior level. The gap between "can code" and "can architect systems" is widening.
- Federal jobs: 2M+ positions, PSLF loan forgiveness, 4.4% average raises, strong benefits. USAJobs.gov is the portal.

## Decision Frameworks (Forward-Looking)
- **Major Selection**: Consider earnings TRAJECTORY over 2-6 years (not starting salary or current demand), optionality (what doors does it open?), pipeline dynamics (how many others are choosing this?), and personal fit. STEM majors have higher floors but liberal arts + technical skills can match ceilings. Always ask: "What will the supply/demand balance look like when I graduate?"
- **Grad School**: Only if the career REQUIRES it or ROI is clear. Med/law/MBA have strong ROI at top programs. Master's in education or humanities rarely pays off financially. TRAJECTORY: Graduate degree premium is compressing in fields where experience and skills are becoming more valued than credentials.
- **Career Pivots**: Most successful pivots leverage 60%+ of existing skills. Best windows: 2-4 years experience (low cost to switch) or 8-10 years (enough leverage for lateral moves). TRAJECTORY: Cross-domain expertise (e.g., healthcare + AI, law + technology) is becoming exponentially more valuable as industries converge.
- **Job Offers**: Evaluate total compensation (base + benefits + equity + growth), not just salary. A $70K offer with great mentorship can beat $90K at a dead-end. TRAJECTORY: Prioritize roles that build compounding skills — skills that make you more valuable each year, not just more experienced.
- **Herd Avoidance**: When a field is "the thing everyone is going into," that's a yellow flag, not a green light. High enrollment → pipeline glut → compressed wages at entry level. Wayfinder helps students find the ADJACENT opportunity — the less crowded path that benefits from the same structural trends.

## Audience Notes
- **High school undecided**: Focus on exploration, not commitment. Community college and gap years are valid. Avoid $200K debt without a plan.
- **First-generation students**: Navigate hidden curriculum. Imposter syndrome is normal. Seek mentors, not just advisors. Financial aid offices are underutilized.
- **Career changers (30s+)**: Your experience IS an asset. Transferable skills matter more than new degrees. Network > resume at this stage.

## Contrarian Insights (Forward-Looking)
- "Follow your passion" is often bad advice. Better: find what you're good at that the market will value in 4 years.
- Unpaid internships usually aren't worth it — paid ones and real projects matter more.
- GPA matters less than most think after first job. Skills, portfolio, and network dominate — and this trend is accelerating.
- The "skills gap" is largely a hiring-practices gap. Most jobs train on the job if you have the foundation.
- Remote work has permanently expanded geographic arbitrage — high-skill, low-cost-of-living is a real strategy.
- Today's "safe" career choice can be tomorrow's disrupted industry. The safest choice is building adaptable, compounding skills — not picking the "right" major based on today's rankings.
- The fields that will matter most in 2028-2032 may not even have established degree programs yet. Students who build strong foundations (quantitative reasoning, systems thinking, communication, technical literacy) will be best positioned to pivot into emerging opportunities.
- Beware the "learn to code" fallacy: the value isn't in the specific skill du jour, it's in the problem-solving mindset. Today it's coding, tomorrow it may be AI orchestration, regulatory navigation, or climate adaptation strategy.
