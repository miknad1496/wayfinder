# Tech Career Transition Maps: Non-Technical → Tech Pathways

## Executive Summary

Career transitions INTO tech from non-tech backgrounds are increasingly viable and often outperform traditional CS degree paths in outcomes. This document catalogs the most successful transition routes, their realistic timelines, true earning trajectories, and failure modes. The data reveals systematic patterns: job-hopping 2-3 years beats internal promotion 10-20%, bootcamp marketing inflates salary expectations by 40-50%, and the highest-value transitions exploit *bridge skills* that non-tech candidates already possess.

---

## 1. Liberal Arts → Tech (UX Research, Product Management, Technical Writing)

### Origin Background
Liberal arts graduates (humanities, social sciences, communications) represent one of the largest non-traditional tech pipelines. These backgrounds excel at narrative, research methodology, and stakeholder communication.

### Typical Tech Roles
- **UX Researcher**: $85K-120K Y1 (no coding required)
- **Product Manager**: $100K-140K Y1 (entry often at Associate PM)
- **Technical Writer**: $75K-95K Y1
- **Content Strategist**: $70K-90K Y1
- **Product Analyst**: $80K-110K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Research methodology**: User interviews, qualitative analysis, and hypothesis testing are identical to academic research. Write research plans in tech exactly as theses—lit review, methodology, findings.
- **Writing systems**: Technical documentation requires structured, audience-aware writing. Liberal arts majors who've written for diverse audiences (academic + public) adapt fastest.
- **Stakeholder analysis**: Understanding competing interests in humanities papers translates directly to managing engineer-designer-business tensions.
- **Narrative construction**: Product strategy docs are narratives. Liberal arts grads construct stronger problem statements and vision documents than engineers.

### Realistic Timeline
- **UX Research**: 6-9 months with portfolio. Path: Google UX Certificate (3 months, ~$200) → portfolio project (3 months) → interviews. No coding needed.
- **Product Management**: 12-18 months. Path: analytics internship or coordinator role → mini-MBA or PM certificate (3 months) → APM program or PM role at startup.
- **Technical Writing**: 3-6 months. Path: tech blog posts + documentation samples → contract roles → full-time.

Fastest route: UX Research (requires fewest prerequisites). Slowest: Product Manager at large company (APM programs competitive, 2-3% acceptance).

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| UX Researcher | $95K | $130K | $160K+ |
| Associate PM | $110K | $160K | $200K+ |
| Technical Writer | $80K | $105K | $130K+ |
| Product Analyst | $95K | $130K | $160K+ |

*Includes base + bonus. PM bonus structures often 15-30% of base at FAANG.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Credibility gap**: Technical teams doubt non-engineers understand feasibility. Solution: learn enough product math (metrics, funnels, cohort analysis) to speak the language. Take one SQL course.
2. **Portfolio requirement for UX**: Weak case studies sink applications. Real UX portfolios show problem definition, research insight, iterations, learning. Lazy portfolios with pretty wireframes don't convert.
3. **Impatience with APM programs**: Google/Facebook APM programs rotate 4-5 roles in 2 years. Candidates expecting to "choose" their team struggle with the shuffle.
4. **Underestimating technical depth needed at PM level**: Senior PMs need to read code reviews, understand latency, know when engineers are sandbagging. Soft skills alone plateau at IC3-4.

**Estimated dropout/failure rate**: 30-40% of career-switchers leave PM/UX within 3 years. Highest churn: those who don't bond with a mentor engineer early.

### Contrarian Insight: Career Advisors Get Wrong

Most advice: "Start as a PM at a big company for prestige." **Wrong.**

Reality: Prestige compounds *slowly* at large companies. A liberal arts grad hired as APM at Google is competing against Stanford CS majors; they'll always feel behind. Meanwhile, the same person hired as PM at a Series B startup (lower bar, more agency) becomes a *practicing* PM faster. In Y3, the startup PM has 2+ product launches, board meeting experience, and hiring decisions. The APM has rotated 4 times and is still learning.

Trajectory: startup PM Y1-2 → FAANG PM Y3+ → VP. Traditional path: FAANG APM Y1-2 → PM Y3 → Senior PM Y5. Both hit Senior PM Y3-5, but the startup route involves actual product ownership.

## 2. Healthcare → Health Tech (Clinical Informatics, Health Data, Telehealth)

### Origin Background
Nurses, physicians, respiratory therapists, and clinical staff understand workflow, compliance, and user pain. This domain knowledge is *not* replaceable and creates 3-5 year advantage over bootcamp grads entering health tech.

### Typical Tech Roles
- **Clinical Informatics Specialist**: $95K-130K Y1 (bridges clinical + data)
- **Health Data Analyst**: $85K-115K Y1 (SQL, HIPAA, reporting)
- **Medical Device QA/Regulatory**: $90K-125K Y1
- **Telehealth Operations Manager**: $80K-110K Y1
- **Clinical Product Manager**: $110K-150K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **HIPAA literacy**: Earned through experience, not certificates. Health tech companies spend months teaching this; you already know it.
- **Workflow documentation**: Charting workflows, medication reconciliation, handoff protocols. Maps directly to requirements documentation and process modeling.
- **Regulatory mindset**: Understanding FDA, Joint Commission, state licensing isn't just compliance—it's embedded product thinking. You can ask "what would regulators reject?" earlier in design.
- **Clinical credibility**: In pitch meetings, you can say "I managed 40 patients daily." Engineers can't. This matters for product buy-in.
- **Failure spotting**: You've seen systems fail at 11pm during shift change. You know which "efficient" designs break under real load.

### Realistic Timeline
- **Data role**: 3-6 months + 1 cert. Path: Google Data Analytics Cert ($200-300, 3-6 months) → portfolio analysis (real datasets: MIMIC-III public, CMS data) → role. HIPAA training is on-the-job.
- **Clinical Informatics**: 12-18 months. Path: Informatics certificate or relevant certs (CHIOC, CPHIMS) + coursework → specialist role. Many programs accept clinical staff without CS background.
- **Clinical PM**: 18-24 months. Path: clinical coordinator → operations → product coordinator → PM. Slower than APM programs but more sustainable (you're inside healthcare; you know the customer).

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Health Data Analyst | $95K | $125K | $150K+ |
| Clinical Informatics | $110K | $145K | $175K+ |
| Medical Device QA | $105K | $135K | $160K+ |
| Telehealth PM | $125K | $165K | $200K+ |

*Note: Healthcare tech salaries are 10-20% lower than FAANG but with 90%+ job security. Less volatility, more predictability.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Culture shock**: Healthcare moves slowly (good for stability, bad for impatient people). Six-month regulatory review feels glacial to someone from startup land.
2. **Coding skill gaps**: Many jump to data roles without SQL depth. They stall at "basic analyst" without ability to optimize queries or pipeline complex transformations.
3. **Clinical + tech gap**: Some assume clinical knowledge alone is enough. It's not. You still need to speak SQL, understand data models, know what "idempotent" means.
4. **Credibility inversion**: Early in career, your clinical background helps. By Y5, if you haven't developed tech depth, younger engineers see you as "the nurse who doesn't code" not "the informatics expert."

**Estimated dropout rate**: 25% in first 18 months (mostly those who underestimated tech learning curve). Another 15% by Y3 (burnout from slow pace). Stability keeps 60%+ long-term.

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "Get a master's in health informatics first." **Mostly wrong.**

Reality: Clinical Informatics master's programs (2 years, $50K-80K) can be replaced by 6 months of self-study + a job offer. The employer will teach HIPAA and domain tools. What you can't learn on the job: SQL, stats, Python. If you're considering an informatics degree, spend 3-4 months on DataCamp (SQL + Python paths), build a portfolio with real healthcare datasets, and apply directly. You'll hit Y1 analyst salary Y0.5 instead of Y2.5.

Exception: If you're a clinician wanting to transition *within your current health system*, an informatics degree/certificate accelerates internal mobility and increases buy-in from leadership.

## 3. Finance/Accounting → FinTech (Financial Data, Compliance Tech, Quant)

### Origin Background
Accountants, financial analysts, and traders have mental models for risk, regulation, and data integrity. FinTech values these models highly because regulatory failures are million-dollar events.

### Typical Tech Roles
- **Financial Data Analyst**: $100K-140K Y1
- **Compliance Tech Engineer**: $105K-150K Y1 (semi-engineering role)
- **Quantitative Analyst**: $120K-180K Y1 (requires strong stats/CS)
- **Risk Systems Engineer**: $110K-155K Y1
- **FinTech Product Manager**: $130K-180K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Regulatory fluency**: Know SOX, AML, KYC, Basel III. You can immediately contribute to compliance roadmaps without learning the domain.
- **Financial statement literacy**: Understand P&L, balance sheets, cash flow. When product roadmap discusses "customer LTV" or "payback period," you parse it faster.
- **Risk thinking**: Mental models for counterparty risk, market risk, model risk. Translate to system risk, data risk, operational risk.
- **Audit readiness**: Experience with audit trails, documentation, evidence gathering. These inform compliance tech design.
- **Reconciliation expertise**: If you've done bank reconciliation, you understand idempotency, eventual consistency, data integrity at scale.

### Realistic Timeline
- **Financial Data Analyst**: 3-6 months + 1 cert. Path: Google Data Analytics Cert → SQL deep-dive (Mode Analytics tutorials, HackerRank) → portfolio projects with public datasets (Kaggle finance data) → role. Finance companies care less about CS degrees; they care about SQL + domain knowledge.
- **Compliance Tech Engineer**: 12-18 months. Path: Finance background + Python/Java bootcamp or self-study (4-6 months) → junior engineer role or "operations engineer" bridge role → compliance tech.
- **Quant Analyst**: 18-36 months minimum. Path: Strong stats + Python (12-18 months intense study). Not realistic without serious math foundation.

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Financial Data Analyst | $120K | $160K | $200K+ |
| Compliance Tech Engineer | $130K | $170K | $210K+ |
| Quant Analyst (phd-track) | $160K | $250K+ | $350K+ |
| FinTech PM | $145K | $200K | $260K+ |

*FinTech salaries are systematically 20-30% higher than general tech due to regulatory risk and talent scarcity. Quant roles can include performance bonuses doubling base.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Underestimating programming depth**: "I know Excel macros" isn't coding. Moving from SQL to production Python requires different thinking. People who treat coding as "advanced Excel" struggle with design patterns, testing, scalability.
2. **Compliance perfectionism**: Coming from finance, you want to over-specify, over-document, over-test. FinTech startups value speed; you might clash with ship-fast culture. You need to learn when "good enough" is actually good enough.
3. **Technical imposter syndrome amplified**: Finance roles have clear hierarchies (associate, director, VP). Tech roles are flatter. You might feel perpetually junior because there's no "VP of Data" step-by-step ladder.
4. **Burnout from regulatory complexity**: Compliance tech is high-stakes but repetitive. Many people find it tedious after 2-3 years.

**Estimated dropout rate**: 20-25% in year 1 (coding too hard), another 20% by Y3 (boredom or culture clash). Retention Y3+ stabilizes at ~60%.

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "Compliance tech is boring but job-safe." **Partially correct but misses upside.**

Reality: Compliance tech is the *least* disrupted vertical in tech. Regulatory requirements change slowly, so knowledge compounds. A compliance tech engineer with 7 years of experience is dramatically more valuable than a 7-year general backend engineer (whose knowledge can be Googled). Career compounding is extreme. But the *mistake* advisors make: they sell compliance tech as "boring safety" instead of "30% salary premium + moat of expertise." It's not boring if you reframe it as "I'm building technical debt prevention for trillion-dollar institutions."

The real trap: compliance tech roles at mid-size fintech (Series B-D) can feel like you're solving the same problem repeatedly. Get to a FinTech that's solving *novel* compliance problems (international, new asset classes, chain-of-custody). That's where the learning compounds.

## 4. Teaching → EdTech / Instructional Design / Corporate Training

### Origin Background
K-12 or higher ed teachers understand learning design, assessment, and classroom dynamics. EdTech companies desperately want this perspective but rarely pay for it.

### Typical Tech Roles
- **Instructional Designer**: $70K-95K Y1 (no coding)
- **Curriculum Developer**: $75K-100K Y1
- **L&D Manager** (corporate training): $80K-110K Y1
- **EdTech Product Manager**: $95K-130K Y1
- **EdTech Data Analyst**: $80K-110K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Learning objective design**: You've written learning outcomes; translate that to feature requirements. "Students will master algebraic equations" → "Users will pass assessment demonstrating concept mastery."
- **Assessment knowledge**: Understanding formative vs summative assessment, construct validity, and rubric design. Most EdTech companies have *terrible* assessments; you can fix that.
- **Pedagogy literacy**: Know constructivism, Bloom's taxonomy, zone of proximal development. EdTech teams often build for "what works in theory" not "what works in classrooms."
- **Failure diagnosis**: You've watched hundreds of students struggle. You know that a "confusing interface" often masks a conceptual gap. Product teams often blame UI when the problem is pedagogical.

### Realistic Timeline
- **Instructional Designer**: 3-6 months. Path: Articulate Storyline course ($1500 one-time) + Google UX Certificate (optional) + portfolio (3 sample courses/modules) → role. No coding required.
- **Curriculum Developer**: 6-12 months. Path: Find EdTech company, start as content contractor → full-time curriculum role. Often internal mobility; easier to transition if you get your foot in.
- **Corporate L&D**: 3-9 months. Path: Move into corporate training role (many companies hire teachers for this—salary $70K-85K to start) → manager role → strategic L&D → move to EdTech.
- **EdTech PM**: 18-24 months. Overlap with UX research path (above).

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Instructional Designer | $80K | $110K | $140K+ |
| Curriculum Developer | $85K | $120K | $155K+ |
| L&D Manager | $95K | $130K | $160K+ |
| EdTech PM | $110K | $155K | $200K+ |

*EdTech salaries are 15-25% below general tech but offer remote-first cultures and mission alignment, which teachers value.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Salary expectations shock**: Teachers earn $45K-75K. EdTech entry roles offer $70K-90K. Feels like a raise, but Y3-Y5 growth is slower than general tech.
2. **Misalignment on pedagogy**: You want courses to match learning science. EdTech company wants to ship fast and iterate. You suggest 3-month user research; they ask for a beta in 2 weeks. Culture mismatch.
3. **Burnout from classroom survivors**: Many EdTech companies have *zero* teachers on product. You're the only voice for "this won't work in real schools." You burn out being that voice alone.
4. **Underbidding in negotiations**: Teachers accept lower offers. Your market rate is $95K; you accept $75K because it's a raise. By Y3, you're $40K behind peers who negotiated harder.

**Estimated dropout rate**: 30% in first year (culture/pedagogy mismatch). Another 20% by Y4 (salary growth disappointment). 50% of career-switchers leave EdTech; those who don't often land in EdTech PM or Director roles.

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "EdTech needs teachers—you have a unique skillset." **Half-true; exploited.**

Reality: EdTech companies will hire you for content/curriculum roles and massively undervalue pedagogical expertise. They view teaching as "domain knowledge" (like healthcare workers to health tech) but don't pay for it. Doctors transitioning to health tech negotiate salary 10-15% higher than non-doctors; teachers don't.

Strategic move: Don't accept an "Instructional Designer" role at $75K when you're worth $95K+ to them. Frame yourself as "Learning Scientist + Product Designer." Take a PM or Product Designer role instead, where you *control* learning experience decisions instead of implementing someone else's bad ideas. Most teachers have stronger design intuition than their product managers; lean into that.

## 5. Military → Tech (Which Branches/MOS Translate Best, Security Clearance Value)

### Origin Background
Military backgrounds offer discipline, systems thinking, and in some specialties, direct transferability to tech roles (signals intelligence, cybersecurity, software development in modern branches).

### Branch/MOS Translation Table

| Branch | Best MOS | Tech Transition | Timeline |
|--------|----------|-----------------|----------|
| Air Force | Cyberwarfare operator, Software engineer (digital applications) | Security engineering, DevSecOps | 3-6 mo |
| Navy | Nuclear reactor operator, IT specialist, Cryptologic technician | SRE, security architecture | 6-12 mo |
| Army | Signal officer, Information security specialist | Cloud infra, security | 6-12 mo |
| Marine Corps | Intelligence analyst, Communications | Data analyst, GIS specialist | 6-12 mo |
| Any | 10+ years experience in technical specialty | Tech leadership, principal engineer | 0-3 mo |

### Typical Tech Roles
- **Security Engineer**: $110K-150K Y1
- **DevSecOps Engineer**: $115K-160K Y1
- **Site Reliability Engineer (SRE)**: $120K-160K Y1
- **Security Architect**: $130K-170K Y1 (only for 10+ years military + cert)
- **Compliance Tech Engineer**: $110K-150K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Systems thinking**: Military ops require understanding nested systems (logistics, communications, supply chains). Directly translates to understanding infrastructure complexity, failure modes, cascading risks.
- **Documentation discipline**: Military requires detailed runbooks, after-action reports, change logs. You're used to audit trails and evidence; you'll be meticulous in tech ops.
- **Clearance + compliance**: Active Secret/Top Secret clearance is valuable. Some FAANG contracts need cleared employees; you earn a 5-15% premium. Compliance mindset transfers directly.
- **Stress tolerance**: You've worked under actual consequences (lives at stake). Startup chaos seems manageable. This isn't skill-based but psychological; valuable nonetheless.

### Realistic Timeline
- **With relevant MOS (cyber, signals, software)**: 3-6 months. Many can move directly to security role.
- **IT specialist or adjacent**: 6-12 months. Path: CompTIA Security+ ($400) + entry-level security analyst → security engineer in 12 months.
- **Non-technical MOS (logistics, operations, intelligence)**: 12-24 months. Path: Data Analyst cert (Google, 3-6 mo) → analytics role → move to security/data infrastructure.
- **Clearance leverage**: If you have active TS/SCI, contractors (Booz Allen, Palantir, defense tech) can hire you Y0 at 10-20% premium with minimal experience.

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Security Engineer (no clearance) | $115K | $150K | $185K+ |
| Security Engineer (active TS/SCI) | $135K | $175K | $220K+ |
| SRE | $125K | $165K | $210K+ |
| Security Architect | $145K | $195K | $250K+ |

*Security roles pay 15-25% premium over general backend/infra. Clearance adds additional 10-20% premium.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Expecting to stay in uniform culture**: Startups are chaos. Flat hierarchies, fast iteration, questioning authority, psychological safety through debate (not command). Military mindset can clash.
2. **Technical knowledge gaps underestimated**: "I managed security ops in the military" doesn't mean you can code or understand modern cloud-native security. You need to learn.
3. **Interview pressure**: Military folks often undersell themselves in tech interviews. You're trained to be humble; tech interviews reward confident, specific technical examples. Mismatch.
4. **Age/experience bias at startups**: 35-year-old with 15 years military experience interviewing at 28-year-old team with "flat structure." They worry you'll be overqualified or clash with culture.

**Estimated dropout rate**: 15-20% (military folks tend to stick with jobs; lower churn than other switchers). But some struggle with interpersonal dynamics (10-15% report "cultural fit" issues Y1-2).

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "Your security clearance is gold." **Misleading.**

Reality: Clearance is an asset only in specific contexts (government contractors, defense tech, large defense vendors). At Apple, Google, or startup fintech, your clearance is worthless. In fact, you might *prefer* not to mention it (hiring managers worry about headhunting by three-letter agencies, which adds friction).

The real play: Leverage clearance + tech skills to land at Palantir, Booz Allen, or other contractors (Y1 $120K-140K) where you're immediately valuable. In 2-3 years, move to FAANG where you're just a talented security engineer. You'll have built a war chest, developed real tech skills, and avoided the startup chaos.

But don't oversell clearance. Overselling leads to overqualified label → not hired.

## 6. Retail/Service → Tech Support → Engineering Pipeline

### Origin Background
Retail, hospitality, and service workers develop customer empathy, troubleshooting, and communication skills. This is the most underrated pathway because it involves multiple transitions (support → specialist → engineer) and bootcamp can accelerate only specific steps.

### Typical Tech Roles (Pipeline)
1. **Year 0-1: Technical Support Specialist**: $45K-65K
2. **Year 1-2: Support Specialist II / Desktop Support**: $55K-75K
3. **Year 2-3: Systems Administrator / Junior SysAdmin**: $65K-85K
4. **Year 3-4: Junior Software Engineer** (with bootcamp): $85K-110K
5. **Year 5+: Mid-level Engineer**: $120K-160K

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Triage discipline**: You've handled angry customers. You know escalation, prioritization, and staying calm under pressure. This translates to on-call incident response, customer support technical issues, and post-mortems.
- **Documentation for non-experts**: Retail teaches you to explain complex policies (returns, warranties, reconciliation) to customers with zero context. Tech documentation skills follow—how to write runbooks for humans, not just machines.
- **Pattern recognition**: You've seen fraud patterns, customer behavior loops, and system failure signatures. You can spot anomalies faster than someone who's only read monitoring dashboards.
- **Systems thinking from operations**: Managing a shift (inventory, staffing, customer flow) teaches you systems. IT infrastructure isn't fundamentally different—components, workflows, failure modes.

### Realistic Timeline

**Total pipeline (retail → engineer): 4-6 years**

- **Support Specialist**: 0-12 months (entry into tech). No certs required; many companies hire retail workers into tier-1 support ($45K-55K).
- **Systems Administrator**: 2-4 years from entry. Requires CompTIA A+ ($500, learn in parallel) → some hands-on SysAdmin work → Security+ (if needed).
- **Bootcamp accelerator**: 3-6 months, $12K-25K. After 1-2 years in support/SysAdmin, do a bootcamp to jump to junior engineer instead of 4-year organic climb. Bootcamp grads from support backgrounds often outperform traditional bootcamp grads because they understand systems.

**Fast path (with bootcamp)**: Retail (Y0) → Support ($45K, Y0-1) → SysAdmin ($70K, Y1-2) → Bootcamp (Y2-3) → Junior Engineer ($95K, Y3-4). Total: 4 years, $95K Y4.

**Organic path (no bootcamp)**: Retail (Y0) → Support ($45K, Y0-1) → Support II ($60K, Y1-2) → SysAdmin ($75K, Y2-4) → engineer track or stay in ops. Total: 4 years, $75K Y4.

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Technical Support | $55K | $70K | $80K |
| SysAdmin | $70K | $90K | $110K |
| Junior Engineer (bootcamp) | $95K | $130K | $165K+ |
| Mid-level Engineer | $120K | $160K | $210K+ |

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Support burnout**: Tier-1 support is soul-crushing for 18+ months. You deal with the same password reset 200 times. Many people quit before year 2.
2. **Bootcamp trap at wrong time**: Bootcamp only works if you have *systems knowledge already*. A bootcamp grad with zero support experience struggles conceptually; a bootcamp grad with 2 years support/SysAdmin thrives.
3. **Bias from hiring managers**: "Why would I hire a former retail worker as an engineer?" You have to overcome perception. This is real; you need strong interview performance + portfolio projects.
4. **Getting stuck in support career**: There's a promotion ladder in support (Tier 1 → 2 → 3 → Support Lead). It's easier to stay in known territory than jump to engineering. Many drift here.

**Estimated dropout rate**: 40% don't make it past Year 1 of support (burnout). Of those who do, 60% successfully transition toward engineering/SysAdmin. Final success rate from retail entry: ~35% hit junior engineer within 6 years.

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "Do a bootcamp, become an engineer, ignore the support pathway." **Wrong for many people.**

Reality: Bootcamp-only grads hit the "bootcamp salary ceiling" ($65K-85K Y1) and plateau. A retail-to-engineer pipeline person hits $95K-110K Y4, and by Y6, they're ahead. Why? Because they have systems context. They understand why uptime matters, debugging methodology, customer impact thinking. That costs 2-3 years to learn the hard way if you've only done greenfield projects in bootcamp.

The move: If you're in support, don't rush bootcamp. Get to SysAdmin first (2 years). Then do a serious bootcamp (12 weeks, full-time) or self-study (6 months) focused on *systems and infrastructure* (Go, Rust, Kubernetes) not generic bootcamp JavaScript. You'll enter at mid-level engineer salary, not junior.

## 7. Law → Legal Tech / Compliance Tech / Privacy

### Origin Background
Lawyers understand regulation, argumentation, precedent, and risk. Legal tech desperately needs domain expertise but has historically underpaid for it (major opportunity here).

### Typical Tech Roles
- **Legal Operations Engineer**: $90K-130K Y1 (operations + contract automation)
- **Compliance Tech Product Manager**: $120K-160K Y1
- **Privacy Engineer**: $110K-160K Y1 (GDPR, CCPA implementation)
- **Legal Data Analyst**: $95K-130K Y1
- **Contract Automation Specialist**: $100K-140K Y1

### Bridge Skills That Transfer (Specific)
- **Regulatory interpretation**: You can read regulations and translate them to requirements. Non-lawyers read GDPR and get confused; you extract obligations.
- **Risk assessment**: "What could go wrong legally?" is your native question. You identify risks others miss. Critical for compliance/privacy product design.
- **Argumentation structure**: Legal writing requires premises → logic → conclusion. This is systems design. You structure technical requirements like legal briefs.
- **Precedent thinking**: Legal experience with precedent and case law translates to understanding system constraints, edge cases, and historical context that shapes current rules.
- **Stakeholder management**: You've managed clients, judges, opposing counsel. You can navigate complex stakeholder landscapes in tech faster than engineers.

### Realistic Timeline
- **Legal Operations**: 6-12 months. Path: Internal legal ops role at large company (many Big Tech hire lawyers for this) → move to legal-tech company.
- **Privacy Engineer**: 12-18 months. Path: Privacy certificate (IAPP CIPM/CIPP, $500-800) + some coding/SQL fundamentals (3-6 months) → privacy engineering role.
- **Compliance PM**: 12-24 months. Path: GC office → compliance team → move to compliance tech company → PM. Or: Legal background + product thinking + 3-6 month ramp.
- **Contract Automation**: 3-9 months. Path: NDA/contract management experience → learn automation platforms (Kaleido, Evisort, etc.) → contract automation specialist → product role.

### Salary Trajectory

| Role | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Legal Operations Engineer | $110K | $150K | $190K+ |
| Privacy Engineer | $130K | $170K | $220K+ |
| Compliance PM | $135K | $185K | $240K+ |
| Contract Automation Specialist | $115K | $155K | $200K+ |

*Legal tech and compliance tech pay 15-30% above general software engineer roles because regulatory risk is high and domain talent is scarce.*

### Failure Modes & Dropout Rates

**Why people fail:**
1. **Imposter syndrome in tech**: You've been an expert for 10+ years; now you're junior again. Many lawyers struggle with this identity shift and bail.
2. **Regulatory conservatism clashing with startup speed**: In law, you move carefully. In legal tech startups, you ship features fast and fix legal issues later. This can cause ethical friction.
3. **Coding/data fluency gaps**: Privacy engineering requires some coding. You can learn it, but many lawyers don't try. They stay in ops/PM where domain knowledge alone is enough.
4. **Salary negotiation mistakes**: Lawyers often accept lower offers ("It's a new field, I don't know market rates"). Legal-tech companies *know* domain experts are scarce; demand top of range. Most lawyers leave 20-40% in salary.

**Estimated dropout rate**: 20% (lawyers are typically determined; low churn). But 30% don't meaningfully engage with tech skills, staying in "legal + product thinking" roles and hitting salary ceiling at Y4-5.

### Contrarian Insight

Most advice: "Legal tech needs lawyers—you're in high demand." **True but leveraged wrong.**

Reality: You're in demand, but legal-tech companies will try to hire you as a "legal product manager" at $100K when you should be commanding $130K as a "Compliance Engineering Manager." The gap: engineers have leverage because they have options. You (lawyer) feel grateful just to be in tech and accept the discount.

Strategic move: Don't be the "lawyer who knows tech." Be the "tech person who happens to know law." This is a mindset shift. Learn coding/SQL seriously (3-6 months, genuinely skill up). Then negotiate from position of strength: "I'm a full-stack engineer with regulatory domain expertise, market rate is $140K+." You'll get hired at startups where engineers are scarce. You'll also be harder to replace, increasing your leverage for future raises.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns: What Actually Determines Success

### Salary Growth: Job-Hopping vs. Internal Promotion
- **Internal promotion**: 3-5% annual raises. Y1 $80K → Y5 $95K.
- **Job-hopping every 2-3 years**: 10-20% bumps per jump. Y1 $80K → Y3 $100K (job 2) → Y5 $125K (job 3).

Real data: Median salary growth for job-stayers is 25-35% over 5 years. Median for job-hoppers is 55-70%. The difference is stark.

**Tactical insight**: For career switchers, year 1-2 is "learning salary" (you're overpaid for learning). Year 2-3, your market value jumps (you're now experienced) but your employer won't reflect that in raises. Job-hop to capture the jump. Internal promotions rarely match market repricing.

### Bootcamp Salary Reality Check
Marketing claims: "Bootcamp grads average $120K in year 1."
Reality: Median is $65K-85K. Mean is inflated by outliers ($150K+ at FAANG).

- 20% of bootcamp grads land tech roles (rest do contracting or pivot).
- Of those who land roles: 60% at $65K-85K, 25% at $85K-110K, 15% above $110K.
- Outliers ($120K+): previously made $100K+ in other fields, or had professional networks, or are exceptional programmers.

**Tactical insight**: If you're considering a bootcamp, the question isn't "will I make $120K?" It's "will I make more than my current career + opportunity cost + $20K bootcamp?" For retail worker ($45K) → bootcamp ($20K) → junior engineer ($90K) in 18 months: math works. For lawyer ($180K) → bootcamp → junior engineer ($110K): doesn't work, even as pivot.

### Retention in First 3 Years
Career switchers leaving tech by year 3: 30-40% (varies by pathway).

Churn drivers:
- **Mismatch on pace**: Tech is fast; healthcare/law/education are slow. 25% of churn is culture clash.
- **Underestimated learning curve**: Thought year 1 would be productive; spent it confused. 20% of churn.
- **Salary disappointment**: Thought they'd be $110K; they're $80K. Recalibration takes time. 15% of churn.
- **Burnout**: Tech roles are intense. Previous careers were different paces. 20% of churn.
- **Better opportunity elsewhere**: Not churn from dissatisfaction, but active recruitment. 20% of switchers get recruited back to their previous industry at higher salary.

**Tactical insight**: Expect the first 18 months to be slower, less lucrative, and more intense than your previous field. Build psychological cushion. Don't quit at month 14 because "this isn't working." Most successful transitions hit stride at month 18-24.

### Certs and Bootcamps: When Worth It

**Worth it:**
- Google Data Analytics cert for non-tech → data role transition. $200-300, 3-6 months, immediate ROI.
- CompTIA A+ for support → sysadmin transition. $500, 2-3 months study, required for many roles.
- AWS/GCP certs for engineers (any background) moving to cloud/infra. 25-30% salary premium.
- IAPP Privacy certs for law/compliance → privacy tech. Signals domain knowledge.

**Not worth it (or worth doing cheaply):**
- Generic "coding bootcamps" without specialization. Skip formal bootcamp; do self-study (free/cheap online) + portfolio.
- Multiple certs stacked (A+ + Network+ + Security+). Pick the one your target job lists.
- MBA for career switcher. Rare case where it helps; usually doesn't for tech transition.

## Final Framework: Your Decision Tree

1. **Do you want to code?** → No: UX, PM, data analyst, legal ops, instructional design, health data, compliance.  → Yes: continue.
2. **Do you have domain expertise (healthcare, law, finance)?** → Yes: Leverage it. Negotiate 10-15% premium. → No: continue.
3. **Can you afford 6-12 months of learning?** → Yes: Slower organic path, lower risk. → No: Bootcamp, 12-18 months, higher risk/reward.
4. **Do you have advanced math/stats foundation?** → Yes: Quant, hard SWE roles. → No: Data analyst, support→engineer pipeline.
5. **Is job security or growth more important?** → Security: Healthcare, legal, compliance, education. → Growth: Startup fintech, venture-backed, infrastructure.

## Summary: The Pattern

The highest-success transitions exploit *specific bridge skills* that non-tech backgrounds already possess, combine 6-18 months of deliberate skill-building (bootcamp or self-study), and require 2-3 job transitions within 5 years to match FAANG engineer salary curves. Those who treat their non-tech background as an asset (not a deficit to overcome) and negotiate accordingly do 20-30% better than those who accept "career changer" pricing.

The contrarian insight across all pathways: **Move fast early.** Years 0-3 are about skill-building and salary floor-setting. Years 3-5 are about consolidating that position. Most switchers optimize for security and comfort in years 0-3, which locks them into lower salary trajectories. The winners job-hop, negotiate aggressively, and treat the first 3 years as active market-rate discovery.
