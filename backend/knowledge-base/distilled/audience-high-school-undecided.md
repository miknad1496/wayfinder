# You Don't Have to Know Yet: A Career Guide for Undecided High School Students

You're 16, 17, or 18 years old. At some point this year (or next), an adult has asked you: "What do you want to be?" And you either:

- Panicked and said something that popped into your head
- Gave the answer you think they want to hear
- Said "I don't know" and watched their face fall
- Changed your answer depending on who was asking

If you're reading this, you probably feel some combination of stressed, confused, and guilty about not having the answer. Here's the truth: that's not a personal failing. It's a feature of being your age.

---

## The Pressure Is Not Proportional to the Decision

Let's start with permission to breathe.

Most adults have changed career paths multiple times. The average person will work for 10+ employers over their lifetime. Many will have 3-5 fundamentally different "careers." Your high school guidance counselor knows this. Your parents know this, even if they don't say it out loud. Yet somehow, at 17, you're expected to know.

Here's why that's absurd: **You've had one life experience so far—being a student.** You've never worked full-time. You've never sustained a project over years. You've never built something real and watched people actually use it. You've never felt the sting of failure on stakes that matter. You've never experienced the grit required to master something difficult.

Career satisfaction research by psychologists like Daniel Pink shows that what actually makes people happy at work isn't "finding your passion" in a lightning-bolt moment. It's three things:

1. **Autonomy** — having control over how and what you work on
2. **Mastery** — getting genuinely better at something you care about
3. **Purpose** — knowing your work matters to someone or something

Notice what's not on that list? The "perfect job title" you imagined when you were 14.

The myth of the "perfect fit" sets you up for disappointment. The data is clear: satisfaction comes from how you work, not which title is on your business card.

## What You Should Actually Be Doing Right Now

Instead of choosing a career, choose **skills to build**. Here's why that's smarter:

**Skills are currency.** They compound. A student who builds genuine proficiency in writing, coding, data analysis, or public speaking at 17 enters adulthood with leverage. A student who can run a small project or lead others has optionality. Skills open doors to jobs that don't even exist yet.

**Skills bridge theory and reality.** When you say "I like science," that's 1,000 different careers. When you say "I built a Python model to predict X," you've learned something about what research actually feels like.

**Skills reveal what actually interests you.** You can't know you hate accounting until you've tried it. You can't know you love UX design until you've iterated on something real.

### Low-Cost Ways to Test Drive Your Interests in High School

**Job shadowing or informational interviews** (free)
- Email someone who has the job you're curious about. Be specific: "I'm thinking about tech, and I'd love to shadow you for a day" is way more likely to get a yes than a vague request. Many professionals say yes because someone asked.
- Talk to 3-5 people in completely different fields. You'll learn what you don't want faster than what you do.

**Volunteer work** (free to low-cost)
- Non-profit orgs, hospitals, schools, community centers, local government. You'll see real work without the pressure of being "hired."
- Pick something you think you might like, but also consider volunteering in an unfamiliar field. Surprises happen.

**Projects you lead** (free with internet)
- Build something. Code something. Write something. Make something. The skill you build matters less than learning what "completing a real project" feels like.
- Start a club. Write articles. Create a video series. The pressure is lower, the learning is real.

**Online courses or certifications** (free to $100)
- A summer course in coding, design, marketing, data analysis, or writing. You'll learn whether the fundamentals click for you.
- Free platforms: Khan Academy, Coursera (audit for free), MIT OpenCourseWare, YouTube. Paid platforms: Skillshare, Codecademy.

**Part-time work** (gets you paid)
- Retail, food service, tutoring, freelance gigs. Yes, these feel unglamorous. But they teach you about reliability, customer interaction, time management, and work culture.
- You'll also figure out quickly whether you care about work being "meaningful" or whether you just want a paycheck (both are valid).

## The Holland Code: A Framework for Thinking About Careers

Career researchers have spent decades studying what kinds of work click with what kinds of people. One useful framework is the Holland RIASEC model. It's not predictive destiny—it's a conversation starter with yourself.

Think about which of these resonates:

**Realistic** — You like hands-on work, building things, figuring out how stuff actually works
- Explores: Engineering, construction, carpentry, mechanics, agriculture, trades
- Real question: Do you like solving practical problems, or do you get bored with the unglamorous details?

**Investigative** — You like research, analysis, solving puzzles, understanding systems
- Explores: Data science, medicine, physics, economics, research, forensics
- Real question: Do you enjoy digging into problems even when there's no immediate payoff, or do you need to see practical applications?

**Artistic** — You like creative, unstructured work; making things that didn't exist before
- Explores: UX design, marketing, journalism, architecture, writing, music, visual arts
- Real question: Can you live with ambiguity and subjective feedback, or do you need clear benchmarks of success?

**Social** — You like working with people, helping, teaching, understanding what makes them tick
- Explores: Counseling, HR, nursing, teaching, social work, organizational development, coaching
- Real question: Do you actually energize from helping others, or do you sometimes find people exhausting?

**Enterprising** — You like leading, persuading, organizing, taking risks, seeing the big picture
- Explores: Consulting, sales, entrepreneurship, politics, business development, management
- Real question: Do you like the uncertainty of building something from scratch, or do you prefer working within established systems?

**Conventional** — You like organizing information, following systems, clear structures, precision
- Explores: Accounting, financial analysis, project management, administration, data entry, operations
- Real question: Does structure feel constraining or enabling to you?

Most people are a combination. You might be Investigative + Realistic (science + building) or Artistic + Social (teaching + creativity). That combination is your fingerprint.

**Your first action:** Rank these honestly. Which two or three describe you? Which one absolutely does not? That's information.

## What About Money? The Earnings Reality Check

Let's talk about the thing adults won't always say: money matters, and also money is more complicated than the rankings suggest.

Here's what the data actually shows:

- A computer science graduate earns ~$75K in year 1. Impressive.
- A visual arts graduate earns ~$28K in year 1. Discouraging.
- An economics graduate earns ~$55K in year 1, but by year 4, they're at the 75th percentile of $110K. This is a "boring" major with huge upside.
- A biology graduate earns ~$35K in year 1—and that's genuinely difficult without grad school.

**The lesson:** Year 1 salary is not the whole story. A field's short-term earnings don't tell you about growth trajectory, whether you need grad school to be competitive, or whether the work will sustain you.

Money is also not deterministic. Some people in low-earning fields live great lives because they've built specific skills or lived cheaply. Some people in high-earning fields are miserable because they sacrificed everything for the paycheck.

**The real financial strategy:** Build skills that are in demand. Learn to sell what you build (technically, creatively, or interpersonally). Don't choose a major based on year 1 salary. Choose fields where the growth trajectory and lifestyle make sense to you.

## College Planning Without Knowing Your Major

Good news: **most colleges don't require you to declare a major until the end of sophomore year.**

Even better: many schools actively support exploration.

**What to look for when choosing a college:**

- **Open exploration programs** — Some schools (Wesleyan, Middlebury, many liberal arts colleges) explicitly design their first two years for breadth. You take classes across fields.
- **Strong advising** — Does the school have accessible academic advisors who help you think through interests? Or does it feel like you're on your own?
- **Access to internships and projects** — Can you test-drive careers while still in school? This matters more than any course title.
- **Community of learners** — Do you know people there with wildly different interests? You'll learn more from them than from lectures.

**Real talk:** The college itself matters less than what you do while you're there. Someone who goes to a state school and pursues 3 internships, starts a project, and seeks out mentors will have more clarity (and opportunity) than someone at an elite school who shows up to class and goes home.

## The Gap Year Option: When and How

A gap year—taking a year between high school and college to work, travel, explore, or volunteer—isn't for everyone. But it's worth considering if:

- You're burned out on school and need a break before committing to more of it
- You have no idea what you want and want to buy some time without the debt of wandering through college
- You want to build some skills and earn money before studying
- You're good at self-direction and won't just sleep until noon for a year

**How to structure it so it's not just "time off":**
- Work at least part of it (to build skills and earn money)
- Travel or volunteer to get out of your hometown's gravity
- Take a focused online course or two (something specific, not random)
- Keep a journal about what you're learning about yourself

**How colleges and employers view gap years:** Actually pretty well now. It's not 1985. Employers have seen that gap-year students often return more focused and mature. Colleges see it as self-direction. The key: come back with something to show for it (a story, a skill, perspective).

## The Parental Pressure Conversation

Your parents probably didn't have infinite options. Or they have a very specific vision: doctor, lawyer, engineer, business owner. Or they're just terrified you'll end up flipping burgers forever.

Here's how to have this conversation:

**Don't:**
- Argue about whether being a doctor is a "good" career (it is, for some people)
- Act like they're wrong for caring about your financial stability
- Say "I'll figure it out" without an actual plan

**Do:**
- Acknowledge what they care about (stability, respect, financial security) — they're not monsters
- Show them you're being strategic, not aimless. "Here's what I'm learning" is better than "I don't know"
- Propose the skills-first approach: "Instead of committing to a major, I want to build X skill and explore Y field in college"
- Share the data: a biology major without grad school plans doesn't actually lead to a "science career," but building coding skills opens multiple doors
- Propose milestones: "By the end of freshman year, I'll have done three informational interviews" or "I'll try a coding bootcamp over the summer"

Most parents soften when they see you're thoughtful, not reactive.

## Your 30-Day Exploration Plan

You don't need to overhaul your life. You need to gather information. Here's something you can actually do:

**Week 1: Self-Reflection**
- Rank the Holland Code types. Be honest.
- List 5 skills you have now (coding, writing, organizing, listening, building, whatever). How did you get them?
- List 3 skills you want to develop. Why?

**Week 2: Talk to Real People**
- Reach out to three people with different jobs. Ask them: "What does your work actually involve?" "What did you not expect about this job?" "What would you change?"
- You don't need to know them. Email a LinkedIn message. Most people say yes.

**Week 3: Try Something**
- Sign up for a free online course in something you're curious about (coding, writing, design, data, marketing, whatever). Do at least 3 modules.
- Or, start a small project: something you'll actually finish in 3-4 weeks.

**Week 4: Reflect and Plan**
- What surprised you? What bored you? What do you want to do more of?
- Pick one skill to go deeper on in the next three months.
- Decide on one school activity, volunteer gig, or part-time job that will build that skill.

That's it. Not a career plan. Just information.

---

## One More Thing

You're facing a decision that shouldn't carry as much weight as it does. That's not your fault. But you have more power than you think.

You can't control the pressure. You can control your response to it. You can choose to be curious instead of certain. You can test before committing. You can build skills that open doors instead of walking through the door you think you're supposed to.

Most of the successful people you know didn't find their path at 17. They found it at 22, or 28, or 35. They built it along the way by saying yes to interesting opportunities, by getting good at something, by working with people who inspired them.

You can do the same. You don't have to know yet.

But you can start exploring today.
