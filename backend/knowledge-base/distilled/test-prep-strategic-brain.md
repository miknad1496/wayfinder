# Test Prep Strategic Brain — PSAT/NMSQT, SAT, ACT

## Purpose

This is the STRATEGIC layer for standardized testing. It does NOT teach how to solve math problems or parse reading passages (see `sat-act-master-brain.md` for that). This brain answers: **When should a student test? Which test? Submit or withhold? And how do you convert a test score into $100K+ in scholarship leverage?**

## MODULE 1: THE PSAT/NMSQT — THE $200K LEVER MOST FAMILIES IGNORE

### What It Actually Is

The PSAT/NMSQT (Preliminary SAT/National Merit Scholarship Qualifying Test) is taken in October of junior year. Most families treat it as a practice SAT. This is a strategic mistake. The PSAT is the ENTRY POINT for the National Merit Scholarship Program, which controls access to approximately $200M+ in annual corporate and institutional scholarships.

### How the Selection Index Works

The PSAT produces a Selection Index (SI) from 320 to 1520, calculated as: (Reading & Writing score × 2) + Math score, all scaled. The SI determines National Merit qualification.

**The Three Tiers:**

| Tier | Selection Index Required | Approximate Percentage | What It Unlocks |
|------|------------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Commended Student** | 208 (Class of 2025) / 210 (Class of 2026). National cutoff, same for all states. Trending UP — expect 211-212 for Class of 2027. | Top ~3% of all test-takers (~34,000 students) | Recognition only. No scholarship from NMSC. BUT: some universities use Commended status to trigger institutional merit aid. |
| **Semifinalist** | Varies by state (range: 210 in New Mexico/North Dakota/Wyoming to 225 in Massachusetts/New Jersey, Class of 2026). See full 50-state table below. | Top ~1% within each state (~16,000 students) | Advances to Finalist competition. This is where the money lives. |
| **Finalist** | Semifinalist + SAT confirmation score + school endorsement + essay | ~95% of Semifinalists advance (~15,000 students) | Eligible for $2,500 NMSC scholarship (small), corporate-sponsored scholarships (medium), and INSTITUTIONAL full-ride scholarships (massive). |

### The State Arbitrage

National Merit Semifinalist cutoffs vary dramatically by state because they're set at the top ~1% of EACH state's test-takers. This creates a geographic arbitrage.

**Strategic implication:** For families who relocate or have dual-residency situations, the state where the student takes the PSAT determines which cutoff applies. This is not gaming — it's understanding the system. The SLM should flag this for any family currently in a borderline SI range who has legitimate multi-state ties.

**Key trend:** 48 of 50 states saw HIGHER cutoffs from Class of 2025→2026, driven by digital PSAT score inflation. Only New Mexico dropped (-1). Nevada and North Dakota held steady. Plan for continued upward drift of 1-3 points annually.

The catch: most require the student to designate that university as their "first choice" with NMSC by a specific deadline (typically February-May of senior year). **The diagnostic question:** "Is this student within striking distance of their state's Semifinalist cutoff?" If yes — intensive PSAT prep in sophomore spring/junior fall is the HIGHEST-ROI test prep investment in the entire system. A 3-point SI improvement can be worth $200K.

## MODULE 2: THE DIGITAL SAT (DSAT) — ADAPTIVE STRATEGY

### The Adaptive Module Logic

The Digital SAT uses a 2-stage adaptive design within each section (Reading & Writing, Math):

**Stage 1 (Module 1):** Mix of easy, medium, and hard questions. Everyone gets the same difficulty distribution. Performance on Module 1 determines the difficulty of Module 2.

**Stage 2 (Module 2):** Either the "harder" module or the "easier" module.

### Exact Routing Thresholds (Verified 2025-2026)

| Section | Questions in M1 | Routing Threshold to Harder M2 | Raw Minimum |
|---------|----------------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| **Reading & Writing** | 27 | ~70% correct | 15-17 of 27 correct |
| **Math** | 22 | ~60-65% correct | 13-15 of 22 correct |

### Score Ceilings and Floors by Module Assignment

| Section | Easier Module 2 — Score CEILING | Harder Module 2 — Score FLOOR |
|---------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Reading & Writing** | 560-590 (HARD CAP — even a perfect easier M2 cannot exceed this) | 470-500 (guaranteed minimum even with zero correct on harder M2) |
| **Math** | 560-650 (HARD CAP) | 440-470 (guaranteed minimum) |

**The critical strategic implication:** Module 1 is the GATEKEEPER. The math is brutal: a student who misses the routing threshold in Reading & Writing is CAPPED at 560-590 in that section — meaning their maximum possible composite is roughly 1390-1450 even with a perfect Math score. For Math, the easier-module cap of 560-650 means a student routed to the easier Math module who also gets the easier R&W module faces a theoretical maximum composite of approximately 1190-1240. Falling into BOTH easier modules makes a 1300+ score mathematically impossible.

This makes Module 1 accuracy the single highest-leverage behavior in the entire test. Every question in Module 1 carries disproportionate weight because it determines your scoring RANGE, not just your score.

### The 5 Strategic Principles for DSAT

**1. Module 1 Is King — Slow Down**
Unlike the old SAT where all questions were equally weighted within a section, the DSAT's adaptive routing means Module 1 mistakes are disproportionately costly. Students should allocate EXTRA care to Module 1, even if it means slightly less time on Module 2. Getting routed to the harder Module 2 is more valuable than having extra minutes on the easier one.

**2. The "Hard Module" Psychological Trap**
When Module 2 feels significantly harder, many students panic. This is BACKWARDS — a hard Module 2 means you did WELL on Module 1 and you're in the high-scoring pool. The student who gets the hard Module 2 and gets 50% right will likely outscore the student who got the easy Module 2 and got 90% right. The SLM must preemptively frame this for every test-taker.

**3. Desmos Mastery Is a Competitive Weapon**
The DSAT provides the Desmos graphing calculator for the ENTIRE Math section. This changes the game: students who master Desmos can instantly solve systems of equations, verify polynomial roots, and graph inequalities — bypassing manual algebra entirely. The strategic play is learning Desmos BEFORE test day as a problem-solving shortcut, not just a verification tool. However: weak students over-rely on ANY calculator, burning 30-45 seconds per problem solvable in 10 seconds mentally. The optimal approach is Desmos for complex systems/graphing, mental math for algebraic manipulation.

**4. Rapid Context-Switching Replaces Sustained Reading**
DSAT R&W features rapid-fire, single-question passages across drastically different subjects — poetry, science, history, social science — in quick succession. Each passage is 25-150 words (vs 500+ on paper SAT). This eliminates the "read the whole passage first" strategy and replaces it with a new skill: fast mental context-switching. Students must practice jumping between unrelated topics without cognitive drag. Skimming is counterproductive (nothing to skim past), but so is lingering — read once, identify the claim or structure, answer, and MOVE.

**5. Score Reporting Speed Creates Retake Strategy**
Scores arrive in 3-5 business days. This enables a new strategy: take the SAT in August before senior year, receive scores within a week, and decide immediately whether to register for the October sitting. Under the old SAT, the 3-week wait often closed the retake window.

### SAT vs. ACT — The Diagnostic

| Factor | Favors SAT | Favors ACT |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Pacing** | More time per question. Better for deliberate, careful test-takers. | Less time per question. Better for students who work quickly and confidently. |
| **Science section** | No science section. | ACT Science rewards fast data interpretation and scientific reasoning. Students with strong science backgrounds gain an edge. |
| **Math scope** | Narrower math coverage. More algebra-heavy. | Broader math including more geometry and trigonometry. |
| **Reading style** | Shorter passages with paired evidence questions. More analytical. | Longer passages with faster reading requirements. More stamina-dependent. |
| **Adaptive format** | Yes — adaptive routing creates score ceiling/floor dynamics. | No — linear format, all questions equally weighted. |
| **Superscoring** | Most schools superscore SAT (best R&W + best Math across sittings). | Fewer schools superscore ACT (but many do). |

**The Wayfinder Diagnostic Protocol:**
1. Take a full-length practice SAT AND a full-length practice ACT under timed conditions.
2. Compare composite scores using the College Board concordance table.
3. If scores are within 1-2 composite ACT points of equivalence → default to SAT (wider acceptance, superscoring advantage).
4. If ACT score is meaningfully higher → prep ACT. Some students simply test better on linear formats.
5. If both scores are strong → submit both if they reinforce each other. Submit only the higher if one is significantly better.

## MODULE 3: TEST-OPTIONAL vs. TEST-REQUIRED — THE 2025-2026 REALITY

### The Post-Pandemic Landscape Has Shifted

Test-optional was a COVID emergency measure that became entrenched. BUT: the pendulum is swinging back. Major policy shifts for the 2025-2026 cycle:

**Schools that have RETURNED to test-required:**
- Harvard (Class of 2029+)
- Brown (2024-2025 cycle onward)
- Cornell (Fall 2026 applicants and beyond)
- Yale (reinstated testing requirement)
- Dartmouth (reinstated testing requirement)
- MIT (never went test-optional)
- Georgetown (never went test-optional)
- University of Texas at Austin (Fall 2025+)
- Purdue
- Georgia Tech

**Schools still test-optional (as of 2025-2026, but VERIFY — this changes annually):**
- Columbia, UChicago, Stanford, UPenn (check each cycle)
- Many liberal arts colleges
- UC system (test-FREE — will not consider scores even if submitted)

### The Strategic Decision Matrix

The question is NOT "is the school test-optional?" The question is: **"Does submitting THIS score help THIS applicant at THIS school?"**

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| Score is at or above the school's 75th percentile | **Always submit.** Score is a pure asset. |
| Score is between the school's 50th and 75th percentile | **Submit.** Still above median, net positive. |
| Score is between the school's 25th and 50th percentile | **Context-dependent.** If other parts of the application are strong (spike EC, stellar essays, legacy, recruited athlete), consider submitting. If the application is already borderline, withhold. |
| Score is below the school's 25th percentile | **Do not submit** at test-optional schools. Apply test-optional. |
| School is test-required | **Must submit.** If score is below 25th percentile, reconsider whether this school belongs on the list. |

### The Hidden Test-Optional Penalty

Wayfinder must communicate this reality: test-optional does NOT mean test-blind. At many "test-optional" schools, students who submit strong scores are admitted at significantly higher rates than those who don't submit. The published data from schools like MIT (which released pre/post comparisons) and Dartmouth (whose faculty published research on this) shows that AOs use test scores as a predictive signal of academic readiness.

**The uncomfortable truth:** For most students at competitive schools, "test-optional" functionally means "we won't hold a missing score against you... but we'll give a boost to those who submit a strong one." The strategic read: if a student CAN score at or above the 50th percentile for a target school, they should submit.

**Exception — truly test-blind schools:** The UC system will not consider SAT/ACT scores in any admissions decision. For UC applicants, test prep ROI is zero (unless the student is also applying to private schools or pursuing National Merit).

### The Score Reporting Tactical Playbook

| Tactic | How It Works | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Superscoring** | Submit multiple SAT sittings. School takes highest R&W from any sitting + highest Math from any sitting. | Always submit all sittings if the school superscores. A bad section on one sitting is hidden. |
| **Score Choice** | Some schools allow you to choose which SAT sittings to report. | Use when one sitting has a significantly low section that would drag down the composite even with superscoring. |
| **ACT Superscoring** | Take multiple ACT sittings. Some schools take highest English + Math + Reading + Science across sittings. | Verify school policy — fewer schools superscore ACT than SAT. |
| **Withholding** | At test-optional schools, don't report any score. | When the best available score is below the school's 25th percentile. |

## MODULE 4: THE TESTING TIMELINE — STRATEGIC CALENDAR

| Grade | Month | Action | Strategic Rationale |
|-------|-------|--------|-------------------|
| **Sophomore** | Spring | Take a diagnostic practice SAT AND practice ACT | Determine which test format suits the student. 2 years of runway for prep. |
| **Sophomore** | Summer | Begin PSAT prep if student is within striking distance of state Semifinalist cutoff | PSAT prep is the highest-ROI activity if NM Finalist → full ride is achievable |
| **Junior** | October | PSAT/NMSQT (the one that counts for National Merit) | This is the ONLY sitting that qualifies for NM. No retakes. One shot. |
| **Junior** | March | First SAT sitting | Early enough to retake in May/June if needed |
| **Junior** | May/June | Second SAT sitting (or first ACT) | Aiming for score that meets or exceeds target school 50th percentile |
| **Junior** | Summer | Score review + retake decision | If not at target, plan August sitting. If at target, stop testing — diminishing returns. |
| **Senior** | August | Final SAT/ACT sitting if needed | Last realistic test date before EA/ED deadlines |
| **Senior** | September | NM Semifinalist notifications released | If qualified, immediately research NM full-ride schools and first-choice designation deadlines |
| **Senior** | October | Last ACT sitting for regular decision | Only if needed. Most students should be done testing by August. |

### The "Stop Testing" Rule

Diminishing returns set in FAST. After 2-3 sittings, score gains plateau. The time spent prepping for a 4th SAT is better spent on essays, extracurriculars, or school-specific research. Wayfinder's hard rule: **Maximum 3 SAT sittings or 2 ACT sittings unless there's a documented reason for continued improvement** (e.g., test anxiety that's being clinically addressed, a clear content gap that's been filled with tutoring).

## MODULE 5: THE ROI FRAMEWORK — TEST PREP AS FINANCIAL INVESTMENT

### Prep Method ROI Analysis

| Method | Cost | Expected Score Gain | ROI Signal |
|--------|------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Self-study (Khan Academy/free resources)** | $0 | 30-60 points (SAT) / 1-2 points (ACT) | Best ROI for motivated, self-directed students. Use this FIRST. |
| **Group course (Kaplan, Princeton Review, etc.)** | $800-$2,000 | 40-80 points (SAT) / 1-3 points (ACT) | Moderate ROI. Better for students who need structure and accountability. |
| **Private tutoring (qualified)** | $2,000-$10,000 | 80-150 points (SAT) / 2-5 points (ACT) | Good ROI if targeted at specific weaknesses. Bad ROI if used as a blanket approach. |
| **Elite private tutoring (specialists)** | $10,000-$30,000 | 100-200 points (SAT) / 3-6 points (ACT) | Justified ONLY if the score improvement unlocks a specific financial outcome (NM Finalist → full ride, or score needed for merit scholarship threshold at target school). |

### The Wayfinder Test Prep Decision

Every test prep dollar should be evaluated against this question: **"What specific financial or admissions outcome does a higher score unlock?"**

- If a 50-point SAT improvement pushes a student from Commended to Semifinalist in their state → that 50 points is worth $100K-$200K in NM scholarships. Invest heavily in prep.
- If a 50-point SAT improvement moves a student from 1450 to 1500 at a school where they're already above the 75th percentile → that improvement has near-zero marginal value. Don't spend more on prep.
- If a student is test-optional everywhere they're applying and their best score is below the 25th percentile → test prep ROI is negative. Invest that time in essays and ECs instead.

This is the Wayfinder calculus: every strategic decision maps to a measurable outcome.
