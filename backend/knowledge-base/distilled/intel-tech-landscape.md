**Intelligence Document | Wayfinder Career Advisory**

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The technology career landscape has undergone fundamental restructuring. The post-2023 layoff era revealed that Big Tech prestige masks systemic culture problems, while mid-market and startup opportunities offer better quality-of-life trade-offs. Entry-level hiring has contracted sharply. Remote work remains fragmented by company. Geographic arbitrage is dead for pure engineering but alive in geographic hubs. AI/ML hype obscures genuine demand. This document reflects what technologists are actually experiencing, not what job postings promise.

**Core Reality:** The tech career is no longer a linear prestige climb. It's a portfolio play across company types, geographies, and roles.

## 1. POST-LAYOFF REALITY: WHAT TECH HIRING ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE NOW

### The Numbers
- **2023-2024:** 262,000+ tech layoffs across major companies (Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, etc.)
- **2025-2026:** Hiring frozen at Big Tech; selective hiring for senior roles and AI specialists only
- **Real hiring:** Happening in fintech, government tech, healthcare IT, and mid-market SaaS
- **Time-to-hire:** 4-8 weeks for mid-market vs 12-16 weeks for Big Tech; 2-4 weeks for startups

### What Changed
The layoff era destroyed the mythology. Experienced engineers discovered:

1. **"We're building products that ship every 2 weeks at my startup vs. waiting 6 months for approval at Google"** — Process burden at scale is real and suffocating
2. **Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs) are now standard layoff machinery**, not rehabilitation. Being on a PIP = plan your exit
3. **Return-to-office mandates are cultural punishment**, not productivity optimization. Companies using RTO as attrition tool for junior staff
4. **Tenure != job security.** 10-year employees laid off with 2 weeks' severance alongside 2-year employees

### Hiring Patterns by Employer Type

| Employer Type | Hiring Volume | Competition | Culture Risk | Compensation Trend |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Big Tech (FAANG) | Low (senior only) | Extreme | High | Flat/declining |
| Established SaaS | Moderate | High | Moderate | Slight increases |
| Startups (funded) | Moderate-High | Moderate | Variable | Above-market for equity believers |
| Enterprise | Moderate | Low-Moderate | Moderate | Stable, sometimes low |
| Government/Civic Tech | Increasing | Very Low | Better than tech | Below-market + benefits |

**Real feedback:** "After 4 years at Google, did some of the worst work of my career. Now I ship features faster in a 30-person startup than I ever did in 5,000 person company."

## 2. NEW TECH CAREER TIERS: COMPANY TYPE MATTERS MORE THAN TITLE

The prestige ladder (Google > Facebook > Amazon > Microsoft > startups) is defunct. Company type now determines lifestyle, growth, and actual work:

### TIER 1: Big Tech (Google, Meta, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft)

**Reality:**
- "Big Tech engineering culture has gotten significantly worse in the last 3 years"
- Process-heavy, slow shipping, constant reorganizations
- PIP culture creates paranoia; tenure-based bonuses become golden handcuffs
- Return-to-office mandates in effect (Meta, Google, Amazon)
- Prestige remains but attracts people seeking stability over impact

**Career Fit:**
- Best for: Mid-career engineers (8-15 YOE) using it as platform credentialing
- Stock compensation still strong but cliff-vesting creates retention lock
- "Prestige trap": Staying 3-4 years damages career flexibility
- Brand value: Real internationally, less relevant with each RTO mandate

**Salary:** $180K-280K base + 30-50% bonus + equity for mid-career IC4-IC5; growth limited

### TIER 2: Established SaaS & Unicorns (Stripe, Figma, Notion, etc.)

**Reality:**
- 40-400 person range where impact is still visible
- Shipping speed and autonomy are real; decision-making velocity is 10x Big Tech
- Equity still meaningful if company is on growth trajectory
- Flatter hierarchies mean faster feedback loops but also less mentorship structure
- Many now offer stock buyback programs (real $)

**Career Fit:**
- Best for: Early-to-mid career engineers (3-8 YOE) wanting to build skills rapidly
- Growth trajectory is faster and more visible
- Burnout risk is real if startup is growth-stage (Series B-D)
- Network density high; talent clustering is significant advantage

**Salary:** $160K-240K base + 20-40% bonus + equity that may vest into real value in 3-5 years

### TIER 3: Mid-Market Enterprise & Fortune 500 Tech Divisions

**Reality:**
- IBM, Cisco, Oracle tech divisions, enterprise SaaS (Salesforce, SAP, Workday)
- Process exists but slower churn of personnel
- Job security genuine; PIP culture is rarer
- Compensation structured (pension, 401k, healthcare) rather than speculative
- Remote-friendly; actually enforce it
- Advancement requires internal politics; harder to jump 2 levels quickly

**Career Fit:**
- Best for: Engineers seeking stability, parents, people with non-work obligations
- Salary grows with tenure; burnout is slow-motion, not acute
- Network benefits are internal mostly; external credibility depends on specific company
- Good intermediate landing spot between startup and retirement

**Salary:** $140K-200K base + 15-25% bonus; stock programs vary; benefits are actual value

### TIER 4: Startups (Seed-Series B)

**Reality:**
- "That startup failure taught me more than 3 years at Google"
- High autonomy, low process, rapid iteration, high failure risk
- Equity lottery: 0.1-2% cap table stakes; outcomes are binary
- Burnout and founder-driven culture can be brutal
- Hiring standards variable; experience requirements inflated

**Career Fit:**
- Best for: Engineers 2-10 YOE seeking portfolio acceleration or taking flyer on upside
- Growth is accelerated and visible; also means burnout is acute
- Resume value is binary: "started-up at Series A" is valuable; "failed startup" is neutral
- Founder relationship critical—toxic founder = toxic experience

**Salary:** $110K-180K base + 0.1-2% equity (highly variable); no bonus structure; high variance in compensation transparency

### TIER 5: Government, Civic Tech & Non-Profit Tech

**Reality:**
- U.S. Digital Service, 18F, various state/federal tech initiatives
- Salary is 60-70% of private sector but job security is absolute
- No RTO mandates; actual work-life balance policies that are enforced
- Hiring is slow but competition is low
- Work on public infrastructure vs. chasing quarterly growth

**Career Fit:**
- Best for: Late-career engineers, parents, people tired of startup treadmill, specialists
- Hiring: No equity stress; no layoffs ever; 0% age discrimination (federal hiring is protected)
- Network: Smaller but higher-quality; many government tech folks transition to policy/VC
- Resume credibility: Increases with each year; institutional trust is real

**Salary:** $100K-160K base; benefits are comprehensive; no bonus culture; job security is lifetime

## 3. HOT VS OVERHYPED ROLES: THE REALITY CHECK

### GENUINELY HOT (Real Demand, Real Shortages)

**Platform Engineering**
- Every mid+ company needs it; job market is undersupplied
- Skills: Kubernetes, CI/CD, internal tooling, developer experience
- Salary: $170K-220K mid-career; shortage is acute
- Why: Companies finally realizing DevOps isn't scalable; need dedicated team
- Career value: High—skills are transferable across companies

**Security Engineering**
- Compliance requirements driving genuine hiring
- Skills: Zero-trust architecture, cloud security, threat modeling
- Salary: $180K-240K; only growing
- Why: Enterprise buying cloud; need people who understand risk
- Career value: Very high; specialized skill is recession-proof

**Data Engineering** (not data science—different field)
- Data pipelines are now critical infrastructure
- Skills: Spark, Airflow, dbt, data warehousing (Snowflake, BigQuery)
- Salary: $160K-220K; very strong market
- Why: Every company has data but few have infrastructure to handle it
- Career value: High; direct path to staff engineer

**DevRel/Developer Advocacy** (Non-engineering income path)
- Stripe, Vercel, Figma, etc. are hiring aggressively
- Skills: Technical writing, community building, speaking, influence
- Salary: $140K-190K + travel budget
- Why: Infrastructure companies need marketing through technical credibility
- Career value: High if you want to move toward product/strategy

### OVERHYPED (Hype >> Reality)

**AI/ML Engineering (General)**
- Headline: "Everyone hiring AI engineers"
- Reality: Only hiring for specific niches (applied ML in production, LLMs, CV)
- Real demand: 20% of "AI engineer" job postings; 80% are just "write Python and sometimes use scikit-learn"
- Salary: Inflated for phds (250K+) but median is $160K-190K for engineers without research background
- Career trap: ML engineer who only writes .ipynb notebooks in Jupyter has limited mobility
- Brutal truth: "Working as SWE but don't know how to code due to reliance on AI tools" is real and it tanks your growth

**AI/ML Actually Worth Building Career Around:**
- Production ML (models in serving infrastructure)
- LLM fine-tuning and RAG systems (real but saturated)
- Computer vision (specific use case; market is bounded)
- Researcher-level work (need PhD; 5% of market)

**Product Management (From Engineering)**
- Headline: "Engineers transition to PM for 20% raise"
- Reality: Requires relationships + some will fail; oversaturated for junior roles
- Salary: Yes, PM makes more ($150K-200K vs $140K engineer at same company) but requires politics
- Career trap: Failed PM transition locks you out of senior engineer track
- Real advice: Don't transition before 8 YOE; know your company's PM credibility

**Data Science (Not Data Engineering)**
- Headline: "Hot field, shortage of talent"
- Reality: Most data science roles pay $130K-170K; heavily competing with new grads
- Market saturation: Bootcamp boom + PhD inflation = soft hiring market
- Career reality: Hot in 2017, competitive in 2026, cooling in 2027
- Recommend: Specialize in specific vertical (finance, healthcare) rather than general DS

## 4. JUNIOR DEVELOPER SQUEEZE: HOW HARD IS ENTRY-LEVEL REALLY?

### The Brutal Truth

"Junior devs not interested in software engineering" — hiring managers' refrain across the industry. Entry-level has contracted sharply:

**Hiring Volume Drop:**
- 2021: Entry-level positions were 20-25% of job market
- 2026: Entry-level positions are 5-8% of job market
- Junior developer salary: Down 5-15% vs 2021 peaks

### Real Entry-Level Numbers (March 2026)

| Education Track | Entry Salary | Realistic Range | Hiring Volume |
|---|---|---|---|
| CS degree (target school) | $75-85K | $70-90K | Decreasing |
| CS degree (state school) | $65-75K | $60-80K | Soft |
| Bootcamp grad | $65-85K (not $120K) | $55-80K | Very soft |
| Self-taught + portfolio | $60-75K | $55-70K | Extremely rare |
| Post-degree internship | $90-110K | $85-115K | High demand |

### Why It Got Harder

1. **AI tools remove junior scaffolding.** Juniors used to learn by fixing bugs and writing CRUD; AI removes the learning path
2. **Companies realized they don't need junior devs.** Mid-level engineers + AI tools > juniors + onboarding overhead
3. **Bootcamp saturation:** Too many bootcamp graduates relative to entry positions; signal degraded
4. **Seniority creep:** "Junior" roles now require 2-3 YOE; true entry-level is rare

### What Actually Works for Entry-Level (2026)

**Strategy 1: Extended Internship Path (Most Reliable)**
- Do 2-3 internships during school ($20-30/hr, or unpaid for prestige)
- Return offer from internship is 80%+ probability if you perform
- This path: 3-4 years total, $80-100K starting salary
- Hardest part: Getting first internship (competition is high; need networks or luck)

**Strategy 2: Geographic Arbitrage for Entry**
- Government tech hiring: No experience required, $65-85K starting
- Civic tech startups: Hiring more junior roles than private sector
- Enterprise IT roles: Fortune 500 often have junior programs; $60-75K
- Advantage: Job security, lower competition, less prestige stigma

**Strategy 3: Bootcamp + Geographic Arbitrage**
- Bootcamp (18 weeks): $15-25K cost, $65-80K entry salary, 6-8 months to job
- Choose bootcamp carefully: Hiring partnerships matter; "outcomes" marketing is often inflated
- Realistic result: 55-65% placement rate within 6 months; 70% within 12 months
- Geographic play: Bootcamp grad in Denver/Austin gets hired faster than San Francisco

**Strategy 4: Non-Traditional Entry (Backend of Tech)**
- Start as Data Center Technician, IT Operations, QA
- Entry point: $50-65K, zero experience required
- Transition path: Learn on the job, move to engineering in 2-3 years
- Real example: 0 YOE Data Center Technician at Amazon, 2 months post-graduation; promoted to SRE role after 18 months

### The Honest Assessment

Entry-level is harder. Bootcamp marketing about $120K starting salaries is dishonest. Real numbers: $60-85K range. Expect 3-4 month job search post-bootcamp. Geographic arbitrage (not San Francisco) increases odds 2-3x. Government and civic tech are less competitive paths. AI tools have eliminated the "learning on the job" ladder that existed for previous generations.

## 5. REMOTE WORK REALITY: RTO MANDATES ARE REAL AND STRATEGIC

### The Actual Landscape (March 2026)

| Company Type | RTO Stance | Salary Impact | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Big Tech (Google, Meta, Amazon) | 3-5 days/week mandatory | -5-10% for remote | Used as attrition tool |
| Established SaaS | 1-3 days/week hybrid | -0-5% | Varies by team |
| Stripe, Figma, Notion | Flexible/remote-first | 0% differential | Still recruiting advantage |
| Enterprise | 2-3 days/week | 0% differential | Actually enforced |
| Startups | Flexible/remote-first | 0% differential | Often fully distributed |
| Government | 0-2 days/week | 0% differential | Actually flexible |

### What Happened to Remote Work

2020-2021: "Remote forever"
2022-2023: "Hybrid is the future"
2024-2025: "RTO mandates are attrition strategy"
2026: Reality = company-dependent; no universal trend

### Why RTO Mandates Happened

- **Culture narrative:** Executives claim office = culture (unproven)
- **Real reason:** Office = easy attrition for underperformers and junior staff
- **Observable fact:** Companies that mandate RTO see higher junior departures, not higher productivity
- **Salary reality:** Remote role salary = local cost of living; SF remote = SF salary; not arbitrage anymore

### Geographic Arbitrage is DEAD for Pure Engineering

**Previous play (2021):** Live in Kansas City, make San Francisco salary ($200K), live like a king
**2026 reality:** "Remote for San Francisco" jobs now specify "Bay Area salary band"; companies equalized across locations

**Geographic Arbitrage is ALIVE in:**
- Government tech: Full remote, federal salary (e.g., $130K anywhere in US)
- Smaller startups: Still pay market rate regardless of location
- European companies hiring US: Sometimes don't adjust for location

**Best geographic plays now:**
- Live in mid-cost city (Austin, Denver, Nashville): Recruit for US companies paying "national average"
- Live in low-cost country (Portugal, Mexico): Work for US company, massive arbitrage
- Live in high-cost city: Accept lower salary but stock options and proximity benefits

## 6. STARTUP EQUITY LOTTERY: WHEN IS IT ACTUALLY WORTH SOMETHING?

### The Math (Harsh Reality)

**Equity offer: 0.1% at Series A**

| Scenario | Probability | Outcome | Your $200K salary + equity = |
|---|---|---|---|
| IPO (exits $2B+) | 0.5% | Yes, vest to $400K+ | $200K + $200K-800K gain |
| Acquihire (exits $300M-2B) | 5% | Maybe, depends on acquirer | $200K + $20K-100K gain |
| Down round/stuck | 60% | Equity underwater | $200K (no equity gain) |
| Complete failure | 35% | $0 | $200K only |

**Expected value of 0.1% equity at Series A startup:** ~$8-15K over 4 years. Not nothing, but not life-changing.

### When Equity Actually Matters

**Scenario 1: Series A, you're one of 20 employees (1%+ equity)**
- If company gets to $1B: Your equity = $10M+
- If company down-round: Your equity = $0
- Real outcome: Only 1-2 employees in the cohort see this
- Probability: <1%

**Scenario 2: Series B/C employee at later stage (0.1-0.3%)**
- More stable trajectory; less upside; equity might be $100K-500K
- Better risk/reward; company already proved product-market fit
- More realistic: 5-10% chance of meaningful equity outcome

**Scenario 3: Early-stage fintech/deeptech with defensible IP**
- Equity *might* be worth something if company has real tech moat
- Example: ML infrastructure company might have better odds than another SaaS
- Probability: Still <5%, but not 0%

### The Honest Framing

**Don't take a startup role for equity.** Take it for:
- Resume acceleration (Series A at Figma > 5 years at Big Tech, for some roles)
- Skill building (autonomy, shipping, ownership)
- Network (founder, investor, other early employees)

**Treat equity as:** Nice-to-have windfall, not compensation plan. If you're betting your financial future on startup equity, you've already lost.

**Real math:** Startup equity package is salary reduction tax on the 95% of startups that fail or get acquired for acquihire terms. Only take if startup mission is genuinely interesting independent of equity outcome.

## 7. NON-ENGINEERING ROLES THAT PAY WELL: ALTERNATIVE PATHS

### Solutions Architect

**What it is:** Bridge between sales and engineering; customize product for enterprise customers

**Salary:** $160K-220K base + 20-40% bonus = $200K-280K total comp

**Career path:** 5+ YOE engineer → Sales Engineer → Solutions Architect (or direct if you had customer interaction)

**Pros:**
- 30-40% higher compensation than ICs
- Less coding, more architecture and problem-solving
- Significant bonus/commission opportunity
- Travel (con or pro depending on preference)

**Cons:**
- Heavy customer interaction (introverts beware)
- Deal cycles create unpredictable pressure
- Reboarding to IC engineer later is difficult

**Real feedback:** "Best thing I did was move to SA after 8 years of IC engineering. Made $100K more year 1, haven't coded in production since, don't regret it."

### Developer Relations / Technical Advocacy

**What it is:** Marketing, community building, and technical credibility for developer tools (APIs, libraries, platforms)

**Salary:** $140K-190K base + benefits (usually no bonus)

**Career path:** IC engineer (backend/full-stack) with strong communication → DevRel

**Pros:**
- Leverage technical credibility + personality
- Speaking at conferences, community building, influence
- Often fully remote; travel is discretionary
- Startup/scale-up DevRel roles offer equity upside
- Shorter meetings, more autonomy than sales roles

**Cons:**
- Income is less than IC/SA but more than pure engineer salary
- Transition back to IC is possible but slower
- Success is harder to quantify; metrics-driven orgs struggle with it
- Can feel like "sales adjacent" if you're engineering-minded

**Hot companies for DevRel:** Stripe, Vercel, Figma, Supabase, CloudFlare, Slack

### Technical Program Manager (TPM)

**What it is:** Project management + technical depth; owns complex cross-functional initiatives

**Salary:** $150K-210K base + 15-25% bonus = $175K-260K total comp

**Career path:** 5+ YOE engineer with process orientation → TPM (or IC4+)

**Pros:**
- High impact across org; visibility with leadership
- 20-30% higher comp than IC at same level
- Path to product, strategy, operations
- Less coding stress; more systems thinking

**Cons:**
- Heavy meeting load; some find it draining
- Success depends on org maturity; dysfunctional orgs = hard role
- Return-to-IC is possible but harder after 3+ years
- Metrics and accountability are more rigid than IC roles

**Realistic assessment:** TPM is management-track adjacent; best for people who enjoy process, systems thinking, and influence without direct people management.

### Sales Engineering

**What it is:** IC engineer who sells; does technical demos, POCs, customer engineering

**Salary:** $120K-160K base + 30-100% commission = $150K-300K+ (highly variable)

**Career path:** Backend/platform engineer with customer interest → Sales Engineer

**Pros:**
- Compensation ceiling is highest of non-manager tracks
- Autonomy in customer relationships
- Deal-closing satisfaction; tangible outcomes
- Often leading to Account Executive (sales) if interested

**Cons:**
- Income highly variable (boom/bust with sales cycles)
- Requires thick skin; customer interaction is high-stress
- Competition is external (other companies); internal (other SEs)
- Hard to code-switch back to pure IC

**Real comp:** Experienced SE at enterprise SaaS: $150K base + $100K+ commission = $250K+ in good quarters. But variable.

### Technical Writer

**What it is:** Documentation, API guides, technical tutorials; credibility-building content

**Salary:** $110K-160K (lower than IC but stable)

**Career path:** IC engineer → Tech Writer (career pivot)

**Pros:**
- Work-life balance better than IC roles
- Fully remote often available
- Lower stress; documentation has deadlines, not on-call
- Growing field; demand increasing

**Cons:**
- Lowest comp of these alternative roles
- Some orgs don't value docs (bad sign of company maturity)
- Career mobility limited; hard to transition back to IC or to other domains

## 8. GEOGRAPHIC ARBITRAGE: BEST OPPORTUNITIES BY CAREER STAGE

### JUNIOR (0-3 YOE)

**Best Markets:**
1. **Austin, TX** — Lower cost of living; mid-market hiring; no state income tax
2. **Denver, CO** — Tech startup growth; lower salary expectations; outdoor lifestyle
3. **Raleigh, NC** — Biotech + software convergence; lower comp but robust market
4. **Remote for government** — Federal tech roles pay $80-110K; full remote; anywhere in US

**Strategy:**
- Bootcamp in Austin/Denver likely = $70-85K offer in market
- Government entry: $75-90K fully remote, job-for-life security
- Avoid: San Francisco ($90-120K but cost of living eats gains)

### MID-CAREER (3-8 YOE)

**Best Markets:**
1. **Remote for Bay Area company** — Negotiate salary at US level even if living in Nashville
2. **Seattle, WA** — Tech hub + less insane than SF; $140K-180K realistic range
3. **New York, NY** — Finance + SaaS hybrid; $150K-200K; cost of living is high but opportunity density is real
4. **International arbitrage** — Lisbon/Mexico City, remote for US company: $140K salary = 2x local cost of living

**Strategy:**
- Negotiate remote at hiring time; lock in US salary
- Don't list location as "Denver"; list as "US" or "Pacific Time"
- International play: 1-2 year visa, build savings, return with network

### SENIOR (8+ YOE)

**Best Markets:**
1. **Anywhere remote + equity upside** — Startup options; location becomes irrelevant
2. **San Francisco/Bay Area** — If targeting venture scale-up or BigTech; salary is actually justified by opportunity
3. **New York** — Finance + fintech, higher ceiling than SF for certain specialties
4. **International senior hire** — EU base (Berlin, London) with US company: $160K+ salary, much cheaper cost of living

**Strategy:**
- Focus on equity upside and opportunity (not cost of living)
- Remote negotiation is easier at senior level
- If considering return-to-office, geographic premium is worth it only if equity/growth is real

### Special Case: Government Tech

**Best for all career stages:**
- Any level: $70-155K salary depending on role/level
- Fully remote, anywhere in US
- Zero age discrimination
- Pension + healthcare are genuine value
- Job security is absolute
- Work is meaningful but bureaucratic

**Example:** SWE hiring for 18F (US Digital Service): GS-13 level = $115-130K fully remote, entry or mid-career. No layoff risk ever.

## FINAL REALITY CHECK: WHAT CHANGED IN 3 YEARS

### What Actually Got Better
- **Mid-market options are genuinely good now.** Stripe, Figma, Notion level—real autonomy, real impact, real compensation
- **Government tech is finally competitive.** Not a sacrifice anymore; legitimately good
- **Remote work is real option** (though RTO is pushback)
- **Equity transparency improving.** More companies sharing cap tables; founders talking about realistic outcomes

### What Got Worse
- **Entry-level hiring collapsed.** Bootcamp grads hit reality wall
- **Big Tech culture degraded.** Not recovering; prestige trap is real
- **Burnout is acute.** Startup hiring standards increased but comp didn't
- **AI created new gap:** People who use AI to write code → skill stagnation
- **Age discrimination is real.** 50+ year-old ex-Googler takes 2 months to land role; same candidate 15 years ago: 1 week

### What Stayed the Same
- **Network = everything.** Referrals convert at 40-60%; cold applications at 2-5%
- **Location still matters.** But it's about time zones and visa status, not necessity of presence
- **Comp is sticky.** If you switched jobs, you might be 15% behind market if you stayed
- **Stock market = career anxiety.** Bad market = slower hiring; good market = hiring spree (see 2021)

## CAREER DECISIONS FRAMEWORK (2026)

**Are you choosing Big Tech?**
- Only if: You're mid-career and want 2-4 year credentialing sprint before moving to somewhere better
- Not if: You care about work-life balance, shipping speed, or long-term happiness

**Are you choosing startup equity?**
- Only if: You care more about learning and impact than compensation certainty
- Not if: You're betting your financial future on exit upside

**Are you choosing bootcamp entry?**
- Only if: You're committed to geographic arbitrage (not SF) and willing to grind 3-6 month job search
- Not if: You have $100K in student debt already; ROI is only positive in low-cost markets

**Are you choosing government/civic tech?**
- Always, if you're 50+ years old, have dependents, or need job security
- Consider if: You want meaningful work with zero layoff risk; trades salary floor for salary ceiling

**Are you choosing remote work?**
- Negotiate remote at hiring time; asking later is harder
- Expect -5% salary if requiring full remote in HCOL area (SF, NYC)
- Know company's RTO mandates before accepting (they'll change, always towards more office)
