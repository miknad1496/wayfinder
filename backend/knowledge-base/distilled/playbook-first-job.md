# FIRST JOB PLAYBOOK: THE FIRST 90 DAYS AND BEYOND

## Introduction

Your first job is not about perfection—it's about building a foundation while you learn the unwritten rules of professional work. This playbook is based on hard-won lessons from thousands of professionals who've navigated this transition successfully. The stakes matter: how you spend your first 1.5-2 years will shape your trajectory, salary growth, and industry reputation for years to come.

---

## 1. BEFORE DAY 1: Preparation and Logistics

### Week 1: Administrative Setup
- Confirm start date, time, location, and who to contact on arrival
- Arrange childcare, transportation, or any logistics you'll need
- Set up home office basics (desk, chair, quiet space) if hybrid/remote
- Review benefits enrollment materials and health insurance options
- Create a simple expense tracking system for relocation (if applicable)

### Week 2-3: Research Phase
- Read your company's latest earnings report or press release
- Review the company's LinkedIn company page—understand recent news, employee count, growth trajectory
- Look up your team on LinkedIn—understand reporting structure and backgrounds
- Google "[Your Company] culture" and "[Industry] trends"—build a mental map
- Find your employee handbook digitally (HR usually sends this)

### Week 4: Mental Preparation
- Accept that you will feel lost for 2-3 months. This is normal.
- Plan to ask many questions. People expect this from new grads.
- Prepare your "tell me about yourself" story (30-60 seconds, focused on this role)
- Identify 2-3 specific skills you want to develop in year one
- Set a realistic expectation: your first job is about learning more than earning

### Practical Checklist
- Confirm address where you'll work
- Arrange parking/transit
- Set alarm for first day
- Prepare 3-5 neutral business outfit options
- Pack small notebook and pen for first week

## 2. FIRST 30 DAYS: Learning Mode

### Days 1-5: Onboarding and Observation

**Your job is to listen and absorb, not to contribute ideas yet.**

- Take detailed notes on systems, processes, and who does what
- When given instructions, write them down—even if you think you'll remember
- Say "I'm taking notes so I get this right" (people like this)
- Learn the informal hierarchy: who actually decides things? Who do people go to for advice?
- Attend all scheduled meetings; don't skip even if "not mandatory"
- Map the physical/digital office: where is everything?

**Red flags to notice (save these observations for later):**
- Are people stressed, relaxed, or somewhere between?
- Do people collaborate or work in silos?
- Is there visible diversity in the office?
- Do people stay late? Is it required or optional?
- What's the energy level around projects and meetings?

### Days 5-15: Relationship Building

**Schedule 1-on-1 coffee/lunch with each teammate (5-10 minutes, informal).**

- Ask: "What do you work on?" "What's been on your plate lately?" "What do you like about working here?"
- Take notes on names, roles, and one thing you learned
- You're building relationships and learning your domain simultaneously
- If you're remote, do Zoom coffee chats

**With your manager (weekly 1-on-1, should be scheduled already):**
- Clarify priorities for your first month
- Ask about communication preferences (Slack? Email? Face-to-face?)
- Understand how they like to receive updates
- Ask: "What does success look like for me in 30 days?"

### Days 15-30: Active Learning

**Start your assigned work, but focus on understanding first:**

- Ask "why" at least 5 times per task: Why this approach? Why now? Why this metric?
- Document processes as you learn them (you'll be the "expert" on what you've written)
- Start a glossary of company jargon and acronyms
- Identify the 3-5 systems you need to master (project management, expense software, communication tools)
- Notice the unwritten rules: How do people communicate? How fast do people respond to messages? What's the dress code reality?

**Critical reminder:** You're being watched in month one, but people are rooting for you. Everyone was new once. Mistakes now are forgiven; arrogance is not.

## 3. DAYS 30-90: Adding Value and Building Credibility

### Week 5-6: Quick Wins Strategy

By now you understand the basic lay of the land. Time to shift from "learning mode" to "contributing mode."

**Identify 2-3 quick wins:**
- Find something that's broken, inefficient, or outdated
- Ask your manager: "I noticed [X]. Could I take a crack at fixing/improving it?"
- Quick wins are not career-changing—they're confidence builders for you and your team
- Examples: fixing a spreadsheet template, cleaning up shared documentation, streamlining a small process

**The psychology:** Quick wins signal you're not just a taker of tasks, you're thinking about the team.

### Week 7-12: Meaningful Contribution

- Your manager should assign you a small project with clear success metrics
- Track your contributions: Write them down with dates and impact
- Start participating in meetings with thoughtful questions (not just staying silent)
- Volunteer for something slightly outside your comfort zone (shows growth mindset)
- Build expertise in one niche area—become "the person who knows about X"

**Feedback checkpoint (around day 60):**
- Request informal feedback from your manager: "How am I doing? What should I focus on?"
- Don't wait for formal review to get a temperature check
- Adjust course early if you're off track

### Career Capital Building (Days 30-90)

- Learn 1-2 key skills your role requires (don't just learn on the job, get formal if possible)
- Attend company events, workshops, or training
- Contribute to team Slack channels or meetings (show you're engaged)
- Build relationships with people in adjacent teams (you'll work with them)

## 4. THE MANAGER RELATIONSHIP: Managing Up

### What Managers Actually Care About

Your manager wants three things from you:

1. **Reliability**: You do what you said you'd do, on time, with quality
2. **Communication**: You tell them bad news early, celebrate wins, and keep them in the loop
3. **Growth**: You're not stagnant; you're learning and improving

### Managing Up Without Brown-Nosing

- Meet your 1-on-1 schedule religiously (don't skip these)
- Come with a brief update: "Here's what I accomplished, here's what I'm working on, here's where I need help"
- Share wins and challenges without making excuses
- Ask for feedback specifically: "What should I be doing differently?" beats "How am I doing?"
- Notice what your manager optimizes for (speed? Accuracy? Collaboration?) and lean into it

### Getting Feedback That Matters

- Feedback is not criticism—it's a gift that helps you improve
- Ask for feedback at the moment, not three months later: "How did I handle that?"
- If you mess up, own it: "I made a mistake here. I'll fix it and prevent it next time. What's your advice?"
- Managers respect people who take feedback and actually change

### Red Flag: Your Manager Isn't Investing in You

If after 60 days your manager hasn't given you clear expectations, feedback, or growth direction—this is a yellow flag. Address it: "I'd like to understand what success looks like for my role in six months. Can we talk about that?"

## 5. OFFICE POLITICS 101: Navigating Without Being Political

### The Reality

Office politics isn't evil—it's just how humans work together. You can't opt out. But you don't have to be manipulative.

### Alliances That Matter

**Your manager:** Your most critical relationship. Invest here first.

**Your peers:** These people will be your allies for years. Be generous with your time and knowledge.

**One person in another department:** Find someone outside your team who's willing to explain "how things really work." Grab lunch.

**Allies are built on trust, not drama.** Share information, help without keeping score, and stay out of gossip.

### The Politics You Actually Need to Know

- **Information flow matters:** Know who talks to whom. Don't tell Person A something you don't want Person B to know.
- **Credit and blame:** Give credit generously. Take responsibility for mistakes. Never throw teammates under the bus.
- **Power dynamics:** Some people have more influence than their title suggests. Figure out who actually decides things.
- **Avoid the middle of conflicts:** If there's tension between departments or people, stay neutral until you understand the history.

### What Gets People Fired (Avoid These)

- Dishonesty or cutting corners
- Being the person who shares confidential information
- Throwing others under the bus in front of leadership
- Being difficult to work with (doesn't matter how smart you are)
- Ignoring feedback or being defensive

## 6. THE PERFORMANCE REVIEW GAME: Your First Review

### How Reviews Actually Work

Most companies do reviews 3-6 months in. Your first review is usually gentler than later ones, but it matters. Here's why: it creates a record.

### What Reviewers Are Looking For

- **Adaptability:** You were new; how did you learn?
- **Reliability:** Did you deliver what was asked of you?
- **Attitude:** Are you coachable? Do you ask for help?
- **Growth:** Can people see you improving in real time?

**Reality check:** Your first review is rarely a "promotion and raise" moment. The goal is "met expectations" or better. That's a win.

### How to Set Yourself Up for a Strong Review

**Document your work:**
- Keep a "wins list" with dates and impact
- Note skills you developed
- Track feedback you received and improvements you made
- Save emails where you solved a problem or saved time

**Narrative matters:**
- Come to the review conversation with your own reflection
- Be honest about what you struggled with (and what you learned)
- Show trajectory: "I struggled with [X] in month one, but by month two I could [Y]"
- Connect your work to company values or goals

**Before the review meeting:**
- Ask your manager for their thoughts informally: "What should I emphasize in our review conversation?"
- Know your own compensation: Are you being paid at market? (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi)
- Understand: Your first review rarely brings significant raises. That's normal.

### After the Review

- Ask specifically: "What should I focus on in the next 6 months to grow?"
- Don't negotiate hard on year-one raises; focus on setting up strong trajectory
- But do note if you're significantly underpaid vs. market (factor this into future decisions)

## 7. WHEN TO LOOK AT "WHAT'S NEXT": The Tenure Question

### The Research Consensus: 1.5-2 Years is the Sweet Spot

You'll see stories of people job-hopping every year for big raises. That works—but it comes with costs (reputation, trust, learning depth). The data suggests:

- **Minimum tenure:** 1.5 years. Leaving before this raises questions in interviews.
- **Ideal for leverage:** 1.5-2 years. You have proof of contribution, new skills, and momentum.
- **Salary growth reality:** Internal raises average 3-5%. Job switches average 10-20%.
- **Exception:** If you're in a toxic environment or being treated unfairly, don't stay for the sake of tenure.

### Red Flags: When to Start Looking (Even Before 1.5 Years)

- No growth opportunity (you're doing identical work month 3 as month 1)
- You're getting negative feedback with no support to improve
- Massive restructuring suggests layoffs are coming
- Your manager is looking for a new job (sign they know something)
- You're being asked to work in ways that violate your values
- Pay is significantly below market for your role/location

### Green Flags: Reasons to Stay Past 2 Years

- You're learning at a rapid pace
- You have a clear path to the next role
- The mission excites you
- You're building something meaningful
- Your manager is invested in your growth

### The Strategic Move (When You're Ready)

If you're going to leave:
- **Timing:** Don't leave before your first review. Leave after it or during a natural project breakpoint.
- **The conversation:** Tell your manager privately before telling anyone else
- **Notice period:** Two weeks is standard; respect it
- **The reference:** Your first manager will be called for every job going forward. Leave professionally.

## 8. REMOTE AND HYBRID: Succeeding Without Physical Presence

### Remote Specific Challenges

- People bond in person; you're missing casual relationship-building time
- You're less visible; you have to communicate more
- Time zone confusion can slow things down
- Onboarding is harder; you don't have casual hallway learning

### Your Remote/Hybrid Advantage

- **Overcommunicate:**document decisions, progress, and context in writing
- **Calendar visibility:** Block time for deep work; make your availability clear
- **Same-time meetings matter:** Attend them even if optional (builds relationships)
- **Async communication:** Write clear, detailed updates so people understand your work
- **Video on:** In the first 6 months, keep video on during calls (builds rapport)

### What Doesn't Change

- Your manager still needs reliability and clear communication
- Office politics still exist; they happen in Slack and email instead
- Relationships are still the foundation of career success
- You still need to find allies and build trust

### The Hybrid Trap

- Don't be the person who's always remote when everyone else is in office
- Go in on big meeting days, team events, and 1-on-1s with your manager
- Physical presence counts, even if it's not said explicitly

## 9. MISTAKES THAT ARE OK VS. CAREER-DAMAGING

### Mistakes That Are Totally Fine (Everyone Makes These)

- **Getting the technical details wrong** (on your first few projects)
- **Asking questions repeatedly** (people expect this)
- **Missing social cues** (you're new; grace period exists)
- **Being slower than experienced people** (learning curve is real)
- **Not knowing company history** (you weren't here)
- **Needing written instructions** (it's actually appreciated)

### Mistakes That Cost You

- **Being dishonest or cutting corners** (trust is everything)
- **Sharing confidential information** (toxic label sticks forever)
- **Throwing teammates under the bus** (people remember this)
- **Missing deadlines without communicating** (unreliability is visible)
- **Being resistant to feedback** (signals you won't grow)
- **Complaining about the company to clients/external people** (gets back to leadership)

### How to Recover From a Real Mistake

1. Own it immediately (don't hide it)
2. Explain what happened without excuses
3. Fix it or propose a fix
4. Tell your manager first, not after they find out
5. Prevent it next time (show learning)

**The rule:** Mistakes are forgiven. Dishonesty is not.

## 10. YOUR 90-DAY CHECKPOINT: Reflection and Planning

### Questions to Ask Yourself

- Have I learned the basics of my role?
- Do I understand how my team creates value?
- Can I execute assigned tasks independently?
- Have I built genuine relationships with teammates?
- Do I know what my manager expects?
- Is this company a place I want to grow?
- What's one area where I've clearly improved?

### Conversation to Have with Your Manager

- "How am I doing compared to expectations?"
- "What should I focus on for the next 90 days?"
- "What skills should I develop?"
- "Where do you see my career going here?"

### Decision Point: Is This the Right First Job?

- **Yes:** You understand the role, people are supportive, and you're learning. Stay and invest.
- **Maybe:** Something feels off, but you can't pinpoint it. Give it to 6 months; reassess then.
- **No:** The environment is toxic, goals are impossible, or you're being treated unfairly. Start looking now (but don't rush).

## 11. THE BIGGER PICTURE: Setting Up Your Long-Term Success

### Your First Year Narrative

By the end of year one, you should be able to say:
- "I delivered [X projects/goals] and learned [Y skills]"
- "I built relationships with [specific people/teams]"
- "I identified [specific area] as my strength"
- "I improved in [specific area] based on feedback"

### The Foundation You're Building

Your first job is about proving you can:
- Learn in a new environment
- Execute reliably
- Work with others
- Take feedback and improve
- Think beyond your own task

These traits matter more than the specific skills. Companies hire first-time employees expecting to teach them technical skills. What they're betting on is your ability to learn, listen, and grow.

### Your Leverage for Year Two and Beyond

After 1.5-2 years, you have:
- **Proof of competence** (you shipped things)
- **Skills** (you learned actual tools and processes)
- **Relationships** (you know people, they know you)
- **Credibility** (you delivered)

This is when you negotiate harder on salary, push for better projects, or confidently look elsewhere for more opportunity.

---

## Final Reminder

Your first job is not about being perfect. It's about being reliable, coachable, and genuinely interested in learning. Show up on time, do what you said you'd do, ask good questions, and build relationships. Do these things, and you'll have options.

The person you are in month one won't be the person you are in month twelve. Growth is the whole point. You've got this.
