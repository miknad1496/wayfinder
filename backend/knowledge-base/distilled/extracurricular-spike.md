# The Narrative Spike Protocol — Quantifying Extracurricular Impact

## Purpose

This brain gives the SLM a QUANTITATIVE framework for evaluating extracurricular activities. The existing `admissions-extracurricular-differentiation-strategy.md` covers the qualitative landscape (commoditization cycle, performed ambition vs. demonstrated capability). This brain adds the MEASUREMENT layer: how to assign a tier, how to define "impact" without human bias, and how to architect a spike narrative for the Common App Activities section.

**Architectural note:** This brain does NOT replace the existing EC differentiation strategy. It sits ON TOP of it as the scoring engine. The differentiation strategy answers "what kinds of activities matter?" This brain answers "how do we score them and build a narrative?"

**Compliance Gate:** Wayfinder evaluates activities using observable metrics. It does NOT guarantee that a specific activity will produce a specific admissions outcome. See `compliance-brain.md` MODULE 1 (College Admissions row).

## MODULE 1: SPIKE vs. WELL-ROUNDED — THE STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

### The Historical Shift

The admissions model at selective schools has undergone three distinct phases:

**Phase 1 — The Well-Rounded Era (pre-2010):** Schools sought students who did many things competently. The ideal applicant had 10+ activities spanning sports, arts, service, academics, and leadership. Breadth was the signal. This model rewarded affluent families who could afford activity-stacking.

**Phase 2 — The Spike Era (2010-2020):** MIT's admissions blog first articulated the preference for "spikey" applicants — students with extraordinary depth in one domain rather than shallow breadth across many. The logic: a class of well-rounded students is less interesting than a well-rounded CLASS composed of individually spikey students. Harvard, Stanford, and others adopted this implicitly.

**Phase 3 — The Verified-Impact Era (2020+):** The spike model itself was commoditized (consultants manufactured "spikes"). The current standard requires verified, measurable impact. Not just depth — PROOF of output. The activity must produce something that exists independently of the college application.

### Why the Spike Wins Mathematically

An AO at a T20 school reads 25-40 applications per day during peak season. Each application gets 8-15 minutes on first read. In that time window:

- A "well-rounded" applicant presents 10 activities at equal weight. The AO remembers NONE of them distinctly. The application becomes "another solid student."
- A "spike" applicant presents 2-3 activities at extreme depth. The AO remembers the SPIKE. The application becomes "the robotics kid" or "the epidemiology researcher." This mnemonic label carries through committee discussion.

**The committee dynamic:** Applications are discussed in committee. An AO advocates for their candidates. The spike gives the AO a 10-second pitch: "This is the student who built a working prosthetic hand in their garage and it's being tested at a local clinic." No committee member forgets that. A well-rounded profile gives the AO: "This student has strong grades, good test scores, lots of activities..." — which describes every competitive applicant.

## MODULE 2: THE 4-TIER ACTIVITY IMPACT SCALE

### The Measurement Problem

"Impact" is subjective — or is it? The SLM needs to assign a tier WITHOUT relying on human judgment calls like "this seems impressive." The solution: a composite scoring system based on OBSERVABLE, VERIFIABLE metrics that correlate with admissions impact.

### The Five Impact Dimensions

Each activity is scored across five dimensions. Each dimension has a 1-4 scale. The COMPOSITE score determines the tier.

**Dimension 1 — SCOPE (Who was affected?)**

| Score | Definition | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 4 | International/National reach | Published in a national journal, competed at international olympiad, product used in 5+ countries |
| 3 | State/Regional reach | Won state competition, regional nonprofit serving 10+ schools, statewide policy impact |
| 2 | School/Community reach | School club president, local volunteer organization, community event organizer |
| 1 | Personal/Classroom reach | Personal project, class assignment extended, self-directed learning |

**Dimension 2 — VERIFIABILITY (Can someone outside confirm this?)**

| Score | Definition | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 4 | Independently verifiable by third parties | Published work with DOI, news coverage, public repository, revenue records, competition results on official lists |
| 3 | Verifiable with one degree of separation | Faculty mentor can confirm, organizational records exist, photos/videos of events |
| 2 | Verifiable by school only | School records, counselor knowledge, but no external documentation |
| 1 | Self-reported only | Student's description with no corroborating evidence |

**Dimension 3 — DURATION & PROGRESSION (How long, and did you get better?)**

| Score | Definition | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 4 | 3+ years with clear escalating responsibility/complexity | Founded freshman year → grew to 50+ members → won regional competition → selected for national program |
| 3 | 2-3 years with some progression | Joined sophomore year → became team lead → expanded scope |
| 2 | 1-2 years, steady involvement | Consistent participation without major escalation |
| 1 | <1 year or one-time event | Summer program, single competition, one-off project |

**Dimension 4 — ORIGINALITY (Did you create or join?)**

| Score | Definition | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 4 | Created something that didn't exist before AND it persists beyond you | Founded an organization that continues to operate, built software in active use, launched a business with ongoing customers |
| 3 | Created something new but it depends on you to continue | Started a project that's impressive but will end when you leave |
| 2 | Took leadership of an existing structure and improved it | Became club president and doubled membership, redesigned existing program |
| 1 | Participated in an existing structure | Member of a club, volunteer at established organization, player on a team |

**Dimension 5 — MEASURABLE OUTPUT (Can you put a number on it?)**

| Score | Definition | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 4 | Multiple quantifiable outcomes | $15K revenue, 500 users, 3 published papers, 200 students tutored with measurable grade improvement |
| 3 | One clear quantifiable outcome | Won 1st place at state science fair, raised $5K for cause, built app with 100+ downloads |
| 2 | Qualitative outcome only | "Improved team culture," "helped students," "contributed to research" — real but unquantified |
| 1 | No discernible outcome | Participation only, no product or result |

### The Composite Score and Tier Assignment

**Calculation:** Sum all five dimension scores (range: 5-20).

| Composite Score | Tier | Label | Admissions Signal |
|----------------|------|-------|-------------------|
| **17-20** | **Tier 1: Apex** | International/National Impact | This activity alone can anchor an application. It's the kind of achievement that makes an AO stop reading and call a colleague over. Examples: Intel ISEF finalist, published peer-reviewed research, startup with real revenue, national team selection. |
| **13-16** | **Tier 2: Regional Distinction** | State/Regional Impact | Strong differentiator. Combined with strong academics and essays, this tier of activity contributes meaningfully to a T20 application. Examples: State science fair winner, regional nonprofit with measurable impact, varsity captain with all-conference honors. |
| **9-12** | **Tier 3: School Standout** | School/Community Impact | Expected baseline for T50 applications. At T20 schools, this tier supports but doesn't anchor. Examples: Club president, community volunteer leader, school team member with good performance record. |
| **5-8** | **Tier 4: Participation** | Personal Development | No direct admissions leverage. Valuable for personal growth but should not occupy prime real estate in the Activities section. Examples: Member of club, recreational hobby, personal blog with small audience. |

### The Anti-Bias Mechanism

The scoring system resists human bias through three design choices:

1. **Observable metrics only:** Every dimension asks "what can be SEEN?" not "how does this FEEL?" A student's research paper either has a DOI or it doesn't. A business either has revenue or it doesn't. The scoring doesn't ask whether the activity is "impressive" — it asks whether specific conditions are met.

2. **Socioeconomic normalization:** The SLM must apply a CONTEXT ADJUSTMENT for Tier 3 feeder-school students (see `feeder-school-diagnostic.md`). A student at a resource-constrained school who scores 12 on this scale (Tier 3) may be demonstrating MORE initiative than a student at a feeder school who scores 15 (Tier 2) because the feeder student had institutional support. The context adjustment works as follows:
   - Tier 3 (feeder diagnostic) school student: +2 to composite score
   - Title I Schoolwide school student: +3 to composite score
   - First-generation college student: +1 to composite score
   - These adjustments stack (maximum +4)

3. **The "Would This Exist?" test:** For any activity scoring Tier 1 or Tier 2, the SLM applies the final filter: "Would this activity exist if college applications didn't?" If the answer is no — if the sole purpose of the activity is the application line item — the tier drops by one level regardless of composite score. This catches manufactured consulting projects.

## MODULE 3: BUILDING THE SPIKE NARRATIVE

### The Activities Section Architecture

The Common App allows 10 activity entries. Strategic allocation:

| Slot | Purpose | Tier Target |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Slots 1-2** | The SPIKE. Your defining activity(ies). Maximum detail. | Tier 1 or Tier 2 |
| **Slots 3-4** | Supporting depth. Activities that reinforce the spike's theme. | Tier 2 or Tier 3 |
| **Slots 5-7** | Breadth signals. Show you're a functioning human with range. | Tier 3 |
| **Slots 8-10** | Fill only if genuine. Empty slots are better than padding. | Tier 3 or Tier 4 |

### The 150-Character Description Formula

Each activity gets 150 characters for description. The Wayfinder formula:

```
[QUANTIFIED ACTION] + [MEASURABLE RESULT] + [SCALE INDICATOR]
```

**Examples:**
- Bad: "Member of robotics team. Built robots for competition. Learned about engineering."
- Good: "Designed autonomous navigation system; team placed 2nd/200 at FIRST Regionals; code deployed in 3 subsequent seasons"
- Bad: "Founded a nonprofit to help underserved students with tutoring."
- Good: "Recruited 15 tutors→served 80 students; avg. math grade improved 1.2 letter grades across 2 semesters"

### The Spike-to-Essay Bridge

The spike activity should connect directly to at least one supplemental essay. The SLM should map:

1. **The spike** → what you DID
2. **The "Why Us" essay** → how this school's SPECIFIC resources advance the spike
3. **The Personal Statement** → how the spike EMERGED from your identity/values

This creates a coherent narrative thread across the application. When the AO reads the activities list, then the essays, then the recommendation letters — they all point to the same core identity. This is not repetition — it's REINFORCEMENT.
