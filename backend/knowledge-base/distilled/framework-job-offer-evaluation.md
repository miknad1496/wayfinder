# How to Evaluate a Job Offer Beyond Salary

## The Hidden Architecture of Career Decisions

The salary number is theater. The real value of a job offer lives in the interlocking dimensions of compensation, growth trajectory, learning velocity, and opportunity cost. This framework gives you the tools to see beyond the headline number and make decisions that compound over your entire career.

Most engineers compare three numbers: base salary, bonus, and equity grant. This is like evaluating a car by looking only at the steering wheel. A truly rigorous evaluation requires understanding how each component adds up, how the opportunity will reshape your skills and optionality, and whether the organization itself is in a growth or decline trajectory.

---

## 1. Total Compensation: The Real Number

### Understanding the Components

**Base Salary**: The cash you're guaranteed. Simple to value, but it's typically 40-60% of total comp in competitive markets.

**Signing Bonus**: Immediate cash, sometimes called a "golden handcuff." In multi-year offers, amortize it. If it's $100k over 4 years, that's $25k/year added to base.

**Annual Bonus**: Usually expressed as a percentage (10-25% of base). This is conditional. Track your company's actual payout rates—Glassdoor comments and levels.fyi reveal which companies actually hit targets. A 20% bonus at a company that only pays 60% of target is worth 12% of base, not 20%.

**Equity (RSUs)**: Restricted Stock Units—the most negotiable component. Value = (grant amount / vesting period) × current stock price. **Critical**: Refresh this calculation at offer acceptance time, not just when offer given. Stock prices move.

**Startup Equity (Stock Options/ISOs)**: Riskier, but potentially higher upside. See the dedicated section below.

**Benefits Package**: Health insurance, 401k match, PTO, parental leave, commuter benefits. These are often hidden cash.

### Calculating Total Compensation: The Formula

```
Total Annual Comp = Base + (Bonus × Base × Actual Payout Rate)
                    + (Annual Equity Vesting)
                    + Benefits Value
```

**Example Formula Breakdown:**
- Base: $180,000
- Bonus: 15% (multiply base × 0.15 = $27,000 target, but company pays 80% = $21,600 expected)
- RSU Grant: $300,000 / 4 year vest = $75,000 annual vesting
- Benefits: ~$18,000/year (health insurance, 401k match, other)
- **Total: $180,000 + $21,600 + $75,000 + $18,000 = $294,600**

### Valuing Equity at Different Company Stages

The value of equity varies wildly depending on company stage, market conditions, and probability of liquidation event.

**Public Company (FAANG, etc.)**
- Value: Face value × current stock price = certainty
- Example: 100 RSUs @ $150/share = $15,000/year
- Risk: Stock price volatility (usually low for mega-cap)
- Recommendation: Value at current stock price, discount 15% for volatility buffer

**Late-Stage Private (Series D+, $1B+ valuation)**
- Value: 40-70% of face value (substantial, but illiquid)
- Path to liquidity: Realistic IPO/acquisition timeline (2-5 years typically)
- Recommendation: Research recent funding round valuations. Check Crunchbase for company trajectory. Value at 50% of stated valuation

**Series B/C Startup (Pre-product-market-fit to scaling)**
- Value: 5-25% of face value (high variance)
- Path to liquidity: Uncertain. Need Series D funding to be likely
- Recommendation: Value at 10-15% of face value. Only count in decision if you'd work there for base salary alone

**Pre-Series A Startup (Seed funding)**
- Value: 1-5% of stated equity grant
- Recommendation: Ignore equity value entirely in compensation calculation. If you take the job, assume equity is worthless lottery ticket

**The Equity Dilution Factor**: Your shares will likely be diluted 20-40% in future funding rounds. If you get 0.1% of the company today, you might own 0.06% in 3 years.

### Stock Options vs. RSUs: The Tax Difference

**RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)**
- Taxation: Ordinary income tax at vesting time
- Example: 100 RSUs vest, stock is $150/share. You owe tax on $15,000 at your ordinary income tax rate
- Timing: You pay tax upfront, even before selling

**ISOs (Incentive Stock Options)**
- Taxation: Capital gains rates if held >1 year + >2 years from grant date
- Cost: You exercise at strike price (often $0.01-0.10 for early startup), then hold
- Risk: Substantial cash outlay to exercise (100,000 options × $0.05 = $5,000 exercise cost)
- Benefit: If company worth $10/share at IPO, your capital gain is taxed as long-term capital gain (~15-20%) instead of ordinary income (~37%)
- Recommendation: For startup equity, push for ISOs instead of NSOs (non-qualified stock options) if given the choice

### Benefits Valuation

Standard benefits package value at tech companies:
- **Health insurance**: $12,000-18,000/year (employer pays ~80%)
- **401(k) match**: $7,000-15,000/year (usually 4-6% of base)
- **Parental leave**: $8,000-12,000/year value (pro-rated for use)
- **PTO**: Value depends on policy. 20 days = roughly $6,900/year at $175k base
- **Commuter/transit**: $2,000-4,000/year
- **Education reimbursement**: $1,500-3,000/year (if offered)
- **Other perks** (gym, meals, etc.): Usually low value, nice-to-have only

Total non-equity benefits: **$38,000-55,000/year in comp value**, but wide variance.

## 2. Career Trajectory: Resume Value and Optionality

### The Compound Interest of Career Capital

A $150k role that builds your optionality is worth more than a $250k role that traps you. Career compounds through skills, relationships, and credentials.

### Assessing Resume Value: The 2-3 Year Test

After leaving, will this job make you more hireable? Use these dimensions:

**Technical Leverage**:
- Will you ship features customers see, or maintain legacy systems?
- Are you on the critical path for important projects?
- Do you have opportunities to own meaningful technical decisions?
- Are you learning current-generation technologies or maintaining year-old stacks?

**Scope of Responsibility**:
- Will you manage people/projects/budgets?
- Are you solving novel problems or executing well-defined tasks?
- Can you take credit for specific outcomes in interviews?

**Company Credibility**:
- Does the company name open doors in interviews? (FAANG >> Series C >> Unknown startup)
- Will your credentials from here be relevant in 3 years?

**Network Quality**:
- Are your colleagues people you'd want working with you again?
- Does the company attract strong engineers, or is talent filtering weak?

### Red Flags for Resume Value

- **High-churn teams**: If the team has >30% turnover/year, something is broken
- **Dying technology stack**: You'll spend 3 years becoming expert in something nobody uses
- **Unclear ownership**: "We work as a team" often means nobody owns anything, so you own nothing
- **Lack of external visibility**: Private company, internal tools only, NDA-heavy work means you can't talk about impact in interviews
- **Title inflation without responsibility**: "Senior Engineer" doing junior work is worse than realistic leveling

### The Manager Question: Why It Matters More Than Company Name

**Research your direct manager obsessively.**

This is the single highest-leverage decision within a job offer evaluation. The same title at the same company can be vastly different based on who you report to.

**Green flags for a manager:**
- They have a track record of promoting their reports
- They've worked with strong engineers before (ask for references)
- They can articulate their philosophy of delegation and feedback
- They've changed teams/levels internally (means they're ambitious)
- They ask substantive questions about your career goals
- They have low direct-report turnover

**Red flags for a manager:**
- More than 2 years in current role without promotion
- They describe "flat hierarchy" while you'd be new (often means no real mentorship)
- They can't articulate a plan for your first 6 months
- They're newer to the team than you would be (no institutional knowledge to transfer)
- They describe team using only negative framing ("We're behind on roadmap," "Everyone's stressed")

### Scoring Career Trajectory

```
Resume Value Score (0-25 points):
- Technical leverage (0-7): Does this job build skills/projects that get interviews?
- Scope expansion (0-6): Will your responsibilities grow during tenure?
- Company credibility (0-6): Does the brand matter in your next role search?
- Network quality (0-6): Quality of colleagues and extended network

Manager Quality Score (0-25 points):
- Track record (0-7): History of developing and promoting reports?
- Clarity (0-6): Can they articulate your first-year plan?
- Ambition (0-6): Are they growing into bigger roles themselves?
- Support (0-6): Do they have capacity to invest in your development?
```

## 3. Learning Velocity: The Rate at Which You Compound

Learning velocity is how fast you gain new skills, perspectives, and judgment.

### The Learning Velocity Framework

**Market exposure** (how close are you to customer problems?):
- Direct: You talk to customers, see real problems → High velocity
- Product: You build features based on product specs → Medium velocity
- Infrastructure: You optimize internal systems → Lower velocity

**Complexity of problems**:
- Novel/ambiguous: Nobody's solved this before → High velocity
- Complex/defined: Hard problem, known solution class → Medium velocity
- Routine/defined: Pattern matching to known solutions → Low velocity

**Feedback loops** (how quickly do you know if you're right?):
- Minutes/hours (web deployment, metrics dashboards) → High velocity
- Days/weeks (hardware, compliance, enterprise sales) → Medium velocity
- Quarters/years (infrastructure, long product cycles) → Low velocity

**Quality of feedback sources** (who tells you if you're wrong?):
- Actual customers, market data, clear metrics → High fidelity
- Product managers, business analysts → Medium fidelity
- Internal stakeholders only → Low fidelity

### The High-Learning-Velocity Job Profile

High learning velocity jobs have these characteristics:
- You ship frequently (weekly or more often)
- Your work directly impacts metrics people care about
- You're solving problems without clear precedent
- You have peers who are better than you at specific skills
- Your manager invests time in your development
- The team tackles multiple domains (you pick up breadth)

### The Learning Velocity Trap

**Beware the "learning" job that's actually just "difficult but routine":**
- Enterprise systems integration at a Fortune 500: Hard work, but you're learning outdated vendor software
- Legacy system maintenance with high complexity but zero visibility: You're getting very good at something nobody else cares about
- Startup pre-product-market-fit: Chaotic environment isn't learning velocity—it's just chaos

### Valuing Learning Velocity Over Salary

A job that accelerates your learning by 1-2 years is worth $30-60k in lifetime earnings:
- Today's offer: $200k base, low learning velocity
- Alternative: $160k base, high learning velocity
- Over 3 years, the second job puts you 18+ months ahead in market value
- Your next job at year 4 will pay $30-50k more because you're ahead of peers

**In early career (years 1-5): Prioritize learning velocity over current salary. The ROI is 2-3x.**

## 4. Company Trajectory: Is This Organization Growing or Declining?

### The Fundamental Question

Is this company positioned for growth in the next 3-5 years, or executing against a declining opportunity? This shapes whether you're building valuable skills or treading water.

### Industry Trajectory Assessment

**Growth industries (next 3-5 years)**:
- AI/LLM infrastructure and applications
- Climate tech, renewable energy
- Biotech and computational biology
- Cybersecurity (always growing)
- Developer tools and infrastructure

**Declining or mature industries**:
- Traditional search (Google/Bing face declining usage trends)
- Traditional advertising networks
- Retail infrastructure
- Print/publishing technology
- Telecommunications infrastructure

**Cyclical/complex**:
- Finance/fintech (growth, but regulation-sensitive)
- Enterprise software (growing, but consolidation happening)
- Social media (mature, acquisition-only growth)

### Company-Specific Growth Signals

**For public companies:**
- Revenue growth rate (>20% YoY is healthy, <5% is concerning)
- Profit margins and direction (expanding or shrinking?)
- Stock price and insider selling (if executives are selling, it matters)
- Analyst sentiment and guidance
- R&D investment rate (high = investing in future)

**For private companies (startup/growth):**
- Funding trajectory: How much growth in funding round sizes?
  - Flat or declining round sizes = growth stalling
  - Growing round sizes = market is buying the story
- Time to next funding round (if VC-backed): If they need another round in <18 months, they're burning fast
- Runway remaining: Estimate (cash × 12) / monthly burn rate
  - <12 months = crisis, risky
  - 12-24 months = some buffer, but pressure to find growth
  - >24 months = breathing room
- Market traction: Customers growing? Revenue growing? LTV/CAC ratio healthy?

**For mature private/acquired companies:**
- Growth rate of business under parent
- Autonomy from parent company (are you being hollowed out for IP?)
- Integration momentum (absorbed into parent = death slow fade)

### Red Flags for Company Trajectory

- **Multiple missed product releases**: Signals execution problems
- **Layoffs disguised as "restructuring"**: Your team is shrinking, and they're not transparent about it
- **Declining hiring pace**: Compare open requisitions quarter to quarter
- **Key executive departures**: If the CTO or VP Engineering just left, something's wrong
- **Shifting business model midstream**: Early sign of failure to execute on original plan
- **Increasing debt/declining cash**: Dangerous for startups especially

### Scoring Company Trajectory

```
Company Trajectory Score (0-20 points):
- Industry growth (0-7): Is the industry expanding or contracting?
- Company growth metrics (0-7): Revenue/user growth, funding, financial health?
- Competitive position (0-6): Winning or losing market share in industry?
```

## 5. Culture Signals: Reading Between the Lines

Culture is the single most variable factor across companies. Two identical roles at similar pay can have wildly different cultures.

### How to Actually Research Culture

**Glassdoor and Blind: The Reality Filter**

Glassdoor reviews are selection-biased (people write when extremely happy or upset). But patterns emerge:
- If top complaint appears in 50+ reviews: It's structural, not anecdotal
- If salary reviews match stated offer: The company isn't lying
- Negative reviews with specific examples > vague praise
- Sort by most recent: Old reviews might not reflect today's reality

**Red flags in reviews:**
- "Work-life balance" complaints in 30%+ of reviews
- "Lack of communication" or "unclear direction" repeatedly
- "Politics" mentioned multiple times (usually signals low psychological safety)
- Burnout/overwork complaints across different teams
- "No growth opportunity" repeatedly

**Green flags in reviews:**
- Specific praise for managers/leadership
- "Learned a lot" and "challenging work" mentioned
- Management actually listens to feedback
- Examples of people promoted or given opportunities
- Diversity and inclusion specific, positive mentions

### The Interview Process as Culture Diagnostics

The interview process reveals culture:

**Process cleanliness**:
- Do interviewers arrive on time? (Signals organizational respect)
- Do you get clear, timely feedback? (Signals decision-making process)
- Does the company follow up on your timeline? (Signals respect for your time)

**Interviewer quality**:
- Are interviewers prepared, or just reading your resume? (Signals hiring rigor)
- Do they know the team/code? (Signals respect for the role)
- Can they articulate why you'd be good fit? (Signals culture clarity)

**Questions they ask**:
- Deep technical questions = they care about craft
- Hypothetical scenarios = they care about thinking style
- "Tell me about a conflict" = they care about collaboration
- Only leetcode style = they're myopic on hiring

**Questions they avoid**:
- Never ask about growth path? = It doesn't exist
- Never ask about what you want to learn? = They don't care about development
- No questions about team dynamic? = Culture is low priority

### Red Flags in Interview Process

- **Ghosting or slow communication**: Shows chaos or lack of respect
- **Interviewers with contradictory messages**: Team doesn't align on strategy/values
- **Interview focused only on what you'll build for them**: No concern for your growth
- **Last-minute rescheduling**: Shows disrespect for your time
- **Pressure to decide quickly**: Good companies don't rush offers
- **Unwillingness to have a team meeting before offer**: Red flag for hidden team issues

### The "2 AM Test": The Moral Compass Filter

Would you be proud to work on this company's problems at 2 AM?

Not cynical, not about money, not about comfort. The real question: **If money were equal, would you choose this over alternatives?**

This filters for alignment:
- Company mission matters to you (or you're neutral)
- Customers aren't being exploited (or you're comfortable with the ethics)
- Product solves real problems (or is honest about what it does)
- Company isn't actively harmful

**Honest self-assessment questions:**
- If you were 80 years old, would you tell your grandkids about this company's impact?
- Are you explaining away concerns about the mission?
- Do you feel guilty about any part of what the company does?
- Would you be embarrassed to tell your friends what this company does?

If answers are unclear: The company fails the 2 AM test. That's OK—not every job needs to be meaningful. But don't pretend it's something it isn't.

## 6. Work-Life Reality: How to Get Honest Information

### The Stated vs. Actual Work Expectation Gap

Every company says "Flexible hours" and "Work-life balance." Most are lying or delusional.

### How to Get Honest Information

**During interviews (final round, with your potential manager):**

"Can you walk me through what a typical week looks like? What would you expect of me in my first 90 days?"

Watch for:
- Vagueness = they don't actually know / it's highly variable
- "Everyone works hard" = cultural expectation to overwork
- Specific examples = realistic
- "First 90 days is startup mode" = Expect sustained pressure initially
- "We encourage people to have a life" = Often genuine signal (stated specifically, not just "work-life balance")

**Ask directly:**

"I have [family commitments / hobbies / side projects]. How would that work here?"

Watch for:
- Immediate reassurance vs. hesitation
- Examples of people who've managed similar balance
- Honesty about busy seasons (that's OK—but honest is better than fake)

**Post-offer conversations:**

Call someone on the team (have the recruiter arrange if possible) and ask:
- "How many hours a week does your role typically take?"
- "Are there times of year that are busier than others?"
- "What does PTO actually look like—do people take it?"
- "Have you worked here during COVID and during normal times? How did expectations change?"

**Glassdoor audit:**
- Search for "hours," "overtime," "crunch," "weekends"
- If 30%+ of reviews mention working weekends → expect it
- Recent reviews > old reviews

### Industry Baseline Work Expectations

**Lowest hours** (typically):
- Government/federal tech jobs: 40 hours/week, strict boundaries, good benefits
- Mature enterprise companies: 40-45 hours/week, predictable
- Public companies past IPO: 45-50 hours/week at senior levels

**Moderate hours**:
- Series B/C startups with product-market-fit: 50-60 hours/week
- Mid-market (50-500 people companies): 45-55 hours/week
- Post-IPO growth-stage startups: 50-60 hours/week

**High hours**:
- Pre-product-market-fit startups: 60-80 hours/week
- Wall Street adjacent tech: 60-70 hours/week
- Crisis mode (layoffs, acquisition, product failure): 60-80+ hours/week

### Evaluating Work-Life Realistically

**Green flags:**
- Team members take PTO and aren't backfilled
- Manager explicitly protects time for professional development
- Synchronous meetings have a "no meeting day"
- People have hobbies/side projects they discuss
- Parental leave actually taken (not available but unused)

**Red flags:**
- "Culture of availability" mentioned as positive
- Slack response expectations during off-hours
- People apologizing for taking vacation
- Always-on on-call requirements without compensation
- Manager suggests "onboarding means extra hours"

## 7. Geographic and Remote Considerations: The Cost of Living Misunderstanding

### The Standard Mistake

"They're offering $200k in San Francisco vs. $150k remote." The remote job is often worth more.

### Real Cost of Living Adjustment

**San Francisco example:**
- Rent (1-bed, decent area): $2,500-3,500/month = $30-42k/year
- Taxes (CA state, federal, local): ~$60k/year at $200k income
- Other cost of living (food, transportation, activities): $15k/year
- **After-tax, after-CoL: ~$95k/year purchasing power**

**Seattle example (same $200k):**
- Rent: $1,800-2,500/month = $22-30k/year
- Taxes (WA state, federal): ~$45k/year (no state income tax)
- Other cost of living: $12k/year
- **After-tax, after-CoL: ~$121k/year purchasing power**

**Remote, $150k (with local market salary):**
- Rent (assuming moderate city or suburbs): $1,200-1,800/month = $15-22k/year
- Taxes (depends on state, federal): ~$35k/year
- Other cost of living: $10k/year
- **After-tax, after-CoL: ~$93k/year purchasing power**

But there's more:
- Remote role from lower CoL city to California company = You keep the lower CoL but get California salary (huge win)
- In-office requirement removes flexibility premium

### Remote Work: What to Actually Value

**Green flags for remote role:**
- Asynchronous-first culture (not synchronous by necessity)
- Good home office setup stipend ($1,500+)
- Clear working hours expectations
- No expectation to be in-office randomly
- Health/gym budget to compensate for commute time freed up

**Red flags for remote role:**
- "Remote but must be available 8-6pm Eastern time" (No actual flexibility)
- Constant video calls replacing office interruptions (Same chaos, different form)
- Remote compensation discount (same role pays 10-15% less remote = avoid)
- Expectation to travel to HQ quarterly (adds $4-8k/year in travel)
- Asynchronous culture stated but everything happens live (culture/reality gap)

### Geographic Arbitrage (The Real Win)

If you can get a California/New York tech salary while living in a lower CoL region:
- $180k Bay Area salary → move to Austin, negotiate to $150k for remote work
- Your $150k is worth ~$95k San Francisco buying power (you're paying Austin rent)
- You've essentially traded 17% salary for 50% better lifestyle and huge financial buffer

## 9. Real-World Examples: Four Job Offers Compared

### Setup

All four candidates are mid-level engineers (4-6 years experience) in the US. Offers are from:
1. **Google (FAANG)** - Mountain View, in-office
2. **Series B Fintech Startup** - San Francisco, in-office
3. **Mid-Market Enterprise** - Chicago, hybrid
4. **Federal Tech (Government)** - Remote (across US)

### Offer 1: Google — Mountain View

**Compensation:**
- Base: $190,000
- Signing bonus: $75,000 (amortized over 4 years = $18,750/year)
- Annual bonus: 20% of base = $38,000 (historically pays 85% = $32,300 expected)
- RSU grant: $600,000 / 4-year vest = $150,000/year
- Benefits: ~$22,000/year (health, 401k match, PTO value)
- **Total annual comp: $412,300**

**Scoring:**
- Comp Score: 10/10 (highest absolute pay)
- Career Score: 9/10 (Google name opens doors forever, strong peers, solid manager development track, but org is large—could get lost)
- Learning Score: 7/10 (you're one of thousands; impact is diluted; peers are strong but you own less; systems complexity is high though)
- Company Score: 9/10 (massive scale, dominant market position, consistent growth, but maturing)
- Culture Score: 6/10 (reviews mixed; "Politics" mentioned frequently; "Amazing to learn, stressful internally"; strong engineering culture but bureaucracy)
- Work-Life Score: 7/10 (40-45 hours/week norm, but crunch projects exist; flexibility good for hybrid/remote; parental leave excellent)
- Geographic Score: 5/10 (Mountain View rent = $3,200/month, after-tax + CoL = ~$160k purchasing power despite $412k comp)
- **FINAL SCORE: 7.5/10**

---

### Offer 2: Series B Fintech Startup — San Francisco

**Compensation:**
- Base: $180,000
- Signing bonus: $50,000 (amortized 3 years before likely exit = $16,666/year)
- Annual bonus: 15% of base = $27,000 (young company, pays 60% = $16,200 expected)
- Equity: 0.08% of company (Series B valuation $200M, so ~$160,000 value at 50% probability discount = $80,000, but amortized over 4-year vest = $20,000/year "expected value")
- Benefits: ~$18,000/year
- **Total annual comp: $234,866 (but equity is speculative)**

**Scoring:**
- Comp Score: 6/10 (lower absolute pay, significant equity risk)
- Career Score: 8/10 (Fintech resume value is good; you'll own meaningful features; Series B is visible startup, great for recruiting; good manager reputation)
- Learning Score: 9/10 (Every day matters; feedback loops are tight—you ship features daily and see impact in metrics; high velocity, novel problems; small team so you learn breadth)
- Company Score: 7/10 (Fintech growing, Series B means market traction exists, but regulatory risk; company has 18-month runway, pressure to find next round)
- Culture Score: 8/10 (Glassdoor: "Intense but fair," "People actually care," "Mission-driven" frequently; interview process was sharp; team seems genuinely collaborative)
- Work-Life Score: 5/10 (Startup reality = 55-60 hours/week; occasional weekends during crunch; manager says "First year is startup mode"; PTO is okay in policy but not in practice)
- Geographic Score: 4/10 (SF rent + CoL = only ~$95k purchasing power despite claimed $234k comp)
- **FINAL SCORE: 6.8/10**

### Offer 3: Mid-Market Enterprise — Chicago (Hybrid)

**Compensation:**
- Base: $165,000
- Signing bonus: $30,000 (no equity, so amortized over expected 4-year tenure = $7,500/year)
- Annual bonus: 12% of base = $19,800 (large companies pay ~80-90% = $16,200 expected)
- No equity
- Benefits: ~$24,000/year (excellent health, 6 weeks PTO, 401k match 6%, parental leave 16 weeks)
- **Total annual comp: $212,700**

## 10. The Decision Framework: Bringing It Together

### Your Personal Variables

The scoring system above is a foundation, but your personal situation adjusts weights:

**If you have dependents or $200k debt:**
- Increase Comp weight to 35%
- Increase Work-Life weight to 15%
- Decrease Learning/Career weights
- Google > Enterprise > Fintech

**If you're in year 1-3 of career:**
- Increase Learning weight to 30%
- Increase Career weight to 25%
- Decrease Comp weight to 15%
- Fintech/Google > Enterprise

**If you're burnt out:**
- Increase Work-Life weight to 20%
- Decrease Comp weight to 15%
- Enterprise/Government > Fintech

**If you're risk-averse:**
- Increase Company Trajectory weight to 20%
- Decrease equity value by 50%
- Enterprise/Government/Google > Fintech

### The 72-Hour Decision Protocol

Once you have two offers with similar scores:

1. **Gut check**: Which one feels right in your body when you imagine day 1? (Ignore logic for 10 seconds)

2. **Manager call**: Get on a call with your future manager. Ask: "What would success look like for me in year 1?" If they don't have a clear answer, this is bad sign.

3. **Sleep on it**: Make a decision, sleep, see if you wake up anxious or excited about it.

4. **Reverse negotiation**: Tell the slightly-lower company "I'm 85% toward the other offer. What could change my decision?" Sometimes they move another $20k.

5. **Commit**: Once decided, don't look back. Regret is a cognitive bias—the decision was solid or you wouldn't have been at 72 hours.

### Non-Negotiable Red Flags

Some things disqualify an offer entirely:

- **Manager is dismissive or can't articulate a plan for you**: Walk away. The best comp can't fix a bad manager.
- **Company is in crisis but hiding it**: Walk away. Crisis mode is where you burn out fastest.
- **Work-life expectations don't match your needs and won't change**: Walk away. You can't negotiate this later—it's cultural.
- **You would fail the 2 AM test**: Walk away. Cognitive dissonance will compound over 3 years.
- **Equity valuation requires 10x growth for you to gain value**: Walk away. You're banking on lottery odds.

## 11. The Long View: What Matters After the Decision

Once you accept an offer, the evaluation shifts:

**The first 90 days:**
- Is the reality matching the interview?
- Are you actually getting the learning velocity promised?
- Is your manager following through on their commitments?

If red flags appear in first 90 days, you can still course-correct (your next job at year 1.5 is better than being stuck for 3 years).

**Year 1 checkpoint:**
- Have you shipped things you're proud of?
- Are you more employable than you were on day 1?
- Is the team and manager investment real?

**Year 2 checkpoint:**
- Are you being promoted or given bigger scope?
- Is the company trajectory matching what was promised?
- Would you choose this job again?

If your year 2 checkpoint answer is "No," you should start looking at options. Two years is the minimum to extract full resume value anyway.

**Year 3 decision:**
- You've now built significant equity (if any)
- You have a track record at this company
- Your next role can be optionality-maximizing (you've already got a credential)

This framework will serve you well throughout your career. Return to it every 2-3 years as you evaluate new opportunities.
